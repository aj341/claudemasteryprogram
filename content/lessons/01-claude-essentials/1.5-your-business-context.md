## Lesson script

### The problem you've probably already noticed

You've done four lessons. You've sent real prompts, seen what Claude produces, and formed a view on where it's useful. But there's a friction point that shows up quickly once you try to use Claude consistently: every time you open a new session, Claude knows nothing about you.

Not your name. Not your business. Not your clients, your usual tone, your compliance constraints, or the tasks you run every week. You're starting from scratch each time. If you want Claude to produce output that fits your actual context, you have to re-explain that context — every single session.

Most people handle this in one of two ways. Either they skip the context entirely and get generic output that needs heavy editing. Or they re-explain it from memory each time, inconsistently, which means Claude gets a slightly different picture of your business every session. Both approaches produce unpredictable results.

There's a better approach: write it once, paste it in once per session, and be done with it.

That's what this lesson is for.

### What a context document actually is

A context document is a short, structured document — 300 to 500 words — that contains the key facts Claude needs to produce useful output for your specific business. You write it once. At the start of any session where the output needs to actually fit your business, you paste it in before your first prompt.

That's it. It's not technical. It doesn't require any special formatting or software. It's a document you write today, save somewhere accessible, and use for the rest of the course — and beyond it.

The context document works because Claude reads everything in the current session. When you paste your context document at the start, every response you get in that session is shaped by it. Claude knows who you are, what you do, who your clients are, and what the guardrails are. You don't need to repeat yourself. You don't need to re-establish your business in every prompt.

One important thing before you write: your context document is about your business, not your clients. It contains your information — your role, your services, your tone, your workflows. It does not contain client names, client financials, case details, personal data, or anything else you'd find in a confidential client file. That boundary matters both practically and legally, which the next section covers.

### What to include — the five components

A context document that works covers five things. Not ten. Not twenty. Five.

**1. Who you are and what your business does.** Your name, your role, your industry, the size of your business, and a one-sentence description of what you do. This is the foundation. Without it, Claude defaults to generic assumptions that may not fit you.

**2. Your clients and what they need.** Who do you work with? What do they typically come to you for? What's the standard scope of a client engagement? You don't need to name anyone — describe your client type. "Small business owners in retail, usually 5–20 staff, needing bookkeeping and quarterly BAS preparation" is exactly the right level of detail.

**3. Your tone and communication style.** How do you write and talk? Formal or conversational? Technical or plain language? Do you use Australian English? Is there anything you never say in client communications — jargon, overly casual language, specific words you avoid? Claude will match whatever style you specify. If you don't specify, it guesses.

**4. Your three to five key recurring tasks.** From your work in Lesson 1.4, you mapped your weekly tasks and identified which ones are best suited to Claude. Name those tasks here. "Draft client emails, write monthly reports, create first-draft proposals, summarise meeting notes, review contracts for key clauses." This tells Claude what you'll be asking it to do regularly, so the output is oriented toward those kinds of tasks.

**5. Your constraints.** This is the section most people skip and later regret. Constraints are the rules Claude needs to follow for your business specifically. What should Claude never produce without your review? What regulations or compliance requirements apply to your work? Are there topics you don't want Claude to speculate on? Is there information that's confidential and should never appear in outputs? This section is what makes your context document a genuine working document rather than a general introduction.

Here is what a well-structured context document looks like in practice:

```
BUSINESS CONTEXT — [Your Name]

Who I am and what I do:
I'm [Name], principal of a two-person bookkeeping practice based in Adelaide.
We work exclusively with small businesses under 15 staff in hospitality and retail.
Our core services are monthly bookkeeping, BAS preparation, and cash flow reporting.
We use Xero for all clients.

My clients:
Small business owners — typically owner-operators or working directors. They're
time-poor, not financially trained, and rely on me to translate numbers into
decisions. They need clear, jargon-free communication. Most of my client contact
is by email, with quarterly catch-up calls.

Tone and communication style:
Warm and direct. Plain language — no accounting jargon unless I specifically ask
for it. Australian English. Sentences that are easy to scan. I never write "please
don't hesitate to contact me" — just "let me know if you have questions."

Key recurring tasks I'll use Claude for:
- Drafting client emails (routine updates, BAS reminders, late-payment follow-ups)
- Writing first drafts of quarterly cash flow summaries
- Summarising meeting notes from client catch-up calls
- Reviewing supplier contracts to flag payment terms and renewal clauses
- Writing internal process documentation for my part-time admin assistant

Constraints:
- I work in a regulated environment. Never produce anything that looks like
  formal tax advice — frame outputs as drafts for my review, not finished advice.
- Do not include specific dollar amounts or tax rates unless I provide them first.
- Confidentiality: do not reference any specific client name or business detail
  I share during a session in a way that could appear in a document.
- When in doubt about regulatory content, flag it clearly and recommend I verify.
```

That document is 280 words. It takes about 10 minutes to write. It will save you hours of context-repeating over the course of this program and well beyond it.

### What NOT to include

A context document is not a brain dump. It's not everything about your business. It's the minimum Claude needs to produce useful output — and nothing more.

There are two kinds of things to keep out.

The first: sensitive client information. Client names, client financials, personal addresses, medical details, legal case information, anything about a specific individual that they gave you in confidence. Your context document lives on your device, in your clipboard, potentially in Claude sessions. It should contain zero client-identifying data.

The second: operational detail Claude doesn't need upfront. Your internal pricing, full staff list, detailed project history, long strategic documents, everything you've ever written. These can be shared with Claude during specific sessions when they're relevant. They don't belong in your context document, which needs to stay short enough to paste and scan in under 30 seconds.

An overly long context document also creates practical problems. Claude reads it, but so do you — every time you decide whether this session needs context. If the document is 1,500 words, you'll stop using it. Keep it to 500 words or under.

### How to use it

Once you've written it, using the context document is straightforward.

At the start of any session where the output needs to fit your business specifically — a client email, a proposal, a report — paste your context document in as the first message. You don't need to say anything else. Just paste it. Claude reads it and uses it for every response in that session.

For quick, low-stakes tasks — a one-line question, a quick fact-check, a calculation — you don't need the context document. Reserve it for sessions where the output matters.

After you paste it, write your first prompt as normal. You don't need to repeat what's in the context document in your prompt. Just ask for what you need. Claude has the context; your prompt can focus on the task.

Here's the simplest version of how a session with a context document starts:

```
[Paste your full context document here]

Draft an email to a client who has missed their monthly bookkeeping fee payment.
The invoice was due 14 days ago. Polite but clear. Under 120 words.
```

That's it. No re-explaining who you are. No re-stating your tone preferences. The context document has done that work.

### Common mistake → better approach

**Mistake:** Writing a context document that's a general business description — something that reads like the "About Us" page on your website. "We are a client-focused accounting firm committed to delivering exceptional results for small businesses across Australia." This is marketing copy. It tells Claude nothing about what you actually need.

**Better approach:** Write the context document for Claude specifically, not for an audience. Think about what a highly capable assistant would need to know on their first day working with you. What tasks will they do? What rules must they follow? How should they write? What's the one thing they should never get wrong? That's your context document. Concrete, operational, specific.

## Worked examples

### Example 1: Bookkeeping practice (full context document)

**Scenario:** A sole-trader bookkeeper in Adelaide writes a context document before using Claude for client communications and internal documentation.

**Prompt:**
```
BUSINESS CONTEXT — Sarah Chen

Who I am and what I do:
I'm Sarah Chen, sole-trader bookkeeper based in Adelaide. I work with 18 small
business clients in hospitality and retail — mostly cafes, small restaurants, and
boutique retail shops. Core services: monthly bookkeeping, BAS preparation, payroll
processing, and quarterly cash flow reports. I use Xero for all clients and
communicate primarily by email.

My clients:
Owner-operators, usually without accounting backgrounds. They need things explained
clearly and quickly — they're running a business, not reading a finance report.
Most are aged 35–55, comfortable with email, less comfortable with numbers. They
trust me to tell them what they need to know without jargon.

Tone and communication style:
Friendly and direct. Plain English. Australian spelling. No accounting jargon unless
specifically requested. Short paragraphs, easy to scan. I use "you'll" and "we'll"
— contractions are fine. I never write "pursuant to" or "as per" — I just say
"because of" or "following."

Key recurring tasks I'll use Claude for:
- Drafting client emails: BAS reminders, monthly summaries, late payment follow-ups,
  onboarding messages for new clients
- Writing first drafts of quarterly cash flow commentary
- Summarising notes from client calls into action items
- Creating internal process guides for tasks I need to document

Constraints:
- I'm a registered BAS agent, not a tax agent. Never draft anything that reads as
  tax advice — frame all financial commentary as "for your records" or "for your
  review" and flag anything that needs a tax agent.
- Never include specific tax rates, ATO thresholds, or legislative references unless
  I provide them first — these change and I need to verify them before using.
- Client confidentiality: if I share specific client names or details during a
  session, do not carry them into template outputs or summaries — keep outputs
  generic and reusable.
- Flag anything uncertain rather than guessing.
```
**Why it works:** Every section is concrete and operational. The tone section gives Claude specific examples of what to write and what to avoid ("as per" → "because of"). The constraints section makes the regulatory boundary explicit and explains the reasoning behind it. This document would produce consistent, usable output across dozens of sessions without any re-explaining.

### Example 2: Residential building company (full context document)

**Scenario:** A project manager at a small residential builder writes a context document to use Claude for site communications, client updates, and subcontractor correspondence.

**Prompt:**
```
BUSINESS CONTEXT — Marcus Webb

Who I am and what I do:
I'm Marcus Webb, Project Manager at Webb Residential Constructions — a family-run
builder in southeast Queensland. We build new homes and do major renovations,
typically 8–12 active projects at any time. Our builds range from $400K to $1.2M.
I report to the director (my father), manage 4 full-time site supervisors, and
handle all subcontractor relationships and client communication.

My clients:
Owner-builders and families building their first or second home. They are not
construction professionals — they're emotionally invested, often anxious about
timeline and cost, and need honest, clear updates. Most communication is by email
and SMS. They respond badly to jargon and well to straight talk.

Tone and communication style:
Direct and honest. Plain language — no construction jargon unless it's necessary
and I define it. I use Australian English. I write short emails — if it runs more
than three paragraphs, I've written too much. I never bury bad news — I state the
issue, the cause, and the next step clearly and quickly.

Key recurring tasks I'll use Claude for:
- Client progress updates (weekly email updates during active builds)
- Variation notices (informing clients of scope changes and cost implications)
- Subcontractor correspondence (scheduling, issue resolution, instruction letters)
- Internal site notes and incident documentation
- Reviewing supplier quotes to flag anything unusual before I sign

Constraints:
- Construction in Queensland is regulated by the QBCC. Never draft anything that
  reads as formal legal advice or that makes commitments outside standard contract
  terms — flag if I'm approaching that territory.
- Do not include specific contract figures, prices, or timeline guarantees unless
  I provide them explicitly in the session.
- I sometimes share site photos or specific project details. Keep these confined
  to the session — do not carry them into template documents.
- If a draft involves a dispute, complaint, or potential claim — flag it clearly
  so I can involve our solicitor before sending.
```
**Why it works:** The constraints section reflects the actual regulatory and risk landscape of a licensed builder in Queensland — QBCC references, contract commitments, dispute flagging. This is what makes the document genuinely operational rather than generic. It tells Claude not just what to produce, but what to watch for.

### Example 3: Using the context document in a real session

**Scenario:** Sarah (from Example 1) starts a Claude session to draft a late payment follow-up email. She pastes her context document first, then writes a task prompt.

**Prompt:**
```
[Sarah's full context document from Example 1 pasted here]

Draft a follow-up email to a client whose monthly bookkeeping invoice is 21 days
overdue. Invoice amount is $380. First time they've been late in two years.
Polite and firm — I want them to pay, but I don't want to damage the relationship.
Under 120 words.
```
**Why it works:** The prompt itself is only two sentences and a constraint. It doesn't need to explain who Sarah is, what tone she uses, or that she writes in plain Australian English — the context document has already done that. The prompt can focus entirely on the specific task. The context document has reduced the prompting overhead to almost nothing.

### Example 4: Spotting a weak context document and fixing it

**Scenario:** A marketing consultant writes a first draft context document. It reads well but won't produce consistent results.

**Weak version:**
```
I'm a marketing consultant who works with small businesses. I help them
with their marketing strategy and content. My clients are usually small
to medium businesses who want to grow. I write professionally and clearly.
I use Claude to help with client work.
```

**Stronger version:**
```
BUSINESS CONTEXT — Priya Sharma

Who I am and what I do:
I'm Priya Sharma, independent marketing consultant based in Melbourne. I work
with B2B services businesses — typically professional services firms (accountants,
lawyers, engineers) with 10–50 staff. Engagements run 3–6 months and focus on
content strategy, LinkedIn presence, and email marketing. I work alone.

My clients:
Principals and partners at professional services firms. They are sceptical of
marketing "fluff" and respond to evidence-based recommendations. They prefer
concise communication. They need to look credible and authoritative to their
own clients — not trendy or casual.

Tone and communication style:
Professional and precise. No marketing buzzwords — I never write "authentic,"
"innovative," or "thought leadership." Evidence where possible. Australian English.
Recommendations with reasons, not just suggestions.

Key recurring tasks I'll use Claude for:
- First drafts of LinkedIn posts for clients (professional services tone)
- Email newsletter copy for client firms
- Summarising client briefing notes into campaign concepts
- Writing case study outlines from client interview notes

Constraints:
- Client work is confidential. If I share a client's name or materials,
  keep them session-contained and never reference them in template outputs.
- Do not produce anything that reads as legal or financial advice — flag it.
- Outputs should be ready for my review, not for immediate client delivery.
```
**Why it works:** The stronger version names the exact industry niches, the client psychology ("sceptical of marketing fluff"), and the specific words to avoid. The weak version describes a job category. The strong version describes an actual business — and gives Claude enough precision to produce output Priya won't need to rewrite.

**Why it works:** This shows exactly how the context document carries forward. The student pastes it in, and the task prompt can focus on the goal. Claude has what it needs to give a relevant recommendation — not a generic one. This is the exact workflow the student will use in Lesson 1.6.

## Notes for the AI grading engine

These notes are read by the grading engine when scoring submissions for this lesson. They are not shown to the student.

- **Reward (high marks):**
  - Context document names a specific industry and client type with enough detail to distinguish the business from a generic description — for example, "B2B IT support for medical practices in Western Australia" rather than "IT support business"
  - Tone section includes specific examples of language to use or avoid, not just adjectives ("plain English, no 'pursuant to,' Australian spelling")
  - Constraints section names at least one real regulatory or professional requirement relevant to the student's industry (e.g., AHPRA, QBCC, ASIC, Privacy Act), or explicitly names categories of output that need human review before use
  - Test prompt shows the student actually using the context document — their prompt does not re-explain things already in the context document, indicating they understand the workflow
  - Claude's response in the test reflects the stated tone and client type — i.e., the context document is doing its job
  - The 1–2 sentence note is genuinely reflective: it identifies something specific that the document either missed or could be sharper on

- **Penalise (dock points):**
  - Context document reads as a general business description or marketing copy — "we are dedicated to delivering value to our clients" — without operational specificity
  - Constraints section is absent, or contains only generic instructions ("be professional," "don't make things up") that apply to any Claude session
  - Tone section is only adjectives with no examples or specifics ("professional and warm")
  - Test prompt re-explains things already in the context document, suggesting the student hasn't understood the workflow
  - Claude's response doesn't appear to have been influenced by the context document — generic output that could have come from any prompt
  - Context document is either under 200 words (too thin to be operational) or over 600 words (too long to be practical)

- **Common 60–69 patterns (near-pass):** The document covers most components but the constraints section is either absent or generic. The student has described who they are and what they do well, but hasn't thought through the rules — what Claude should flag, what shouldn't go into outputs, what regulatory environment applies. In feedback, name the missing or thin constraint, explain why it matters for their specific industry, and give them one concrete example of what a better constraint would look like in their context. End by telling them to keep this document open for Lesson 1.6.

- **Common 80+ patterns (excellent):** The context document reads like a real operational brief — someone picking it up with no other context could immediately start working effectively for this business. The constraints section names specific regulatory or professional requirements. The tone section includes at least one concrete example of language to use or avoid. The test prompt demonstrates that the student understands they don't need to repeat the context in their task prompt. - **Feedback tone:** Direct, specific, and kind. Quote the student's own words from their context document when identifying wins or gaps — never give generic feedback. If the constraints section is thin, name the specific regulatory or professional context that applies to their industry and explain what a constraint in that area would look like. - **Resubmission gating:** If the student resubmits, compare their revised context document against the stored original. The most common required revision is strengthening the constraints section. If the revision added length without adding specificity, say so directly: longer is not better — sharper is better. Point to the one section that still needs more precision.

## Industry personalisation hooks

The five components of a context document are universal, but the content within each section shifts significantly by industry. In the **who you are** section, the key swappable nouns are: industry (trades, health, finance, property, retail, agency), business size, and primary service type. In the **clients** section: client description, communication channel preferences, and sophistication level. In the **tone** section: formality level and industry-specific language to avoid (legal jargon for lawyers, medical jargon for health, technical jargon for engineers). In the **tasks** section: the specific recurring outputs Claude will help with — which differ entirely by industry. In the **constraints** section: the regulatory environment is the primary differentiator — AHPRA for health practitioners, ASIC/AFS licensing for financial services, QBCC for Queensland builders, Privacy Act considerations across all industries. The worked examples in this lesson represent bookkeeping (Example 1), construction (Example 2), and marketing consulting (Example 4) — personalised course variants can substitute the student's own industry into any of these examples while leaving the five-component structure unchanged.
