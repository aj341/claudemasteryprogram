## Learning objectives
By the end of this lesson you'll be able to:
- List 10–15 recurring tasks from your own work with enough specificity to assess each one
- Categorise those tasks using the frequency × skill framework introduced in this lesson
- Identify your top 3 Claude opportunities and articulate why each one is a good fit
- Rule out at least one task and explain why Claude is not the right tool for it
- Leave this lesson with a concrete task map you can refer back to throughout the course

## Lesson script

### Why "I should use AI more" doesn't work

Most people who've used Claude a few times fall into the same pattern. They use it when they remember to, for whatever task is in front of them at that moment, without any real system. A few weeks in, they've had some wins and some misses, and they can't quite explain why.

The problem isn't the tool. The problem is the approach. "Using AI more" is not a strategy. It's an intention, and intentions don't survive a busy week.

The fix is simple: know exactly which tasks you want Claude to handle. Not in theory — in your actual work, in the specific tasks you do every week. When you have that list, using Claude becomes a decision you've already made, not a decision you have to make fresh each time.

This lesson is where you build that list. You'll identify your recurring tasks, run them through a simple framework to see which ones are good candidates for Claude, and leave with a concrete task map — something you'll actually use, not something you file away and forget.

There's a second reason this matters. From Lesson 1.5 onward, this course personalises examples and exercises to your actual work. The task map you build here is how the platform knows what your work looks like. The more specific you are in this lesson, the more relevant every subsequent lesson becomes.

### A 2×2 framework for categorising your tasks

Not every task is equally suited to Claude. The useful question isn't "can Claude help with this?" — it usually can, in some way. The useful question is "is this one of the tasks where Claude saves me meaningful time or improves the quality of what I produce?"

A simple framework helps answer that. Plot your tasks on two axes: how often you do them, and how much skill or judgment they require from you.

**Axis 1 — Frequency:** Is this task something you do every week, or something you do occasionally — once a month, once a quarter, when a particular situation comes up?

**Axis 2 — Skill level:** Does this task require significant professional judgment, deep knowledge of your clients or context, or are the parameters fairly fixed and the main challenge is time and effort?

This gives you four quadrants:

**High-frequency, lower-skill tasks.** These are your quick wins. You do them often, they're fairly routine, and the main cost is time. Client appointment reminders, formatting reports, drafting standard email responses, filling in templates — Claude can handle these well, and the cumulative time saving across a week adds up faster than you'd expect. Start here.

**High-frequency, higher-skill tasks.** These are your highest-value opportunities. You do them constantly, and they're the tasks that actually require your expertise — writing proposals, producing analysis, drafting advice, creating client-facing content that has to sound like you. These are harder to hand to Claude wholesale, but used as a co-writer or thinking partner, Claude can shorten a 2-hour task to 45 minutes. This quadrant is where most students find the biggest long-run gains.

**Low-frequency, lower-skill tasks.** These tasks come up occasionally and don't take much judgment. Use Claude when they arise — there's no urgency to build a system around them, but it's worth knowing you have the option. Meeting agendas for one-off presentations, travel expense summaries, onboarding checklists for a new hire.

**Low-frequency, higher-skill tasks.** These require your professional judgment, but they don't happen often enough to be your focus. Use Claude for research and early drafting — it can get you 60% of the way there — but these tasks need more careful human review than the others. Submissions to regulators, complex client contracts, anything with significant legal or compliance implications.

You don't need a perfect categorisation. The goal is to quickly sort your tasks so you know where to direct your attention in the next four lessons.

### What makes a task "Claude-suitable"

Before you build your list, here are four signals that a task is a good fit for Claude.

**The input and output are both text.** Claude reads text and writes text. If a task involves reading something and producing something written, Claude can help. If it involves physical work, a live conversation, or a real-time system, Claude is the wrong tool.

**The task benefits from drafting or summarising.** Writing a first draft, summarising a long document, extracting key information from a report — these are Claude's natural habitat. If a task is currently costing you time because you're staring at a blank page or wading through long documents, Claude is likely to help.

**The task doesn't depend on information Claude doesn't have.** Real-time data — current stock prices, live system status, today's news — is outside Claude's reach unless you're using a version with web access enabled. Anything requiring information that's private to your business (client records, internal databases) needs to come from you, pasted in or described in the prompt.

**Professional sign-off is still possible.** Claude producing a draft doesn't mean the draft goes out. For tasks where accuracy matters — a client-facing document, a regulatory submission, a formal letter of advice — Claude helps you produce the first version faster, and you review it before it leaves your desk. That's fine. Where it doesn't work is when the task requires a licensed professional's judgment and there's no review step in the process. That's a boundary, not a failing of the tool.

### Identifying quick wins versus strategic uses

When you look at your task list, you'll probably notice two types of opportunity.

Quick wins are tasks you can try with Claude today, with a basic prompt and minimal preparation. These tasks are clearly text-based, the parameters are well-defined, and the output doesn't carry high stakes if it needs a revision or two. A status update email, a meeting summary, a draft FAQ for your website, a first pass at a job advertisement. These are the tasks to try in this week's practice.

Strategic uses take a bit more setup. They're often your most important recurring tasks — the ones where the quality of the output really matters. To use Claude well on these, you need a good prompt, probably a few passes to refine the output, and a clear review step. But once you've figured out the prompt that works for, say, your monthly client report, you have a template you can reuse every month. The upfront time pays back quickly.

The mistake people make is trying to start with their hardest, most high-stakes task — because that's where the potential upside is biggest. But the first time you use Claude for something that really matters, you haven't built the skill yet to get the best out of it. Start with a quick win. Build confidence in the tool and in your prompting. Then bring Claude into the work that matters most.

Here is what that looks like in practice. Before you try to use Claude to draft your quarterly business review, try it for the summary email you send your team after each weekly meeting. Same writing skill required. Lower stakes. You'll learn what works for your style, your context, and your team — and you'll be ready to tackle the quarterly review with a prompt that actually produces something useful.

```
Before you build your task map, run this quick check prompt in Claude:

"I run a [type of business] and I'm mapping the tasks I want to use Claude for.
Here are five tasks I do regularly: [list them]. For each one, tell me:
1. Whether it's a strong, moderate, or weak candidate for Claude
2. The main thing that would make it work or not work

Give me a clear answer for each — no hedging."
```

This check takes under two minutes. It often surfaces considerations you hadn't thought of — and it's a useful first exercise in being specific about your work.

### Common mistake → better approach

**Mistake:** Listing tasks at too high a level. "Client communication" is on the list. "Report writing" is on the list. These entries look like tasks, but they're actually categories. When you sit down to use Claude for "client communication," you still have to decide which client, which communication, what it should say, and what format it should take. The high-level entry doesn't help you act.

**Better approach:** Write each task as you'd write it in a project management tool — specific enough that someone reading the list could start on the task without asking a clarifying question. "Write the weekly project status email to our three main retail clients" is a task. "Draft a holding response for clients who enquire about pricing before we've quoted" is a task. "Summarise the meeting notes from our fortnightly ops review into action points for the team" is a task.

When your task map is specific at this level, it becomes genuinely useful. You can look at it on a Monday morning and decide which of your Claude tasks to work on first. Vague entries on a list produce no action. Specific ones produce exactly the prompt you need to write.

## Worked examples

### Example 1: Operations manager identifying quick wins

**Scenario:** An operations manager at a small logistics firm wants to know where to start with Claude.

**Prompt:**
```
I'm the operations manager at a small freight logistics company in Perth — around
25 staff. I handle: weekly ops reports to the director, driver briefing notes,
client exception notifications when deliveries are delayed, and monthly KPI
summaries for our 10 largest accounts.

Which of these four tasks is the best place to start with Claude? Tell me which
one to try first and why, and what I'd need to include in my first prompt to
make it work for that task.
```
**Why it works:** The prompt lists four specific, named tasks rather than job categories. Asking for a recommendation (which one first, and why) produces a useful answer rather than a list of possibilities. Asking for the first prompt elements means the answer is immediately actionable.

### Example 2: Marketing coordinator building a full task map

**Scenario:** A marketing coordinator at an accounting firm wants to systematically identify where Claude fits in her week.

**Prompt:**
```
I coordinate marketing for a mid-size accounting firm in Melbourne. My recurring
weekly tasks include:
- Writing the monthly client newsletter (takes me about 3 hours each time)
- Drafting social media posts for LinkedIn (2-3 per week)
- Preparing talking points for the partners' quarterly client events
- Summarising client feedback surveys into a monthly report for the partners
- Writing job ads when we hire (2-3 times per year)

Using the frequency × skill framework — frequency being how often I do it,
skill being how much professional judgment it requires — place each of these
five tasks in the right quadrant. Then tell me which two to try with Claude
first and which one needs the most careful review before I send it out.
```
**Why it works:** The student provides the framework (frequency × skill) and asks Claude to apply it to her actual tasks. This produces a structured analysis, not a generic answer. The final question — which one needs careful review — shows she's already thinking about quality control, which is the right instinct for client-facing content at a professional services firm.

### Example 3: Sole trader ruling out a task

**Scenario:** A self-employed financial planner wants to be clear about where Claude doesn't belong in his work.

**Prompt:**
```
I'm a self-employed financial planner in Brisbane, licensed under my own AFSL.
I'm mapping out which tasks I'll use Claude for and which ones I'll keep entirely
to myself.

I want you to be direct: which of these tasks should I NOT use Claude for,
even as a starting draft? And for each one you flag, tell me exactly why —
not a general warning, a specific reason that applies to financial advice in
Australia.

Tasks:
1. Drafting statements of advice for clients
2. Summarising a client's current financial position before a review meeting
3. Writing my monthly email newsletter on general financial topics
4. Preparing questions for a client discovery meeting
5. Writing my internal compliance log notes
```
**Why it works:** The explicit instruction ("be direct," "not a general warning, a specific reason that applies to financial advice in Australia") prevents Claude from producing a hedged non-answer. Asking for task-by-task assessment of what NOT to use Claude for is exactly the kind of boundary-setting that a regulated professional needs to do — and Claude can help them think it through clearly.

### Example 4: Agency account manager spotting strategic uses

**Scenario:** An account manager at a small creative agency wants to identify where Claude can improve the quality of her work, not just save time.

**Prompt:**
```
I'm an account manager at a 12-person creative agency in Auckland. My most
important recurring tasks are:

- Writing client briefs before we kick off a project (usually 2-3 per month)
- Preparing for client review presentations — pulling together notes on what
  we're presenting and why
- Writing end-of-project reports that summarise results and learnings
- Drafting scope-of-work documents for new project proposals

I'm not looking for time savings — I'm looking for tasks where having Claude
as a thinking partner would actually improve the quality of what I produce.
Which of these four tasks is the best candidate for that, and how would I
use Claude differently for quality improvement versus speed?
```
**Why it works:** The question distinguishes between two different types of value — time and quality — and asks Claude to engage with the more nuanced one. This produces a more useful answer than a generic "Claude can help you with all of these." The student's framing also signals she's already thought about this and wants a real answer, which tends to produce one.

### Example 5: Retail business owner with mixed digital and physical tasks

**Scenario:** A small retail shop owner wants to understand which of her tasks actually translate to Claude and which don't.

**Prompt:**
```
I own a small homewares retail shop in Hobart — physical store plus an online
shop on Shopify. My recurring tasks include:

- Writing product descriptions for new stock (weekly, around 10-15 per week)
- Responding to customer enquiries via email and Instagram DMs
- Writing the monthly email to our customer list
- Managing stock reorders — working out what to reorder based on what's sold
- Writing social media captions for our product posts
- Processing refunds and updating customers about their returns

Using the criteria for Claude-suitable tasks, tell me which of these six tasks
are a strong fit, which are a weak fit, and which won't work at all — with a
brief reason for each.
```
**Why it works:** The task list is concrete and industry-specific. Asking for a three-tier assessment (strong fit, weak fit, won't work) is more useful than a binary yes/no. The student has included some tasks that clearly won't work (stock reorders require real-time data, returns processing requires system access) — and seeing those correctly excluded confirms the framework is working.

## Graded deliverable

**Title:** Your task map

**Brief:** This is the hands-on output of Lesson 1.4. You're going to document your actual recurring work, categorise it using the frequency × skill framework, and identify where to start with Claude.

Don't write hypothetical tasks or tasks from a job you used to do. Write the tasks you actually do, in the words you'd use to describe them to a colleague. The specificity of what you submit here directly shapes how useful the rest of this course is — from Lesson 1.5 onward, the platform uses your task map to personalise examples to your work.

Once you've completed your task map (see What to submit below), paste everything into the **Submission box** at the bottom of this lesson on the Claude Mastery platform. The AI grading engine returns your grade card within 60 seconds.

**What to submit:**
1. A task list of 10–15 recurring tasks from your work — weekly or monthly tasks, written specifically (e.g. "Write the end-of-week update email to our 4 project clients" not "client emails"). For each task, note: the approximate frequency (weekly, fortnightly, monthly) and your best-guess quadrant from the framework (high-frequency/lower-skill, high-frequency/higher-skill, low-frequency/lower-skill, or low-frequency/higher-skill).
2. Your top 3 Claude opportunities — the three tasks from your list you'll try with Claude first. For each one, write one sentence explaining why it's a good candidate (be specific: what makes it Claude-suitable? time cost? text-heavy? clear parameters?).
3. One task explicitly ruled out — a task from your list, or one you considered including, that you've decided Claude is not the right tool for. Write 2–3 sentences explaining why: what makes it unsuitable (real-time data? professional sign-off without review? physical action? something else?).

**Where to submit:** Paste everything into the **Submission box** at the bottom of this lesson on the Claude Mastery platform. The AI grading engine returns your grade card within 60 seconds.

**Length guide:** Task list: 10–15 tasks, 1–2 lines each. Top 3 opportunities: one sentence of rationale per task. Ruled-out task: 2–3 sentences. Total submission: approximately 300–500 words.

**Pass mark:** 70 / 100. If you score below 70, the platform shows your grade card with feedback per criterion and activates a **Revise & Resubmit** button after a 24-hour cooldown.

**Rubric (100 points total — pass at 70):**
- **Specificity (25 pts)** — Tasks are written with enough detail that the grader can identify the student's industry and role. Generic entries like "emails," "reports," or "client communication" without further description score low. Specific entries like "Draft the monthly performance summary I send to our 6 franchise partners" score high. The top 3 opportunities include a specific, named rationale — not just "it takes a lot of time."
- **Structure (25 pts)** — All three components are present and clearly labelled: the full task list with frequency and quadrant notes, the top 3 opportunities with rationale, and the ruled-out task with explanation. The task list contains 10–15 items. Tasks are categorised (quadrant assigned) — not just listed.
- **Constraint clarity (25 pts)** — The ruled-out task explanation names a specific reason Claude is unsuitable — real-time data dependency, need for professional sign-off without review, physical/system action required, or similar. "Claude might make mistakes" is not a sufficient reason. The top 3 opportunity rationales name specific Claude-suitability criteria from the lesson (text-based input/output, benefits from drafting, clear parameters, etc.).
- **Outcome focus (25 pts)** — The task map is grounded in the student's real work. The top 3 opportunities are clearly the student's best starting points — not the easiest tasks or the most impressive-sounding ones, but the ones where Claude is genuinely likely to save time or improve quality. The ruled-out task shows the student understands Claude's limits in their specific context, not just in general.

## Notes for the AI grading engine

These notes are read by the grading engine when scoring submissions for this lesson. They are not shown to the student.

- **Reward (high marks):**
  - Task list entries are specific enough that the grader can picture the student's actual job — role, industry, and type of work are all visible from reading the list
  - Each task has a frequency note and a quadrant assignment — the student has done the categorisation work, not just listed tasks
  - Top 3 opportunity rationales reference specific Claude-suitability criteria from the lesson (text-based, benefits from drafting, clear parameters, no real-time data required) rather than vague positives ("it takes a long time" or "Claude would be good at this")
  - The ruled-out task explanation names a precise reason tied to the student's specific context — not a generic "Claude isn't reliable enough"
  - Task variety is evident — not 10 variations of the same type of task

- **Penalise (dock points):**
  - Task list has fewer than 10 items, or items are category labels rather than named tasks ("writing" instead of "write the weekly project update to our clients")
  - No frequency or quadrant notes — the student has listed tasks but not categorised them
  - Top 3 rationales are generic ("this is text-based" applied to all three) or repeat the same reason across all three opportunities
  - Ruled-out task is absent, or the reason given is vague ("Claude might get it wrong") without a specific explanation tied to the task or the student's context
  - Task list appears hypothetical or generic — tasks that could belong to any small business in any industry, with no visible industry or role context

- **Common 60–69 patterns (near-pass):** The student submits all three components but task descriptions remain vague — "write emails to clients" rather than "write the weekly check-in email to our 5 active project clients." Frequency is noted but quadrant assignments are missing or generic. The top 3 rationale says something like "this one takes a lot of time" without connecting to a specific Claude-suitability criterion. The ruled-out task explanation is present but thin — one sentence where two or three were needed. In feedback, quote one of their vague task descriptions back to them and show them what a specific version would look like. Ask: who is this email to? How often? What does it typically cover?

- **Common 80+ patterns (excellent):** The task list reads like a real person's working week — you can tell the student's industry, their role, and the type of clients or colleagues they work with. Every task has a frequency and a quadrant. The top 3 rationales are different from each other and each names a distinct reason why that task suits Claude. The ruled-out task explanation is thoughtful — it addresses the specific reason that task is unsuitable and shows the student understands what that means for their workflow. The submission as a whole feels like a genuine working document the student will refer back to.

- **Feedback tone:** Direct, specific, and kind. Quote the student's own task descriptions when identifying wins or fix points — never generic. If task entries are vague, quote one back and show exactly what a more specific version would say. Always end with one concrete next step: identify which of their top 3 Claude opportunities to try first in the next lesson, and name the specific prompt element they should focus on to make that task work.

- **Resubmission gating:** If the student resubmits, compare against the original stored in the platform. The most common resubmission fix is adding specificity to task descriptions and rationales. If they've done this — more specific task descriptions, quadrant assignments where there were none, richer rationale for top 3 — name that improvement explicitly and reference what changed. If the resubmission is essentially the same list with a few extra words but the task descriptions are still vague, say so directly: "Your second submission has more words, but the task entries are still at the category level. The fix is precision, not length." Then quote one entry and show the specific version.

## Industry personalisation hooks

The five worked examples span five distinct business types: freight logistics (operations), professional services/accounting (marketing coordinator), regulated financial advice (sole trader planner), creative agency (account management), and retail (physical and online). The core teaching — the 2×2 framework, the four Claude-suitability criteria, quick wins versus strategic uses, and the common mistake on task specificity — is universal and stays constant. Swappable nouns in the lesson script include "project clients" (agency or construction), "franchise partners" (retail or hospitality), "compliance log notes" (financial services, health), "KPI summaries" (any B2B services business), and "product descriptions" (e-commerce, retail, food and beverage). In personalised lesson variants, Example 1 (quick wins) and Example 5 (mixed fit tasks) are the highest-priority substitutions — replace the industry, tasks, and location with the learner's context while keeping the prompt structure and the three-tier assessment format intact.
