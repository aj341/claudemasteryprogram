## Lesson script

### Choosing the right first Project

The single biggest mistake people make when setting up their first Claude Project is trying to build the most complete one possible. They pick the broadest, most ambitious use case — "a Project for everything marketing" or "a Project for all client communications" — and then spend so long trying to configure it that they never actually use it.

Don't do that.

Your first Project should solve one specific problem you have this week. Not next month. This week. The test is simple: could this Project save you a meaningful chunk of time or produce a clearly better output before Friday? If yes, it's the right starting point. If you're thinking months ahead, you've picked the wrong one.

Here's how to identify it. Look at your task list from Lesson 1.4 — the map of your recurring weekly work. Find the task that has all three of these qualities: you do it often, it's frustrating to do from scratch each time, and it requires consistent context that you're currently re-explaining manually. That's your first Project.

For most small business owners, one of these three candidates usually wins:

**Client communication.** You write emails and messages to clients constantly, and Claude doesn't know your tone, your business name, your typical relationship style, or any of the recurring topics. A Client Communication Project changes that. Every message Claude helps you draft already knows who you are.

**Proposal or quote drafting.** You write similar proposals regularly, but each one needs your specific service structure, pricing framework, and language. A Proposal Project makes every draft start from your actual offering, not a blank page.

**Internal documentation.** You create SOPs, training notes, and process guides but they never quite sound like your business. A Documentation Project gives Claude your terminology, your standards, and your format preferences so the output is ready to use, not ready to rewrite.

Pick one. Build it properly. Use it for two weeks. Then build the next one.

### Writing effective custom instructions

Custom instructions are the core of any Project. They tell Claude who you are, what this Project is for, and how it should behave every time you use it. A weak set of instructions produces output that's almost right but always needs work. A strong set produces output you can use with minimal editing.

Use the 5-part structure. Every part matters.

**Part 1 — Who you are.** Your name, your role, your business name, what your business does, how many people work there, and what state or region you're in. Claude needs to know the actual facts — not a general category. "I'm a sole trader" is almost useless. "I'm Sarah Park, sole-trader accountant in Hobart, servicing 22 small business clients in hospitality and retail" gives Claude what it needs to calibrate everything that follows.

**Part 2 — What this Project is for.** State the specific purpose of this Project in one or two sentences. This sets the scope. If the Project is for client proposals, say so. Claude should understand, from this section alone, what kinds of tasks to expect. It should also understand what's out of scope — don't ask Claude to guess the boundaries.

**Part 3 — What Claude should always do.** List the consistent behaviours you want in every response. Use Australian English. Match the tone of the voice you've described. Start every proposal with the client's name and our company name in the header. Always flag anything that looks like a compliance risk. These are the standing instructions that apply to every single interaction in this Project.

**Part 4 — What Claude should never do.** This is the section most people skip, and it's where a lot of Projects fail. "Never do" instructions need to be specific and operational — not generic. "Never be rude" is not useful. Claude isn't going to be rude. Useful "never do" instructions sound like this: "Never suggest strategies that require more than two people to execute." "Never include pricing estimates — I provide those manually after Claude drafts the scope." "Never use the words 'leverage' or 'synergy' in any output." "Never recommend the client use another provider for any part of our core service." These are constraints that have real consequences if violated.

**Part 5 — The default output format.** What does a typical output from this Project look like? Bullet lists? Short paragraphs? A structured document with headings? A specific word count range? State it here. If most of your outputs are emails under 200 words, say so. If most are structured proposals with four sections, describe those sections. Format defaults save you the effort of specifying format in every prompt.

Here's what a complete set of custom instructions looks like in practice:

```
WHO I AM
I'm Marcus Webb, Project Manager at Webb Residential Constructions — a family-run
builder in southeast Queensland. We build new homes and major renovations, typically
8–12 active projects at any time. I manage four site supervisors and handle all
subcontractor and client communication. I report to my father, who is the director.

WHAT THIS PROJECT IS FOR
This Project is for drafting and reviewing client-facing communications: progress
updates, variation notices, delay notifications, and responses to client questions.
This Project is NOT for internal operations, financial documents, or legal correspondence.

WHAT CLAUDE SHOULD ALWAYS DO
- Write in plain, direct Australian English — no construction jargon unless I use
  it first
- Keep emails to three paragraphs or fewer unless I specifically request more
- When a message involves a scope or cost change, structure it as: (1) what changed,
  (2) why it changed, (3) what happens next
- Flag any draft that involves a dispute, complaint, or potential claim with the
  note "REVIEW BEFORE SENDING — possible dispute territory"

WHAT CLAUDE SHOULD NEVER DO
- Make any commitment about final delivery dates — I'll add those manually
- Suggest the client speak to a competing builder or third-party consultant
- Use apologetic language that implies the company is at fault before I've confirmed
  that — write "we're looking into this" not "we're sorry for the problem"
- Include specific dollar figures in any draft — I always insert those after review

DEFAULT OUTPUT FORMAT
Email format: subject line, then three short paragraphs of plain prose. No bullet
lists in client emails. Professional but conversational tone — not corporate.
Under 200 words unless I request otherwise.
```

That is 271 words. It takes 20 minutes to write once. It saves you time and inconsistency across every session you use this Project.

### Common mistake → better approach

**Mistake:** Writing "never do" instructions that describe generic AI behaviour — things like "never make things up" or "always be accurate." These are baseline expectations for any Claude interaction. They don't actually constrain the Project's behaviour in ways that matter for your specific work.

**Better approach:** Write "never do" instructions that reflect the specific risks and standards of your business. Ask yourself: what's the one thing Claude could produce in this Project that would cause me a real problem? A commitment I didn't authorise? A tone that damages a client relationship? A suggestion that conflicts with my service model? That's your "never do." It should be specific enough that you can picture the exact output it prevents.

### Selecting and preparing knowledge documents

Custom instructions tell Claude how to behave. Knowledge documents tell Claude what to know. They're different, and it's worth being clear on that distinction before you start uploading files.

Knowledge documents are the reference material Claude draws on when you're working. For a Client Communication Project, that might be your service agreement template, your standard terms, or a one-page description of each service you offer. For a Proposal Project, it might be your pricing tiers, your scope definitions, and a set of example proposals that represent the quality and format you're aiming for.

The rule for selecting documents is: include what Claude genuinely needs to know to produce a better output than it could without it. That sounds obvious, but most people violate it by treating their Project like a filing cabinet. They upload everything that feels relevant and end up with a cluttered, unfocused Project where Claude has to wade through documents to find what it needs.

Keep it focused. A good first Project has two to four documents, not twenty. Each document should answer one of these questions: What do we actually offer? What does a finished output look like? What rules apply to this type of work?

What not to include: confidential client records, anything containing personal data about individuals, financial statements, documents that change frequently enough that Claude's version will be out of date within weeks, and long strategic documents that contain only a small amount of information Claude actually needs. If a document is 50 pages but only 5 pages are relevant, extract those 5 pages and upload that version.

Prepare your documents before uploading. Remove client names if they appear. Check that the document actually represents your current practice — don't upload an SOP from 2022 if it's been superseded. Give each file a clear filename so you can tell at a glance what it contains.

### Testing your Project before you rely on it

Before you use this Project for real work, run three test prompts. Each test checks a different thing.

**Test 1: Context check.** Ask Claude to summarise what it knows about you and this Project. You're checking that the custom instructions have been read and understood. The response should name your business, describe the Project's purpose, and be able to list the key "always do" and "never do" instructions. If Claude gives you a generic response or seems to be missing key facts, your custom instructions need revision.

**Test 2: Task test.** Send a realistic task — the kind of thing you'll actually use this Project for. If it's a Client Communication Project, draft a message about a common scenario: a project update, a response to a query, a variation notice. Review the output against your expectations. Does it match your tone? Does it apply the format defaults? Does it avoid the things you told it to avoid? This is where you catch the gaps in your instructions before they matter.

**Test 3: Constraint test.** Send a prompt that deliberately triggers one of your "never do" rules. If you told Claude never to include pricing estimates, ask it to draft a proposal and see if it includes pricing. If you told it to flag dispute-related content, send a message that describes a client complaint and check that the flag appears. If your constraints are working, Claude should either comply with the rule or tell you why it can't. If it ignores the constraint entirely, rewrite that instruction to be more specific.

Once all three tests produce results that look right, your Project is ready. Use it for real.

### The maintenance cycle

A Claude Project is not a one-time setup. It's a working document that should evolve as your business does.

You need to update your Project when any of the following happen: your services change, your standard terms change, your team structure changes, you find Claude consistently getting something wrong (that's a sign a rule is missing or unclear), or a worked example you uploaded is no longer representative of what you want.

The maintenance interval for most small businesses is every one to three months. Set a calendar reminder. Open your Project, read the custom instructions, and ask yourself: does this still describe how my business works? Does the "never do" list still cover the risks I actually face? Are the knowledge documents still current?

This is a 15-minute task when you do it regularly. It becomes a two-hour task if you let the Project drift for 12 months without checking.

The students who get the most value from Claude Projects are not the ones who set up the most sophisticated configuration on day one. They're the ones who use the Project consistently, notice what's working and what isn't, and make small adjustments over time. A Project that's been refined over six months is dramatically more useful than one that was carefully planned and never touched again.

## Worked examples

### Example 1: Client communication — residential trades

**Scenario:** A plumber sets up a Project for customer-facing communications.

**Prompt:**
```
I need to send a message to a customer named Kerry. We completed her hot water
system replacement yesterday. The job went well — finished on time, no surprises.
I want to follow up, make sure she's happy, and mention our annual service reminder
system so she stays on our books.
```
**Why it works:** The custom instructions for this Project already contain the business name (Blackwater Plumbing), the service area (Sunshine Coast), the tone (warm and direct, never corporate), and a "never do" rule about not mentioning competitors. The prompt itself is short because the Project carries all the standing context. Claude produces a message that sounds like it came from this specific business, not a generic trades company.

---

### Example 2: Proposal drafting — marketing consultant

**Scenario:** A marketing consultant uses a Proposal Project to draft a new engagement scope.

**Prompt:**
```
Draft a proposal for a new client: a 12-person financial planning firm in
Canberra. They want help with their LinkedIn presence and a quarterly email
newsletter for their existing clients. Three-month engagement. Budget
conversation hasn't happened yet — leave pricing blank.
```
**Why it works:** The Proposal Project's knowledge documents include a scope template, service descriptions for LinkedIn and email marketing, and two anonymised example proposals. The "never do" instructions include never including pricing estimates (only the consultant adds those). Claude drafts a structured, on-brand proposal in the right format. The consultant adds the numbers, reviews the scope, and sends it — rather than starting from a blank page.

---

### Example 3: Internal documentation — retail manager

**Scenario:** A retail store manager uses a Documentation Project to write onboarding materials for new casual staff.

**Prompt:**
```
Write the opening shift checklist for a new casual staff member starting in
our homewares section. Should cover everything from unlocking to being
ready for customers. Keep it short enough to laminate.
```
**Why it works:** The Documentation Project's custom instructions specify the store name (Trellis & Co, Hobart), the tone (friendly, clear, not condescending), and a format default (numbered steps, plain English, under one A4 page). The knowledge documents include the current store layout, the POS system name, and the previous version of the checklist. Claude produces an accurate, on-brand draft that reflects this specific store's setup — not a generic retail template.

---

### Example 4: Testing a constraint

**Scenario:** A bookkeeper tests their Client Communication Project before using it.

**Prompt:**
```
Test prompt: draft a response to a client who's asking whether they should
switch from cash to accrual accounting. Include your recommendation.
```
**Why it works:** The Project's "never do" instructions include: "Never give a recommendation on accounting method changes — flag this for me to address personally or refer to the client's accountant." When Claude receives this prompt, it should produce a draft that acknowledges the question but explicitly flags that the recommendation needs the bookkeeper's review before sending — and directs the client to their accountant. If the constraint is working, this is exactly what happens. If Claude ignores it and gives a recommendation anyway, the bookkeeper knows the instruction needs to be rewritten.

---

### Example 5: Updating a Project after 60 days

**Scenario:** A photographer updates their Quote & Booking Project after noticing a consistent gap.

**Prompt:**
```
I've been using this Project for two months. I keep having to manually add
"payment terms: 50% deposit on booking, 50% on delivery" to every quote
because Claude keeps leaving it out. How should I update my custom
instructions to fix this?
```
**Why it works:** This is exactly the kind of maintenance prompt that keeps a Project useful. The photographer noticed a consistent gap — a missing piece of information that Claude should know by default. The fix is straightforward: add payment terms to the custom instructions under "what Claude should always do." This is how Projects improve over time — not by rebuilding from scratch, but by making small, targeted refinements based on real use.

## Graded deliverable

**Title:** Build your first working Claude Project

**Brief:** This lesson's deliverable is the real thing. You're not writing about a Project or planning one — you're building and testing an actual Claude Project for your own business, and submitting the evidence that it's working.

Start by choosing which Project you'll build, using the criteria from the lesson. Write your custom instructions using the 5-part structure. Select and upload your knowledge documents. Run all three test prompts. Then submit your work below.

This deliverable is graded on the quality of what you built, not on what you planned to build. Paste everything into the **Submission box** at the bottom of this lesson on the Claude Mastery platform. The AI grading engine returns your grade card within 60 seconds.

**What to submit:**
1. **Your full custom instructions text** — all five parts, as they appear in your actual Project; no bracket placeholders, real business details throughout
2. **Your knowledge document list** — the name of each document you added and a one-sentence explanation of why you included it (or a note if you chose to include no documents and why)
3. **Your three test prompts and Claude's responses** — paste all three test prompts and Claude's full responses; label each as Test 1 (context check), Test 2 (task test), and Test 3 (constraint test)
4. **A 100-word assessment** — is this Project ready to use in real work? Be honest: what's working well? What's still missing or not behaving as expected? What's the first thing you'd refine?

**Where to submit:** Paste everything into the **Submission box** at the bottom of this lesson on the Claude Mastery platform. The AI grading engine returns your grade card within 60 seconds.

**Length guide:** Custom instructions: 200–400 words. Knowledge document list: 1–2 sentences per item. Three test prompts + full responses: paste in full, no trimming. Assessment: 80–120 words.

**Pass mark:** 70 / 100. If you score below 70, the platform shows your grade card with feedback per criterion and activates a **Revise & Resubmit** button after a 24-hour cooldown.

**Rubric (100 points total — pass at 70):**
- **Specificity (25 pts)** — Do the custom instructions use real business details throughout — named business, actual role, real industry context, specific service descriptions? Instructions that describe a generic category of business ("I am a small business owner who does marketing") rather than a named, specific operation score low. Instructions that read as if they could only have been written by one particular business score high.
- **Structure (25 pts)** — Do the custom instructions follow the 5-part structure taught in the lesson: who you are, what this Project is for, what Claude should always do, what Claude should never do, and the default output format? All five parts must be present and substantive. Labelling the parts without filling them in does not pass this criterion.
- **Constraint clarity (25 pts)** — Are the "never do" instructions specific and operational? Instructions like "never be rude" or "always be accurate" do not demonstrate constraint clarity — they describe baseline Claude behaviour. Instructions like "never suggest strategies that require more than two staff to execute" or "never include a pricing estimate — I add those manually" are specific, consequential, and testable. The three test prompts must include a constraint test that actively checks one of the "never do" rules.
- **Outcome focus (25 pts)** — Does the 100-word assessment honestly identify gaps, not just confirm that the Project works? A high-scoring assessment names at least one specific thing that's still missing or imperfect and states what the student would do to fix it. An assessment that reads as "it all worked great, very impressed" with no critical observation scores low on this criterion regardless of the quality of the rest of the submission.

## Notes for the AI grading engine

(Not shown to student)

- **Reward (high marks):**
  - Custom instructions name the actual business — not a category or a placeholder — and contain specific, observable facts about the business context: industry, team size, client type, service names, geographic location where relevant
  - All five parts of the custom instruction structure are present and substantive; "what this Project is for" explicitly states both what the Project covers and what it does not
  - "Never do" instructions are specific and consequential — they describe outputs that would cause real problems for this business, not generic AI caution
  - Test 3 (constraint test) uses a prompt that genuinely triggers a "never do" rule and the student reports on whether Claude complied — a student who chose a constraint that can't be triggered by a simple prompt and notes that in their assessment should receive partial credit
  - The 100-word assessment names a specific gap or refinement — not a vague sense that something could be better, but a concrete missing piece: a rule that isn't in the "never do" list, a knowledge document that would improve accuracy, a format default that isn't quite right
  - Knowledge document rationale is clear: the student explains why each document helps Claude produce better output, not just that the document is "relevant"

- **Penalise (dock points):**
  - Custom instructions still contain bracket placeholders such as [business name], [industry], [client type] — indicates the student did not complete the personalisation step
  - "Never do" section is absent, or contains only generic constraints that apply to any Claude interaction ("never make things up," "always be professional")
  - Test 1 (context check) shows Claude giving a generic or confused response, and the student did not notice or address this — the Project is not working and the student hasn't caught it
  - Three test prompts are all identical or very similar in type — the student has not tested the Project across different scenarios
  - The 100-word assessment claims the Project is fully ready with no gaps — this is almost never true of a first build and suggests the student did not read the output critically
  - Knowledge documents are listed but no rationale is provided for inclusion

- **Common 60–69 patterns:** Student has built a real Project with genuine business detail but the "never do" instructions are thin or generic. The custom instructions cover who they are and what the Project is for, but constraints are missing or vague. In feedback, quote their "never do" section back and ask: what's the one output this Project could produce that would cause you a real problem? That answer is their missing constraint. Give them one concrete example of what a strong constraint for their industry would look like, then ask them to rewrite the section and resubmit.

- **Common 80+ patterns:** Custom instructions read as if they were written by someone who knows exactly what outputs they need and what could go wrong. The "never do" rules are specific enough to picture the output they prevent. The three tests are genuinely distinct — context check, task, and constraint — and the student reports honestly on what each test revealed. The 100-word assessment names at least one specific gap and a concrete next step for addressing it. The student has included knowledge documents that reflect actual business content, not placeholder or sample files.

- **Feedback tone:** Direct, specific, kind. Quote the student's own words — from their custom instructions, their test prompts, or their assessment — when identifying wins or gaps. Never give generic feedback ("your instructions were good") without quoting what specifically worked. If the "never do" section is thin, quote it and contrast it with what a specific, operational constraint looks like. End every grade card with one concrete next step: the single highest-value change they could make before their next real use of this Project.

- **Resubmission gating:** Compare the resubmission against the original. The most common required improvement is strengthening the "never do" section or making the 100-word assessment genuinely honest about gaps. If the student has added a specific "never do" constraint where there was a generic one before, name that change explicitly and explain why it matters. If the assessment now names a specific gap where before it was only positive, acknowledge that shift as exactly the kind of critical thinking the criterion rewards. If the resubmission is longer but not more specific, say so: "You've added more words but the constraints are still describing baseline Claude behaviour rather than your specific business risks. Try answering: what could Claude produce in this Project that would cost you a client or cause you to send a message you'd regret?"

## Industry personalisation hooks

The 5-part custom instruction structure applies uniformly across industries. The swappable content in each part is determined by the student's own business — the lesson does not presuppose an industry. The worked examples cover residential trades (plumbing), professional services (marketing consulting), retail store management, bookkeeping, and photography, which represent a broad spread of the AU/NZ small business audience. Personalised course variants can add or substitute examples for health practices, legal administration, real estate, or hospitality without altering the structure. The three-test protocol (context check, task test, constraint test) is universal. The maintenance cycle guidance is deliberately framed as a calendar-based habit rather than tied to any specific business type, making it applicable across all student profiles.
