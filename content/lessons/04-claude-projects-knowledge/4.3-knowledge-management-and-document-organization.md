# Lesson 4.3 — Knowledge management and document organization

**Module:** 04 · Claude Projects & Knowledge

## Learning objectives

By the end of this lesson you'll be able to:
- Distinguish between "all your business documents" and the specific knowledge Claude actually needs for a given Project
- Sort your documents into the three knowledge categories: business context, process knowledge, and client/project knowledge
- Prepare any document so Claude can read and use it effectively
- Build a lightweight maintenance habit so your knowledge documents stay current
- Recognise what should never go into a Project

## Lesson script

### Not all documents belong in a Project

When people first discover that Claude Projects can hold documents, the instinct is to upload everything. Company brochure, price list, last year's proposals, photos of the team, the supplier contract from 2021, the spreadsheet you sort of use to track jobs. Everything gets loaded in, and then the Project is treated like a searchable filing cabinet.

This is the wrong mental model, and it creates real problems. Claude isn't a search engine for your files. It works best when the knowledge in a Project is deliberately chosen — not comprehensive, but relevant. Every document you add increases the chance that Claude draws on the wrong thing, or gets confused by conflicting details, or buries the useful information under material that doesn't apply.

The right question is not: what do I have? The right question is: what does Claude need to do this specific task well, that it doesn't already know?

That reframe changes which documents you choose. It also changes how you write or reformat them. A Project's knowledge is not a digital archive. It's a briefing pack — the minimum set of well-organised documents that gives Claude the context it needs to do real work without asking you to fill in the gaps every time.

### The three knowledge categories

Once you're thinking in terms of relevance rather than completeness, three categories cover almost everything a well-set-up Project should contain.

**Business context.** This is who you are, how you operate, and what makes your business distinct. It answers the background questions Claude would otherwise ask or guess at. Your business name, location, what you do, who your clients are, how you communicate, what you don't offer. The tone document from Lesson 1.5 belongs here. So does a one-page "about us" written for Claude — not for customers, but as a plain-English briefing on your business that a new team member would need on day one.

Business context documents don't change often. Once they're written and in the Project, they're mostly stable. You'll update them when something material changes — a new service line, a different client type, a rebrand — but you're not touching them weekly.

**Process knowledge.** This covers how you do things. Your quoting process. How you handle client complaints. The steps you follow when onboarding a new customer. How you structure a proposal. What happens after someone books a job. These documents exist so Claude understands your workflow and can help you at each stage of it — not just generate generic output, but output that fits how your business actually operates.

Process documents are most useful when written as clear steps, not paragraphs of explanation. Claude reads a numbered list of seven steps more reliably than a 400-word description of the same process. Format matters here, and you'll cover exactly why shortly.

**Client and project knowledge.** This is the context specific to a current engagement — a client brief, a project scope document, notes from a discovery call, a client's brand guide, the agreed deliverables for a contract you're delivering right now. This category is different from the other two: it changes with every new project and isn't useful once the project is done.

Most business owners have one Project per client or per active engagement, and the client/project knowledge documents in that Project are specific to that work. When the engagement ends, the Project is archived. The business context and process documents stay in your main Projects; the client-specific material goes with the client's Project.

Keep these three categories distinct. If you find yourself wondering where a document belongs, ask: does this describe my business permanently, does it describe how I do something, or does it describe a specific current piece of work? That question usually resolves it.

### Format matters: how to prepare a document for Claude

Not all document formats are equal. Claude reads some reliably; others produce variable results. The ranking from most reliable to least:

**Plain text** is the most reliable. A `.txt` or `.md` file with headings, numbered steps, and short sections is what Claude reads with the highest accuracy. When you write a document specifically for Claude — a process guide, a business context brief, a set of instructions — write it in plain text.

**Structured PDF** (text-based, not scanned) works well when the document is logically formatted. A proposal template or a price list in a clean PDF is fine. Claude can read it accurately if the text is selectable — i.e., not a scan or an image of a document.

**Image-based PDFs and photos of documents** are the least reliable. Claude can read them using vision, but accuracy drops when the layout is complex, when there's a lot of small text, or when the image quality is low. If an important document only exists as a scanned PDF or a photo, transcribe the relevant parts into plain text before adding it to a Project.

Length matters too. Claude holds a large amount of context, but that doesn't mean longer is better. A 200-word process document that covers exactly what Claude needs outperforms a 2,000-word one that buries the key steps in background explanation. For each document in your Project, ask: what does Claude actually need from this? Then strip everything else.

Structure matters as much as length. A heading followed by five bullet points beats a five-paragraph essay on the same content. Here's why: when Claude is generating output that needs to draw on your knowledge document, it scans for structure. Headings act as labels. Numbered lists signal sequence. Short paragraphs signal distinct points. Dense paragraphs of mixed information make it harder for Claude to locate what it needs quickly.

When preparing a document for a Project, follow this approach:

```
## Document name

One-line summary of what this document is for.

### Section heading

- Point one
- Point two
- Point three

### Section heading

Step 1: [action]
Step 2: [action]
Step 3: [action]
```

This structure takes five minutes to apply to a document you've already written. It pays back in more accurate outputs every time Claude draws on it.

### Common mistake → better approach

**Mistake:** Uploading an existing business document directly to a Project without reformatting it.

A client-facing brochure written to impress prospects is not a useful knowledge document for Claude. It's written in marketing language, it omits operational detail, it prioritises persuasion over accuracy, and it buries the information Claude actually needs (what you do, for whom, at what price, with what constraints) inside copy designed for a different purpose.

**Better approach:** Write a separate Claude-facing version of that document. Keep it short, plain, and specific. Write it the way you'd brief a smart new team member on their first day — factual, direct, structured. "We are a four-person bookkeeping firm in Hobart. We work exclusively with trade businesses. Our clients are typically 1–10 employees. We use Xero. We do not do tax returns — we refer those to our partner firm, Hargreaves Tax. Our standard turnaround for monthly reconciliation is five business days." That briefing is more useful to Claude than a six-page brochure.

### The update problem

Knowledge documents go stale. This is the most common reason a Project that worked well three months ago is producing weaker output now. The business changed. The pricing structure changed. A new service was added. A client finished their project. The knowledge documents didn't change with them, and now Claude is working from an outdated picture of your business.

The update problem doesn't need a complicated solution. You don't need a review calendar or a quarterly audit. You need one habit: when something about your business changes that a knowledge document covers, update the document that day.

The trigger is the change, not the calendar. You add a new service — update your business context document the same afternoon. You change your quoting process — update the process document before the next time you run the quote workflow. You onboard a new client — create or update their client knowledge document before you start using the Project for their work.

The sign that a document has gone stale isn't always obvious. Sometimes it shows up as Claude getting details slightly wrong — mentioning a price you no longer charge, describing a step that you've since removed, or failing to account for a constraint that now matters. When you see that, the fix is simple: find the document that contains the outdated information, correct it, and move on.

There's also a harder version of the update problem: knowledge that never existed as a document. Claude is making assumptions about how you handle something, because you never wrote down how you actually handle it. The output is off, but not because the document is wrong — there is no document. In that case, the fix is to write one. Five minutes of documentation now prevents the same correction ten times in the future.

### What not to store in a Project

Just as important as what goes in is what stays out. Three categories of information should never be stored in a Claude Project.

**Sensitive credentials.** Passwords, API keys, account numbers, login details, PINs. Claude Projects are not a password manager. Even if you trust the platform, storing credentials in a knowledge document creates a single location that could expose them if your account were ever accessed by someone else.

**Financial account details.** BSB and account numbers, credit card details, full banking records. A reference to the bank you use is fine. Your actual account number is not.

**Live operational data that changes frequently.** Your current job queue, today's staff roster, your bank balance, the status of open invoices. This kind of data goes stale within hours or days. Putting it in a knowledge document means Claude will use the stale version. Operational data belongs in the tools you use to manage it (your job management software, your accounting platform) — not in a static knowledge document.

The test is simple: if this information changed tomorrow and Claude used the old version, would that cause a problem? For credentials and financials, the answer is always yes. For operational data, it's almost always yes. Keep those out.

## Worked examples

### Example 1: Bookkeeping firm building a business context document

**Scenario:** A sole-trader bookkeeper in Hobart sets up a Client Communication Project and writes her core business context document for it.

**Prompt:**
```
You are helping me write client-facing communications for my bookkeeping
practice. Use this business context document when drafting any email,
proposal, or update to clients.

--- BUSINESS CONTEXT ---

Practice name: Harlow Books
Owner: Natalie Harlow
Location: Hobart, Tasmania
Years operating: 7

Who we work with:
- Trade businesses only (builders, plumbers, electricians, landscapers)
- Typical client: 1–8 employees, based in greater Hobart
- We do not work with retail, hospitality, or professional services firms

What we do:
- Monthly reconciliation (Xero)
- BAS preparation and lodgement
- Accounts payable and receivable management
- End-of-year preparation (hand-off to tax accountant)

What we don't do:
- Tax returns (we refer to Hargreaves Tax, Sandy Bay)
- Payroll processing
- Financial advice or planning

Turnaround times:
- Monthly reconciliation: 5 business days after statements received
- BAS: lodged 2 weeks before due date

Communication style:
- Plain English only — no accounting jargon in client emails
- Direct and practical — our clients are tradies, they read fast
- No corporate language or filler phrases

---

Draft an onboarding email for a new client: Reeve Plumbing, owner Marcus
Reeve, starting next month. Welcome him, confirm what's included in his
service, explain the next step (he needs to give Natalie Xero access and
his bank read-only view), and set the expectation for turnaround time.
Under 200 words.
```
**Why it works:** The knowledge document is plain text with clear sections and short, scannable points. It tells Claude what the practice does and what it does not do — both matter. The communication style instruction is specific enough to shape the output meaningfully. The prompt then calls on that document directly to do a real piece of work.

### Example 2: Tradie using a process document to handle quote follow-ups

**Scenario:** A painting contractor in Newcastle uses a Project to write follow-up emails after quotes. He loads a process document so Claude follows his actual follow-up sequence.

**Prompt:**
```
You're helping me write quote follow-up emails for my painting business.
Use the process document below. Do not deviate from the sequence.

--- FOLLOW-UP PROCESS: RESIDENTIAL QUOTES ---

Step 1: Send quote same day or next morning.
Step 2: If no response in 3 business days, send follow-up 1 (check in,
        any questions, restate availability).
Step 3: If no response after 5 more business days, send follow-up 2
        (last contact, soft close, offer to adjust scope if needed).
Step 4: If still no response, mark job as cold. No further contact.

Tone for all emails:
- Friendly and professional — not pushy
- We don't discount on follow-up unless the customer raises price first
- We don't mention competitors

---

I'm writing Follow-up 1 for a residential repaint quote sent Monday.
Client: Sandra Yuen, inner west Newcastle. Job: exterior repaint, 3BR
weatherboard, quoted $4,200. No response received.

Write the follow-up email. Under 150 words.
```
**Why it works:** The process document is numbered, sequential, and short. It removes ambiguity about tone (friendly but not pushy), establishes a rule Claude should follow (no discounting), and gives Claude the structure before the variable details of this specific job. The prompt references the step number directly so Claude knows exactly where in the sequence it is.

### Example 3: Marketing consultant distinguishing what to store vs. what to leave out

**Scenario:** A freelance marketing consultant in Perth sets up a client Project and decides which documents to include.

**Prompt:**
```
I'm setting up a Claude Project for a new client, Bloom Garden Centres
(three locations in the Perth metro area). I've attached their brand
guidelines document, which includes:

- Brand values and personality (3 pages)
- Logo usage rules (image-based, visual only)
- Approved colour palette and fonts (image-based, visual only)
- Target customer description (1 page, text)
- Tone of voice guidelines (1 page, text)
- Retail trading hours for each location (changes seasonally)
- Financial targets for this quarter (confidential, internal only)

Based on this list, which documents or sections should I actually include
in the Project knowledge, and which should I leave out? Give me a clear
recommendation with a one-line reason for each.
```
**Why it works:** This prompt uses Claude to help decide what goes into a Project — which is a legitimate and useful application. It describes the full document set and asks Claude to apply the same logic this lesson teaches: what's useful, what's image-only (low reliability), what's operationally live, what's sensitive. The response gives the student a practical model for auditing their own document set before loading anything in.

### Example 4: HR consultant preparing a stale document for update

**Scenario:** An HR consultant in Auckland has a Project with a business context document she wrote 18 months ago. Her services have changed. She updates it.

**Prompt:**
```
I need to update my business context document for my Claude Project.
Here is the current version, followed by what has changed since I
wrote it.

--- CURRENT DOCUMENT ---

Practice name: Kāpiti HR Advisory
Owner: Renata Hollis
Location: Auckland (remote-first, NZ-wide clients)
Services: HR policy writing, employment contract review, performance
review frameworks, staff handbook creation.
Clients: Businesses with 5–50 staff, across any industry.

--- WHAT HAS CHANGED ---

1. I no longer take clients outside Auckland and Wellington — too much
   travel. Update the geographic scope.
2. I added recruitment advisory as a service in March this year —
   shortlisting and interview design only, not headhunting.
3. I dropped employment contract review — I found it was too legally
   risky without a solicitor partner. Remove it.
4. I now work primarily with professional services firms (accounting,
   legal, consulting) — update the client description to reflect this.

Rewrite the business context document as a clean updated version
incorporating all four changes. Keep the same format as the original.
```
**Why it works:** This prompt shows the update habit in action. The student doesn't rewrite the document from scratch — they bring the current version, list exactly what changed, and ask Claude to produce a clean updated version. The change list is specific and itemised, which means Claude applies each update precisely rather than interpreting vague instructions.

### Example 5: Café owner building a knowledge kit for a content Project

**Scenario:** A café owner in Fitzroy, Melbourne, sets up a Project for writing social media content and decides which three documents to include.

**Prompt:**
```
I run a café in Fitzroy called Little Smoke. I'm setting up a Claude
Project to help me write Instagram captions, email newsletters, and
specials board copy. I want to load three knowledge documents.

Here are the three documents I've drafted. Review each one and tell me:
(1) Is the format well-suited for Claude to use — or should I restructure
    it? (2) Is there anything I should remove because it's too operational
    or too sensitive to store here?

--- DOCUMENT 1: ABOUT LITTLE SMOKE ---

Little Smoke is an all-day café in Fitzroy, Melbourne. We opened in 2019.
Owner: Danny Ferraro. We seat 45 inside and 20 outside. We serve specialty
coffee (single-origin, rotating quarterly from two suppliers), brunch until
2pm, and a lighter afternoon menu. We close at 4pm daily. We don't do
dinner service. We're known for our seasonal menu changes and our
relationships with local producers. Our clientele is locals aged 28–45,
regulars, and design/creative industry workers from nearby studios.

--- DOCUMENT 2: BRAND VOICE ---

Tone: Warm, specific, unhurried. We describe food and coffee the way a
curious person who actually cooked it would — not like a menu, not like
a press release. We mention producers by name when we can. We don't use
café clichés ("artisanal," "curated," "craft").

Things we always do: Name the producer or origin. Describe one sensory
detail. End with something that feels human — a suggestion, an observation,
a small detail that makes you want to come in.

Things we never do: Claim we're "Melbourne's best." Use exclamation marks
in captions. Post generic food photography copy ("treat yourself!").

--- DOCUMENT 3: CURRENT SPECIALS AND PRICES ---

Filter coffee this week: Halo Ethiopia Yirgacheffe, washed, light roast.
$5.50 small / $6.50 large.
Brunch special: Corn fritters with whipped feta, roasted cherry tomatoes,
and chilli oil. $21. Running Tue–Thu this week only.
Afternoon special: Tahini banana bread, $6. Available from noon.
Staff names: Danny (owner), Priya (head barista), Luca, Sasha, Bec.

Write your review of each document.
```
**Why it works:** Document 1 and Document 2 are well-suited for Project knowledge — stable, text-based, relevant to the content task. Document 3 is a classic example of operational data that changes weekly and doesn't belong in a Project. This prompt surfaces that distinction in a realistic way and models the kind of document audit the lesson teaches students to do before submitting their deliverable.

## Graded deliverable

**Title:** Build your knowledge kit

**Brief:** You've learned what knowledge means in the context of a Claude Project, how to sort it into categories, how to prepare documents so Claude can actually use them, and what to leave out. Now you're doing it for real. In this deliverable you'll audit your own documents, select the strongest candidates for your main Project, and prepare one of them properly — then prove it works by using it in a live prompt.

This isn't a planning exercise. By the time you submit, you should have a real document that's sitting in a real Project and has been used to produce a real piece of output. Paste everything into the **Submission box** at the bottom of this lesson on the Claude Mastery platform. The AI grading engine returns your grade card within 60 seconds.

**What to submit:**
1. **Document list** — 4–6 documents you've identified as Project-worthy, each with a one-line note on which category it belongs to (business context / process knowledge / client-project knowledge) and why it earns its place in the Project
2. **Prepared document** — The full text of the best document you reformatted or wrote for Claude (or a 200-word excerpt if the document is long). This should be plain text, structured with headings and short sections — not a wall of prose
3. **Working prompt and response** — A prompt that uses that document to do a real piece of work, plus Claude's full response pasted in. The prompt should reference the document or context explicitly — not just assume Claude knows to use it
4. **Future additions note** — An 80-word note on what you'd add to the knowledge kit next: which specific knowledge gaps would most improve Claude's output for this Project, and what documents you'd write or find to fill them

**Where to submit:** Paste all four items into the **Submission box** at the bottom of this lesson on the Claude Mastery platform. The AI grading engine returns your grade card within 60 seconds.

**Length guide:** Document list: 4–6 items, one line of justification each. Prepared document: full text or 200-word excerpt — no minimum length, but it must be complete enough to use. Working prompt: as long as needed. Claude's response: pasted in full, not summarised. Future additions note: 80 words.

**Pass mark:** 70 / 100. If you score below 70, the platform shows your grade card with feedback per criterion and activates a **Revise & Resubmit** button after a 24-hour cooldown.

**Rubric (100 points total — pass at 70):**
- **Specificity (25 pts)** — Are the documents real business documents — not generic? Does each selection have a clear, business-specific reason for inclusion? A list of six document types that could belong to any small business scores low. A list tied to a named business, with one-line justifications that explain exactly what Claude needs that document for, scores high.
- **Structure (25 pts)** — Is the prepared document clearly structured with headings and short sections? Could Claude locate a specific piece of information in it quickly? A wall of prose with no visual hierarchy scores low. A document with named sections, short lists or steps, and a clear logical order scores high.
- **Constraint clarity (25 pts)** — Does the working prompt tell Claude which document or context to draw on — explicitly, not by assumption? Does the prompt give Claude enough constraint (task, format, tone, length) to produce output without significant manual correction after the fact?
- **Outcome focus (25 pts)** — Does the future additions note identify specific knowledge gaps — not generic ones? "I'd add more detail" scores low. "I'd add a document describing how we handle warranty callbacks, because Claude currently guesses at our process and I have to rewrite that section every time" scores high.

## Notes for the AI grading engine

These notes are read by the grading engine when scoring submissions for this lesson. They are not shown to the student.

- **Reward (high marks):**
  - The document list names real documents from the student's actual business, not generic document types. Justifications explain what specific task or gap each document addresses in the Project.
  - The prepared document uses clear headings, short sections, and plain English. It is written for Claude, not for a customer — it prioritises accuracy and completeness over persuasion.
  - The working prompt explicitly tells Claude to use the knowledge document by name or by reference ("using the business context document above" / "based on the process doc I've provided"). The prompt also includes at least one specific constraint on format or output length.
  - Claude's response is pasted in full and shows visible alignment with the document's content — the output reflects the specific details in the knowledge document rather than generic content Claude would produce without it.
  - The future additions note names a specific knowledge gap tied to a specific task failure — "Claude guesses at X, I correct it every time, I'd write a document about Y to fix this."

- **Penalise (dock points):**
  - The document list is generic ("about us doc, price list, process doc, tone guide") with no business-specific justification for each selection.
  - The prepared document is a wall of prose — no headings, no structure, no visual hierarchy. Or it is a copy-paste of a customer-facing document (brochure, website copy) without Claude-specific rewriting.
  - The working prompt does not reference the knowledge document — it just sends a task and assumes Claude has the context. Or the prompt is so vague (no tone, no format, no length constraint) that the output is generic rather than shaped by the knowledge document.
  - Claude's response is summarised rather than pasted in full.
  - The future additions note is generic: "I'd add more background about my business" or "I'd include more documents about my clients" without naming a specific gap or explaining what task it would improve.
  - Any submission that includes passwords, account numbers, financial credentials, or operational live data in the prepared document. Flag this clearly in feedback and explain why it should be removed.

- **Common 60–69 patterns:** The document list is specific enough — real business documents with reasonable justifications — but the prepared document is under-structured. The student uploaded or pasted an existing document without reformatting it: still written in customer-facing language, dense paragraphs, no headings. The working prompt works, but the output needed correction because Claude couldn't locate the relevant detail in an unstructured document. In feedback, quote one section of the prepared document and show exactly what a heading and two bullet points would do to make that section more usable for Claude.

- **Common 80+ patterns:** The document list is tight — 4–5 documents with specific, task-grounded justifications that explain both why the document is included and what task it enables. The prepared document has visible structure: a one-line summary at the top, named sections, short bullet points or numbered steps. The working prompt references the document explicitly and includes format constraints. Claude's response is clearly shaped by the document — it reflects specific details (names, prices, steps, tone rules) rather than generic content. The future additions note names a specific task where Claude currently has to guess, and identifies the specific document that would eliminate that guessing.

- **Feedback tone:** Direct, specific, and kind. Quote the student's document text when pointing to what's working or what needs restructuring. If the prepared document is unstructured, show a concrete before/after using a sentence from their own submission. If the future additions note is generic, push back with a specific question: "What's the last thing you corrected in Claude's output that a document could have prevented?" End with one concrete next step — either a specific structural fix to the prepared document, or the exact document the student should write next for this Project.

- **Resubmission gating:** Compare the revised submission to the original. If the prepared document gained visible structure (headings, short sections, numbered steps where there were paragraphs before), name that improvement explicitly and quote before and after. If the working prompt now references the document explicitly where it didn't before, acknowledge it. If the future additions note moved from generic to specific — if the student named a real task gap — quote the improvement. If the resubmission adds no structural improvement to the document and only lengthens the prose, flag that directly: length is not structure.

## Industry personalisation hooks

The five worked examples span bookkeeping (business context document for a trades-focused practice), trades (process document for quote follow-ups), marketing consulting (document selection audit), HR consulting (stale document update), and hospitality (knowledge kit review for a café). In personalised lesson variants, these examples should be redrawn for the student's industry — the structural teaching (three categories, format hierarchy, update trigger, what to exclude) is universal. The deliverable prompt "your main Claude Project" can be supplemented with two or three industry-specific document suggestions to help students identify candidates. For professional services: a service scope document, a client communication standards document, and an engagement process overview. For trades: a quoting and follow-up process document, a job types and exclusions list, and a client expectations document. For retail and hospitality: a brand voice document, a product or menu description guide, and a supplier relationships document. The exclusions teaching (credentials, financials, operational live data) applies universally and should not be removed from any personalised variant.
