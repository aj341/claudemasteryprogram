## Lesson script

### From one-off tasks to workflows

Most people start using Claude the same way. A task appears. They open Claude, type a prompt, get an output, and move on. The task is done.

That's useful. But it's not where the real time savings come from.

The bigger gain comes when you stop treating Claude as a task tool and start treating it as part of a workflow. A workflow is a recurring, multi-step process — something you do weekly, fortnightly, or monthly, with mostly the same inputs and mostly the same required outputs. Every business has several of these. The question is whether Claude has a defined role in each one, or whether you're improvising every time.

When Claude has no defined role in a workflow, you repeat the same mental overhead every time: what context does Claude need here? What format should I ask for? What am I actually asking it to do? That friction adds up. It doesn't feel like much per task, but across a month of repeating the same process, it's significant.

When Claude has a defined role, that overhead drops to near zero. You know which steps Claude handles. You know what prompt to use. You know what you get back and what you do with it. The process becomes repeatable, and repeatable processes are the foundation of a business that doesn't depend entirely on you thinking hard every day.

This lesson is about designing those defined roles.

### What a Claude-assisted workflow looks like

A workflow is a sequence of steps with an output at the end. When you design a Claude-assisted workflow, you do three things:

**First: map all the steps.** Write out the full process from start to finish. Don't skip steps because they seem obvious. "Check the brief" is a step. "Notify the client" is a step. Every action is a step.

**Second: decide which steps Claude handles, which you handle, and which are handoffs.** Not every step belongs to Claude. Some require your judgement, your relationships, or information Claude can't have. Some are genuinely faster for you to do yourself. Some steps are transitions — places where Claude produces something and you take it to the next phase, or where you gather information and pass it to Claude.

**Third: write the prompt for every Claude step.** Once you know which steps Claude handles, write the prompt for each one. The prompt should include where this step sits in the workflow — what came before it and what the output will be used for. That context makes Claude's output fit the workflow instead of just answering a question in isolation.

The workflow map doesn't have to be elaborate. A numbered list with "Claude" or "You" or "Handoff" next to each step is enough. The goal is clarity, not complexity.

### Three AU/NZ small business workflow examples

Here are three workflows that work well in this model. Each shows the step map, identifies the Claude steps, and names the handoff points.

**Client onboarding workflow**

This is the process that runs from "client has signed" to "client is set up and the work can begin." Most service businesses have this process. Most run it inconsistently because there's no defined system.

Step 1 — You receive the signed contract. (You)
Step 2 — Claude drafts the welcome email with next steps and appointment details. (Claude)
Step 3 — You review and send the welcome email. (Handoff: Claude → you → client)
Step 4 — Claude generates the onboarding checklist for this client type from the Project knowledge base. (Claude)
Step 5 — You confirm the checklist is correct for this specific client and tick off any items that don't apply. (You)
Step 6 — Claude drafts the internal project brief your team needs to start work. (Claude)
Step 7 — You review the brief, add anything Claude couldn't know, and share it with your team. (Handoff)

In this workflow, Claude handles three of the seven steps: the welcome email, the checklist, and the internal brief. You handle the parts that require judgement about the specific client and relationship. The handoffs are explicit: Claude produces something, you review it before it goes anywhere.

**Monthly reporting workflow**

This is the process of producing a monthly performance summary — for yourself, a business partner, or a client. The data exists somewhere. The job is to interpret it and communicate it clearly.

Step 1 — You pull the numbers from your accounting software, analytics platform, or whatever system tracks the relevant metrics. (You)
Step 2 — You paste the numbers into Claude with a brief note on anything significant that happened this month. (Handoff: you → Claude)
Step 3 — Claude drafts the narrative report section — what the numbers mean, what changed, what the pattern suggests. (Claude)
Step 4 — You read the draft and check whether the interpretation is accurate. This is the critical check. Claude works from the data you gave it; it can't know if the numbers look weird because of an error in your system. (You)
Step 5 — Claude drafts the action items or "next month focus" section based on the narrative. (Claude)
Step 6 — You approve, edit, and send. (Handoff)

The workflow is efficient because Claude handles the writing and the interpretation logic. You handle the data gathering and the accuracy check — the parts that require knowing what the numbers actually mean in your specific business context.

**Content creation workflow**

This is the process of producing regular content — blog posts, social posts, email newsletters — for a consistent publishing schedule. The challenge isn't writing one piece; it's repeating the process without it consuming your week.

Step 1 — You note the topic, the angle, and any timely hook (an industry event, a recent project, a seasonal theme). (You)
Step 2 — Claude drafts the long-form piece from your brief. (Claude)
Step 3 — You read the draft and identify what needs fixing: missing specifics, wrong tone, anything that doesn't reflect your actual view. (You)
Step 4 — Claude revises based on your feedback. (Claude, after handoff)
Step 5 — Claude produces the short-form variations: the social captions, the email subject line, the newsletter intro. (Claude)
Step 6 — You do a final read on everything before scheduling. (You — this is always the last step)

The key handoff point in this workflow is step 3. You have to read the draft before it moves forward. Skipping that check — sending Claude's output straight to step 5 or straight to publishing — is how you end up with content that's factually accurate but sounds nothing like you, or that makes a claim you wouldn't actually make.

### The handoff problem

Handoffs are the most important part of any Claude-assisted workflow, and the part most people underdesign.

A handoff is a point in the workflow where Claude produces something that will affect the real world — an email that will be sent to a client, a report that will go to a business partner, a post that will be published. At every one of these points, you need a review step before the output leaves your hands.

This isn't about distrust. Claude's output quality in a well-designed workflow is high. The issue is that Claude produces text from what you gave it, and what you gave it might be incomplete, slightly wrong, or missing context that you know but didn't write down. Claude doesn't know what it doesn't know. It will produce a confident, well-structured output even when it's working from information that has a gap.

Your review at the handoff point is the place where you close that gap. It doesn't have to be long. A 30-second read of a client email before you hit send is a handoff review. A 2-minute check of a monthly report narrative to confirm the interpretation matches what actually happened is a handoff review.

The rule is simple: Claude's output goes through you before it goes to anyone else. No exceptions, even when the workflow is running smoothly and Claude's outputs have been good for three months.

### Common mistake → Better approach

**Mistake:** Using Claude in a workflow without telling it where the step fits.

You're running your monthly report workflow. At step 3, you paste the numbers and ask: "Write a summary of this month's performance." Claude produces a reasonable paragraph. But it's written like a standalone piece — it doesn't connect to anything before or after. It reads like a fresh interpretation, not like a section of an ongoing document that your business partner has been reading for eight months.

**Better approach:** Include the workflow context in the prompt:

```
I'm preparing our monthly business report for April 2026. This narrative
section follows the metrics table my business partner Marcus has already
seen. It should interpret the numbers — not restate them — and connect
to the "next month focus" section that follows.

Revenue: $41,200 (March: $38,500, up 7.3%)
Appointment fill rate: 87% (target: 85%, up from 83% in March)
Retail product sales: $2,100 (target: $3,500 — second month below target)

Write the narrative section in plain, direct English. 150–200 words.
Marcus reads quickly and wants honest interpretation, not spin. Mention
the retail underperformance and connect it to the staff incentive plan
starting in May.
```

The difference is the framing: "this follows the metrics table" and "connects to the next month focus section" tells Claude what comes before and after. That context changes how it writes the output. It becomes a section of a workflow, not an isolated task.

### Systemising versus automating

Here's a distinction that matters for most people in this course.

Automating a workflow means software does the steps without you. A trigger fires, a tool runs, an output appears — and you weren't involved. Automation requires technical setup: Zapier, Make, API connections, code. For most non-developers running small businesses, automation is not accessible without outside help, and it introduces reliability risks when something breaks.

Systemising a workflow means you have a clear, documented, repeatable process that you run consistently. You're still involved. But the process is defined, Claude's role is clear, the prompts are written, and the steps happen in the same order every time. No special software. No integrations. Just a process that's designed.

Systemising is accessible to anyone, right now, without technical skills. You design the workflow, write the prompts, and run it. It's manual in the sense that you're doing it — but it's not improvised. Every instance of the workflow runs the same way. That consistency is where the time saving and quality improvement come from.

For most small business owners, systemising is the right goal. It's achievable, sustainable, and it doesn't break when a software integration fails.

You might automate parts of a workflow later, with help, once you know the process well. But you can't automate a workflow you haven't systemised first. The system comes before the automation.

Start by systemising two or three workflows that currently run on gut feel and repetition. Document the steps. Assign Claude's role. Write the prompts. Run it the same way three times. Then decide whether anything in that process would benefit from automation.

That's the correct order.

## Worked examples

### Example 1: Client onboarding welcome email — professional services

**Scenario:** A bookkeeping practice in Wellington uses a Project with client context to generate the welcome email when a new client signs on.

**Prompt:**
```
I'm running the client onboarding workflow for Clearwater Bookkeeping.
A new client has just signed. This is step 2 of the onboarding: generating
the welcome email. It will go to the client directly after I review and
approve it.

New client: Helena Mwangi, owner of Mwangi Cleaning Services
Service they've signed up for: Monthly bookkeeping and quarterly BAS
preparation
First appointment: Monday 4 May, 10am via Zoom
What to bring to the first session: Last two years of tax returns, current
bank statements for all business accounts, any outstanding supplier invoices

Our onboarding tone: warm and organised. Helena chose us because she said
her previous bookkeeper was "impossible to reach." Acknowledge we're easy
to contact and that she's in good hands — without making it sound like
marketing copy.

Write the welcome email. Under 200 words. Plain English. Sign off as
Rachel Ng, Senior Bookkeeper.
```
**Why it works:** The prompt names the workflow step ("step 2 of the onboarding") and what happens next ("will go to the client directly after I review and approve it"). That framing tells Claude this is a handoff point, not a draft. It also includes the specific client concern — the previous bookkeeper being unreachable — which allows Claude to address it without making it feel like a generic reassurance.

### Example 2: Monthly report narrative — trades business

**Scenario:** A plumbing business owner in Canberra runs a monthly reporting workflow and uses Claude to write the performance narrative for his accountant.

**Prompt:**
```
I'm running the monthly reporting workflow for Highton Plumbing. This is
step 3: drafting the narrative section. It follows the metrics table my
accountant Priya has already received. It's followed by the "next quarter
priorities" section I'll write myself.

March 2026 numbers:
Revenue: $84,300 (February: $71,200 — March traditionally stronger)
Jobs completed: 47 (breakdown: 31 residential service calls, 9 new builds,
7 commercial)
Average job value: $1,793
Gross margin: 38% (target 40%, down from 41% in February)
Outstanding invoices over 30 days: $12,400 (up from $7,200 in February)

Context Priya doesn't have: The margin dip is partly because we had two
large commercial jobs that required subcontractors. Subcontractor cost
wasn't budgeted correctly — I've updated the estimating template so it
won't repeat. The invoice figure is one slow commercial client — they've
committed to paying by 12 April.

Write the narrative. 150–200 words. Direct, no spin. Priya is experienced
and reads this in 3 minutes — she doesn't need hand-holding. Explain the
margin dip and the invoice movement with the correct context.
```
**Why it works:** The prompt explicitly positions this as one step in a longer workflow — it tells Claude what came before (the metrics table) and what comes after (the next quarter priorities section the owner writes himself). The "context Priya doesn't have" section is the critical addition: it gives Claude the information needed to interpret the numbers accurately, which is exactly the kind of thing that gets missed when you skip the handoff review.

### Example 3: Content creation — first draft of a blog post

**Scenario:** A financial planning firm in Sydney uses a content workflow to produce monthly blog posts for their website.

**Prompt:**
```
I'm running the content creation workflow for Meridian Financial Planning.
This is step 2: generating the first draft of this month's blog post.
After this, I'll review it and give feedback before we move to step 5
(social captions and email newsletter intro).

Topic: The most common mistake people make when calculating how much
they need to retire
Angle: Most people focus on the total number and get paralysed. The more
useful question is: what does a good month cost you now, and how does
that change after you stop working?
Audience: Our blog readers — professionals aged 45–60 in Sydney who are
thinking seriously about retirement but haven't committed to a date.
Tone: Plain English. Confident, not preachy. We don't use jargon like
"drawdown strategy" or "superannuation optimisation" — we explain things
the way a knowledgeable friend would.
Length: 600–700 words. Subheadings every 150–200 words. No bullet points
in the main body — this is editorial writing, not a listicle.

Do not include a call to action at the end. That's added separately.
```
**Why it works:** The prompt names both the current step and the next step in the workflow, so Claude knows this is an intermediate output that will be reviewed and iterated before being used. The tone constraints are specific and include concrete examples of jargon to avoid. The instruction to omit the call to action is a workflow constraint — it belongs to a different step — and stating it prevents Claude from adding something that would either be duplicated or contradict the separately prepared version.

### Example 4: Onboarding checklist — retail store with seasonal staff

**Scenario:** A gift shop in Queenstown uses a Project with an onboarding knowledge document to generate a role-specific checklist each time a new seasonal staff member starts.

**Prompt:**
```
I'm running the staff onboarding workflow for Alpine Gifts Queenstown.
This is step 4: generating the onboarding checklist for a new casual staff
member starting this weekend.

New staff member: Tama Ngata
Role: Casual sales assistant, weekends only
Start date: Saturday 26 April
What's different about this hire: Tama has retail experience but no
prior experience with our POS system (Shopify POS) or our gift-wrapping
service — flag both for priority training.

Use the onboarding knowledge document in this Project to generate Tama's
first-week checklist. Organise it as: Day 1 tasks, Week 1 tasks, and
Sign-off items (things I need to confirm with Tama before end of week 1).

Plain English. Practical, not corporate. Tama doesn't need a welcome speech
— just a clear list of what to learn and when.
```
**Why it works:** The prompt references the Project knowledge base explicitly, which tells Claude to use the stored onboarding document rather than generating a generic checklist. The specific flags for POS training and gift-wrapping mean the output is tailored to this hire, not a copy-paste of a standard list. The three-section structure (Day 1, Week 1, Sign-off) is the workflow format — consistent across every new hire, regardless of their background.

### Example 5: Content workflow — social captions from a reviewed draft

**Scenario:** A landscape architecture firm in Adelaide uses step 5 of its content workflow to generate social captions after the long-form post has been approved.

**Prompt:**
```
I'm running the content workflow for Banksia Landscape Architecture.
The long-form blog post has been reviewed and approved. This is step 5:
generating the social captions and LinkedIn intro.

Here is the approved blog post:

[The approved blog post text goes here — pasted in full]

Generate the following:
1. LinkedIn caption — 120–150 words. Professional but warm. Ends with
   a question to invite comments from other designers or architects.
   Do not copy the subheadings from the blog post — write fresh framing.
2. Instagram caption — 60–80 words. First sentence is a hook — make it
   about the outcome for the client, not about us. One relevant hashtag
   maximum, placed at the end.
3. Email newsletter intro — 60 words maximum. Tells the reader what the
   post is about and why it's worth their 4 minutes. Link text: "Read
   the full post."

Tone across all three: same voice as the approved post — don't drift
toward marketing language in the shorter formats.
```
**Why it works:** This prompt is explicitly step 5 of a documented workflow — it only runs after the long-form post has been reviewed and approved. That sequencing matters: it prevents generating social content from a draft that might still change. The three-output format is consistent every month, which means the results land in the same format the team expects for scheduling. The "don't drift toward marketing language" constraint addresses a common failure mode in short-form content generation.

## Graded deliverable

**Title:** Design a Claude-assisted workflow for your business

**Brief:** You've read how Claude-assisted workflows are structured and seen three different workflow types in action. Now you're going to design one for a real recurring process in your own business — and test one step of it.

This is not a theoretical exercise. The workflow you design should be for a process you actually run, with real steps and a real output. It should be something you do at least twice a month. The deliverable has four parts: naming the workflow, mapping the steps, testing one step with Claude, and giving an honest assessment of whether the workflow would actually save you time or just create a new system to maintain.

Paste everything into the **Submission box** at the bottom of this lesson on the Claude Mastery platform. The AI grading engine returns your grade card within 60 seconds.

**What to submit:**
1. **Workflow name and frequency** — What is the workflow called, and how often does it run? Be specific: not "client communications" but "sending a project completion summary after each job closes." One to two sentences.
2. **Step map** — A numbered list of every step in the workflow, with each step labelled: Claude, You, or Handoff. Handoff steps should note what Claude produces and who receives it next.
3. **One step attempted** — Choose one Claude step from your map. Write the prompt you'd use for that step — include the workflow context (where in the process this sits, what came before, what the output will be used for). Paste Claude's response in full.
4. **100-word assessment** — Does this workflow actually save you time, or does it create complexity that costs more than it saves? Be honest. Include a rough time estimate: how long does the workflow currently take you, and how long would it take with Claude? Where might the workflow break down?

**Where to submit:** Paste everything into the **Submission box** at the bottom of this lesson on the Claude Mastery platform. The AI grading engine returns your grade card within 60 seconds.

**Length guide:** Workflow name and frequency: 1–2 sentences. Step map: as many steps as the process actually has — no minimum or maximum, but don't skip steps to make the map look tidy. Attempted step prompt: full prompt text plus Claude's complete response. Assessment: 100 words, no more.

**Pass mark:** 70 / 100. If you score below 70, the platform shows your grade card with feedback per criterion and activates a **Revise & Resubmit** button after a 24-hour cooldown.

**Rubric (100 points total — pass at 70):**

- **Specificity (25 pts)** — Is the workflow named with enough precision to know exactly what it covers and when it runs? "Sending a project completion summary to clients after each job closes" is specific. "Client communications" is not. Does the step map reflect the real steps in the real process — not an idealised version — including the human steps and the handoff points?

- **Structure (25 pts)** — Does the step map clearly distinguish Claude steps from human steps, with explicit handoff points labelled and described? Can a reader follow the map and understand what happens at each step, who is responsible, and what moves the process forward? A map with unlabelled steps or no handoffs scores low.

- **Constraint clarity (25 pts)** — Does the attempted step's prompt include the workflow context Claude needs: where in the process the step sits, what came before it, and what the output will be used for next? A prompt that reads like a standalone task — with no reference to the workflow — scores low, even if the output is good.

- **Outcome focus (25 pts)** — Does the 100-word assessment include a specific time estimate — current versus Claude-assisted — and an honest identification of where the workflow might fail? An assessment that is entirely positive ("this would save so much time") without naming a single failure risk scores low. An assessment that includes a genuine tension — "it saves 40 minutes but I'd need to be disciplined about the review step or it would create more work, not less" — scores high.

## Industry personalisation hooks

The three workflow types in this lesson — client onboarding, monthly reporting, and content creation — apply across most AU/NZ small business industries. In personalised lesson variants, the examples within each workflow type should be redrawn to match the student's industry. For trades businesses, strong workflow candidates include the quote-to-job-start sequence, the job completion and invoice workflow, and the end-of-month subcontractor reconciliation. For retail and hospitality, high-value workflows include the new product or menu item launch process, the weekly roster and briefing workflow, and the seasonal promotion planning cycle. For professional services — accounting, legal, consulting — client onboarding and engagement close-out workflows are the most commonly under-systemised and the highest-value targets. The systemising versus automating distinction in this lesson is especially important for students who have encountered Zapier or Make and assumed that automation is the goal — personalisation can reinforce that building a clear manual system first is both more achievable and more durable than jumping to automation.
