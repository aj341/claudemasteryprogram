## Lesson script

### This is the real work

You have completed thirty lessons. You have built a prompt engineering practice, a Projects infrastructure, and a set of extended capabilities. Every lesson up to this point gave you a bounded exercise — a specific skill to practise, a specific output to produce. The curriculum decided what you worked on.

Module 6 is different. You decide what you work on.

The capstone is a four-part project, spread across Lessons 6.1 through 6.4. You pick one real business challenge and build a complete Claude-assisted solution for it. Not a lesson exercise — an actual piece of work that matters to your business. This lesson, 6.1, is the design phase. You map the problem, define the solution approach, name the tools you will use, and plan the implementation before touching Claude.

That sequence matters. Design before build. Problem before solution. Criteria before output. The lessons up to here have been about developing the skill of working well with Claude. Module 6 is about demonstrating it on something real.

The design document you produce in this lesson is the brief for everything that follows. Lesson 6.2 is the implementation — you build what you planned. Lesson 6.3 is evaluation — you measure what you built against the criteria you set today. Lesson 6.4 is the presentation — you explain what you built, what it produced, and where you would take it next. The choice you make in 6.1 carries through all four lessons. Choose something worth the time.

### Choosing the right challenge

Most students arrive at Lesson 6.1 with one of two problems. The first is choosing something too vague: "I want to improve my client communications" or "I want to use Claude more in my business." These are directions, not challenges. You cannot build a solution for a direction. You cannot measure whether it worked.

The second is choosing something too large: "I want to automate my entire onboarding process" or "I want Claude to handle my social media." These are projects that would take months with a team. A capstone that cannot be completed in the time you have is a capstone that will fail.

The right challenge sits in a specific middle ground. It is concrete enough to describe in two or three sentences. It is significant enough that solving it produces a real result for your business — not a nice-to-have, but a genuine improvement in time, quality, or consistency. And it is achievable with the Foundation tier toolkit: prompts, Projects, and extended capabilities. No APIs, no external automations, no developer dependencies.

Here is the test to apply to any challenge you are considering. Answer all three:

**Is it specific?** Can you name exactly what is broken or slow or inconsistent? Not "my quoting process is inefficient" but "I spend 45 minutes per quote manually pulling specifications from supplier PDFs, calculating margins, and formatting the output — and I send 6–8 quotes a week." The second version names the task, the time cost, the frequency, and the friction point. That is specific.

**Is it significant?** Would solving this make a real difference — in time saved, quality improved, or consistency increased? A challenge that saves five minutes once a month is not worth a four-lesson capstone. A challenge that saves two hours a week, or removes a task you have been avoiding, or produces consistently better outputs for clients — that is worth the work.

**Is it achievable with your current toolkit?** The Foundation tier is prompts, Projects, file processing, and vision. If your solution requires the Claude API, third-party automation, or code, it is outside scope for this capstone. That does not mean those things are impossible — it means they belong in a future project, after this course. Keep the scope inside what you have built.

### Defining your objectives and success criteria

Before you decide how to build anything, define what success looks like. This sounds obvious. Almost no one does it before they start.

Success criteria serve two functions in this capstone. First, they constrain your solution design — if you know you need to reduce quote time from 45 minutes to under 15, you design a solution aimed at that specific target rather than building something vague and hoping it helps. Second, they give you a clear answer in Lesson 6.3 when you evaluate whether the solution worked. Without criteria, evaluation is just an opinion.

A useful success criterion has three properties. It is measurable — you can count it or time it or compare it. It is attached to the challenge — it measures the specific thing you are trying to change, not a proxy. And it is realistic — it is achievable with the solution you are planning, not a wish for perfection.

Good success criteria for a capstone:
- Quote drafting time reduced from 45 minutes to under 15 minutes
- Client summary emails score 4 out of 5 or higher in a self-review against the checklist I defined
- All weekly status reports produced with a consistent structure — zero missing sections — over four weeks

Poor success criteria:
- Clients are more satisfied (not measurable without a survey or system)
- Claude produces better outputs (compared to what, measured how?)
- I use Claude more (frequency is not an outcome)

Name at least two measurable success criteria before you start designing your solution. They become the standard your Lesson 6.3 evaluation will be held to.

### Mapping your Foundation capabilities

The Foundation tier gives you a specific set of tools. The capability map in your design document is where you identify which tools your solution will use — and why those tools fit this problem.

The minimum requirement is three capabilities across different modules. Using three prompt templates from Module 2 counts as one capability, not three. The map needs breadth — at least one capability from Module 2, one from Module 4, and one from Module 5.

More importantly, the capability map should explain the logic. Not "I will use a Project from Module 4" but "I will use a Project from Module 4 to hold the supplier specifications and pricing data that Claude needs to reference in every quote session — without it, I would have to re-paste this context each time." The capability is justified by the problem, not just listed.

Here is an example capability map entry written well versus written poorly:

```
POOR ENTRY:
Capability: File uploads (Module 5)
Use: Upload supplier documents

GOOD ENTRY:
Capability: File uploads — Module 5, Lesson 5.2
Use: Upload supplier specification PDFs at the start of each quote session.
Claude reads the document and extracts the specifications I need (model,
dimensions, weight rating, lead time) before I write the prompt for the
quote. This replaces the manual reading and note-taking step that
currently takes 15–20 minutes per quote.
```

The good entry names the specific document type, the specific output Claude extracts, and the specific step it replaces. That is a capability map entry that tells you exactly how implementation should work when you get to Lesson 6.2.

Work through each capability you plan to use and ask: why does this capability fit this specific problem, and what specific task in my workflow does it replace or improve?

### Building your implementation plan

The implementation plan is the last component of your design document. It is not a project management plan or a business case. It is a practical, step-by-step description of what you will actually do in Lesson 6.2 to build the solution.

Three to five steps is the right length. Each step should name one concrete action, describe what gets done in that action, and indicate roughly how long it should take. If you cannot describe what gets done in a step, the step is not defined well enough.

A common mistake at this stage is confusing planning with building. The implementation plan describes what you will build in 6.2 — it does not require you to start building it now. Write the steps in enough detail that a stranger could follow them and understand what the end state looks like.

**Common mistake → Better approach**

Mistake: Writing a high-level plan that sounds complete but is vague: "Step 1: Set up a Claude Project. Step 2: Build prompts. Step 3: Test the system." This plan describes categories of work, not actual work. You cannot follow it in Lesson 6.2 without making it up as you go.

```
VAGUE PLAN (do not do this):
Step 1: Create a Project for quoting
Step 2: Add prompts
Step 3: Test with a real quote
Step 4: Refine

SPECIFIC PLAN (aim for this):
Step 1: Create a Quoting Project in Claude. Upload the two supplier
  specification PDFs I use most often. Write the Project instructions
  to define Claude's role as a quoting assistant who knows my margin
  targets (28%) and standard terms. (~15 mins)

Step 2: Write the extraction prompt template — the prompt I will use
  to pull specifications from a newly uploaded supplier PDF at the
  start of each quoting session. (~20 mins)

Step 3: Write the quote drafting prompt template — the prompt that
  takes the extracted specs plus client requirements and produces
  a formatted quote draft in my standard layout. (~20 mins)

Step 4: Run the full workflow on a real quote from this week.
  Document what works and what needs adjustment. (~30 mins)

Step 5: Revise both prompts based on the test. Finalise the workflow
  description I will include in my 6.3 evaluation. (~15 mins)
```

Better approach: Write each step as a named action, not a category. Name the specific prompt, document, or Project element you are building in that step. Include a rough time estimate so you can plan the 6.2 lesson realistically. When you read the plan, you should be able to start following it immediately.

### What you are building toward

The design document you produce in this lesson is not an academic exercise. It is a working brief. In Lesson 6.2, you open that document and follow it. In Lesson 6.3, you return to the success criteria you wrote today and measure against them honestly. In Lesson 6.4, you present the result — what you built, what it produced, and what you learned.

That four-part structure is the closest this course gets to real project work, because it is real project work. Not a simulation, not a case study — an actual solution you designed and built for your actual business, using a toolkit you spent five modules developing.

The best capstone projects in this course share one characteristic: specificity from the start. The students who produce the strongest 6.3 evaluations are the ones who wrote the sharpest design documents in 6.1. The challenge is named precisely. The success criteria are measurable. The capability map explains why, not just what. The implementation plan is specific enough to follow.

That precision is available to you right now, before you open Claude. It does not require advanced skills — it requires clarity about what you are actually trying to do. Take the time in this lesson to get that clarity. The rest of the module depends on it.

## Worked examples

### Example 1: Quoting workflow — trades business

**Scenario:** A solar installation company wants to reduce the time spent drafting customer quotes from supplier specification sheets.

**Prompt:**
```
I run a solar installation business in Brisbane with three installers.
I want to design a Claude-based solution for my quoting process before
I start building anything.

Here is my current situation:
- I receive 8–12 quote requests per week
- Each quote requires me to check specifications across 2–3 supplier
  PDFs (panel brand, inverter brand, battery brand)
- I spend 40–50 minutes per quote pulling specs, calculating costs at my
  standard margins, and writing the quote document in my template
- The output needs to look professional — clients compare quotes

Help me design a solution. I want you to:
1. Confirm whether this challenge is specific and achievable with
   Claude's Foundation tier tools (no API, no automation)
2. Suggest which specific capabilities I should use and why each one
   fits this problem
3. Propose a 4-step implementation plan in enough detail that I can
   follow it in a single working session next week

My margin target is 28% across all products. My quote template has five
sections: client details, system overview, product specifications, pricing
breakdown, and warranty terms.
```

**Why it works:** The student provides the specific numbers (8–12 quotes, 40–50 minutes, 28% margin) before asking for design input, which gives Claude real constraints to work with rather than abstract problems. The three-part output structure ensures Claude produces something usable for the design document: a feasibility check, a capability map, and a step-by-step plan. This is Claude being used in the design phase to sharpen the design — not to start building.

### Example 2: Client onboarding documents — professional services

**Scenario:** A financial planning practice wants to standardise the client summary documents produced after first appointments.

**Prompt:**
```
I run a financial planning practice in Melbourne with two planners.
After every first appointment, we write a client summary that covers
the client's financial situation, their goals, and our initial
recommendations for the next steps. These summaries vary significantly
in quality and length depending on who writes them. The inconsistency
creates problems when clients work with both planners.

I want to design a Claude-based solution that produces consistent,
high-quality client summaries based on a structured set of notes from
the appointment.

Here is what I am bringing to the design phase:
- My current summary template (I will paste it below)
- A sample of appointment notes from a recent client (anonymised)
- The two main complaints we have received about summaries: they are
  sometimes too long, and the recommendations section lacks specificity

Design challenge: I want summaries to always be under 600 words, always
follow the template structure, and always include at least three specific
next steps in the recommendations section.

[Template pasted below]
[Sample notes pasted below]

Before I start building, give me a capability map and a 4-step
implementation plan.
```

**Why it works:** The student has defined the problem in measurable terms before asking for design input: under 600 words, always follows template structure, minimum three specific next steps. The two client complaints are named directly — this gives Claude concrete quality problems to design around, not vague quality goals. Pasting both the template and sample notes means Claude can produce a capability map and implementation plan tailored to real inputs.

### Example 3: Weekly reporting — retail business

**Scenario:** A gift store owner wants to reduce the time spent producing a weekly sales and stock summary for her two part-time staff.

**Prompt:**
```
I own a gift and homewares store in Adelaide. I have two part-time staff
and I send them a weekly update every Monday morning covering sales
performance from the previous week, what stock needs reordering, and
focus tasks for the week ahead.

Currently I spend 45–60 minutes on this update because I compile it
manually from three sources: my POS sales report (exported as PDF),
my stock tracking spreadsheet (exported as CSV), and my own notes
from the week.

I want a Claude solution that reduces this to under 20 minutes.

Here is the design challenge: I am not technical. I cannot build
automations or connect software. I need a solution that works by
uploading the three files to Claude each Monday and producing the
update from them in a consistent format.

Before I build anything, help me think through:
1. Is this achievable with Claude's file processing capabilities
   without any technical setup?
2. What is the correct prompt structure — do I write one prompt that
   processes all three files, or a sequence of prompts?
3. Give me a 3-step implementation plan that accounts for the fact
   that my stock spreadsheet has 400+ rows and my POS report is
   4–6 pages.
```

**Why it works:** The student has pre-empted the most important constraint — no technical setup, just file uploads — and built it into the brief. The three-part question structure is well-calibrated: feasibility check first, then solution approach, then plan. Mentioning the file sizes (400+ rows, 4–6 pages) is a strong design-phase move — it tells Claude to account for document length in the prompt structure recommendation, which affects how the solution should work.

### Example 4: Job description writing — recruitment agency

**Scenario:** A small recruitment agency wants to produce consistent, well-structured job descriptions faster.

**Prompt:**
```
I run a boutique recruitment agency in Sydney specialising in
marketing and creative roles. My two consultants spend 30–40 minutes
writing each job description from scratch, even for similar roles
we place regularly. The quality varies — some JDs are excellent,
some are vague and produce poor applicant quality.

I want to design a Claude-based solution that:
- Produces consistent JDs in under 15 minutes per role
- Uses a standard structure we agree on (I will define it)
- Captures the tone of each client's brand rather than sounding generic

This is for my 6.1 capstone design. I am not trying to build anything
yet — I am designing it first.

Help me think through the capability map. I have access to Claude
Projects, prompt templates, and file uploads. I do not have API access.

Specifically:
- Should this live in a Project, or is it a standalone prompt template?
- If a Project, what goes in the knowledge base?
- What would the final prompt template look like structurally?
  (Give me the template structure, not filled-in content)
```

**Why it works:** Stating "I am not trying to build anything yet — I am designing it first" keeps the conversation in design mode. The capability question is well-framed: not "how do I do this" but a choice between two specific approaches (Project vs standalone template) with a follow-on question about knowledge base content. Asking for the template structure rather than a filled-in template is the right design-phase move — it produces a planning artefact without pre-empting the implementation choices in 6.2.

### Example 5: Design document — completed example

**Scenario:** A graphic designer has completed the design phase and submits her full design document as a reference.

**Prompt:**
```
I am completing Lesson 6.1 and I want you to review my draft design
document before I submit it. Tell me honestly: is my challenge specific
enough? Are my success criteria measurable? Does my capability map
explain why each capability fits my problem — or just list them?
Do not rewrite any section. Tell me which parts pass and which parts
need to be sharpened before I submit.

Here is my draft:

CHALLENGE STATEMENT:
I run a two-person graphic design studio in Canberra. Our biggest
time drain is writing project proposals — we produce 10–15 proposals
per month, and each one takes 90 minutes to write because we
customise every section from scratch even when the project type is
similar. We want to reduce proposal writing time to under 30 minutes
per proposal without reducing the quality or personalisation that
our clients expect.

SOLUTION OVERVIEW:
I will build a proposal-writing system in Claude using a dedicated
Proposals Project. The Project will hold our proposal template,
a bank of our best past proposal sections, and our brand voice
guide. For each new proposal, I will paste the client brief and
run a structured prompting sequence that produces a first draft
in our voice, following our template, in under 20 minutes. I
will then spend 10 minutes reviewing and personalising before sending.

CAPABILITY MAP:
1. Claude Projects (Module 4, Lesson 4.2) — holds the template,
   past sections, and brand voice guide so Claude has context
   for every session without re-pasting
2. Prompt templates (Module 2, Lesson 2.3) — a saved prompt
   sequence I use for every proposal, with variable fields for
   the client name, project type, and timeline
3. File uploads (Module 5, Lesson 5.2) — for when the client
   has sent a detailed brief as a PDF; Claude reads it and extracts
   the requirements before I run the drafting prompt

IMPLEMENTATION PLAN:
Step 1: Create the Proposals Project. Upload our current proposal
  template and our three highest-rated past proposals as reference
  examples. Write Project instructions defining Claude's role as a
  proposal-writing assistant who matches our studio tone. (~20 mins)
Step 2: Write the main proposal drafting prompt. It should take
  four inputs: client name, project type, key deliverables, timeline.
  Output: a complete proposal draft following our template structure.
  (~25 mins)
Step 3: Test the prompt on two real client briefs from last month.
  Compare outputs against the actual proposals we sent. (~30 mins)
Step 4: Revise the prompt and Project instructions based on gaps
  found in testing. Document the final workflow. (~20 mins)

SUCCESS CRITERIA:
- Proposal drafting time reduced from 90 minutes to under 30 minutes,
  measured across 5 real proposals in the first two weeks of use
- Proposal structure consistency: every proposal produced includes
  all 6 sections from our template — measured by checklist review
  before sending
```

**Why it works:** Using Claude to review the design document before submitting is legitimate design-phase work — it catches gaps without asking Claude to do the thinking for you. The instruction "do not rewrite any section — tell me which parts pass and which need sharpening" keeps control with the student and produces targeted feedback rather than a replacement document. The design document itself demonstrates what a strong submission looks like: a specific, quantified challenge; a solution overview that describes the mechanism; a capability map with module references and use-case logic; an implementation plan with named artefacts and time estimates; and success criteria with measurement methods.

## Graded deliverable

**Title:** Capstone Design Document

**Brief:** This is the foundation for the next three lessons. Everything you build in Lesson 6.2, measure in Lesson 6.3, and present in Lesson 6.4 will be based on the design document you submit here. The more precise and complete this document is, the more effectively you can execute the implementation phase — and the more clearly you can evaluate whether it worked.

The AI grading engine assesses this submission across four criteria: specificity, structure, constraint clarity, and outcome focus. A submission that passes on all four criteria gives you a clear, executable brief for the rest of Module 6. A submission that falls short on one or more criteria will show you the gaps before you start building — which is a better time to find them than after. Paste your complete design document into the **Submission box** at the bottom of this lesson on the Claude Mastery platform. The AI grading engine returns your grade card within 60 seconds.

**What to submit:**

1. **Business challenge statement** — 2–3 sentences. Name the problem, why it matters, and how you know it is real. Include specific numbers where possible: time cost, frequency, volume. Avoid abstract descriptions like "inefficient processes" — name the specific process, the specific friction, and the specific cost to your business.

2. **Solution overview** — 3–4 sentences. Describe the Claude-assisted solution you will build and how it works. Not what it will achieve — how it functions. Name the type of solution (a Project, a prompt workflow, a file processing sequence, or a combination), the inputs it requires, and what it produces.

3. **Capability map** — List each Foundation tier capability you will use. For each capability: name it, name which module it came from, and explain in one to two sentences why it fits this specific problem — what specific task does it handle, what does it replace or improve? Minimum three capabilities across at least two different modules.

4. **Implementation plan** — 3–5 steps in plain language. Each step names one concrete action, describes what gets built or produced in that step, and includes a rough time estimate. The plan should be specific enough to follow in a single working session.

5. **Success criteria** — At least two measurable signals that will tell you the solution worked. Each criterion should be countable or timeable — not "better quality" but "output reviewed against checklist with zero missing sections" or "time from client brief to first draft reduced from X to Y."

**Where to submit:** Paste everything into the **Submission box** at the bottom of this lesson on the Claude Mastery platform. The AI grading engine returns your grade card within 60 seconds.

**Length guide:** Challenge statement: 2–3 sentences. Solution overview: 3–4 sentences. Capability map: one to two sentences per capability, minimum three entries. Implementation plan: 3–5 steps, 1–3 sentences each with time estimate. Success criteria: 2–4 bullet points, each measurable.

**Pass mark:** 70 / 100. If you score below 70, the platform shows your grade card with feedback per criterion and activates a **Revise & Resubmit** button after a 24-hour cooldown.

**Rubric (100 points total — pass at 70):**

- **Specificity (25 pts)** — Is the challenge real and named with enough precision that the grader can picture the actual business problem? Does the challenge statement include specific numbers — time cost, frequency, volume — rather than vague descriptions? Are the success criteria measurable by an agreed method, not just improvements in a general direction? A challenge statement that says "improve my quoting process" with no specifics scores in the 6–10 range. A statement that says "reduce quote drafting time from 45 minutes to under 15 minutes across my 8–10 weekly quotes, currently delayed by manual specification extraction from supplier PDFs" scores 20+.

- **Structure (25 pts)** — Does the submission include all five components, clearly labelled and in the correct order? Is each component substantively completed — not a single sentence standing in for a full section? The capability map must include at least three entries with module references. The implementation plan must include at least three steps with concrete descriptions. Missing components or significantly underdeveloped sections lose marks proportionally. A submission with four strong sections and one missing section scores no higher than 18/25 on this criterion.

- **Constraint clarity (25 pts)** — Does the capability map explain why each capability fits this problem — not just list capabilities that exist? Does each entry name the specific task the capability handles and what it replaces or improves? Is the implementation plan specific enough to follow — named artefacts, named prompt types, time estimates — or is it a list of categories? A capability map that says "I will use a Project (Module 4)" with no explanation of what goes in the Project or why it fits scores in the 12–16 range. A map entry that explains the specific knowledge base content and the specific problem it solves scores 20+.

- **Outcome focus (25 pts)** — Does the design document show that the student understands why each capability fits their specific problem — not just that they know the capabilities exist? Does the solution overview describe a mechanism, not a wish? Do the success criteria connect directly to the challenge — measuring the specific thing that is broken, not a proxy? A solution overview that says "I will use Claude to help me work faster" scores in the 6–10 range. An overview that describes the workflow mechanism, the inputs, and the output type scores 20+.

## Notes for the AI grading engine

These notes are read by the grading engine when scoring submissions for this lesson. They are not shown to the student.

- **Reward (high marks):**
  - Challenge statement includes specific numbers — time cost, frequency, volume — that make the problem concrete and verifiable
  - Capability map entries each contain a "why this capability" explanation that connects directly to the problem described in the challenge statement — not a general description of what the capability does
  - Implementation plan steps name specific artefacts being built (a named Project, a named prompt template, specific document types for upload) rather than describing categories of work
  - Success criteria are attached to the challenge — they measure the specific thing the challenge names, with a measurement method described, not just a direction of improvement
  - Solution overview describes a mechanism — inputs, process, output — rather than an aspiration or a general intention to use Claude

- **Penalise (dock points):**
  - Challenge statement uses vague language ("inefficient," "inconsistent," "better quality") without naming specific tasks, frequencies, or time costs
  - Capability map lists capabilities without explaining why they fit this problem — entries that describe what a capability is rather than what specific role it plays in the solution
  - Implementation plan steps are categories, not actions — "build prompts" rather than "write a drafting prompt that takes these four inputs and produces this output format"
  - Success criteria are not measurable — "clients will be happier" or "outputs will be more consistent" with no measurement method
  - Solution overview is aspirational rather than mechanical — it describes what the student wants to achieve without describing how the solution will actually work

- **Common 60–69 patterns (near-pass):** The student has done the thinking — the challenge is real, the solution is plausible, the components are present. But the specificity is thin. The challenge statement describes the problem area without naming the specific task, time cost, or frequency. The capability map lists three capabilities with module references but the "why" explanations are generic — they describe what the capability does in general rather than what specific role it plays in this solution. The implementation plan has four steps but they read as categories ("set up Project," "write prompts," "test," "refine") rather than actions. The success criteria state a direction ("reduce time," "improve consistency") without a measurement method. In feedback, quote the student's challenge statement and ask: how long does this task currently take you, and how often do you do it? Then quote one capability map entry and ask: what specific content will go in this Project that is specific to your challenge — not generic knowledge about Claude? Those two questions are the concrete next steps toward resubmission.

- **Common 80+ patterns (excellent):** The challenge statement reads like a project brief — specific task, named time cost, named frequency, named friction point. The capability map demonstrates systems thinking: each capability is justified by its role in the workflow, and together the capabilities cover the full solution without gaps or overlap. The implementation plan reads as a session plan — a stranger could follow it and understand what the end state of each step looks like. Success criteria are paired with measurement methods: not just "time reduced" but "time reduced from X to Y, measured across five real runs in the first two weeks of use." The solution overview describes a mechanism the student clearly understands — inputs, process, output — without over-promising. Acknowledge the precision of the design document specifically. Name the strongest component and explain what makes it strong. End with one concrete observation about what the implementation phase (6.2) will require based on this design — a specific prompt type, a specific knowledge base decision, or a specific testing approach — that the student can carry directly into that lesson.

- **Feedback tone:** Direct, specific, and kind. Quote the student's own words when pointing out wins or gaps — do not speak in generalities about what the submission should have done. This is the start of the capstone, which is a genuine commitment of time and effort, and that deserves acknowledgment — but acknowledge the specific ambition in their design, not the fact that they reached Module 6. Always end with one concrete next step pointing toward 6.2: a named action they can take in the implementation phase based on what they have designed here.

- **Resubmission gating:** If the student resubmits, compare against their previous submission. Reward visible improvement in the criteria they previously failed. If the challenge statement gained specific numbers that were missing, name that improvement. If the capability map entries gained "why this capability" explanations, name that improvement. If the implementation plan moved from categories to named actions, name that improvement. If the resubmission is essentially unchanged with added words but no added specificity, say so directly and name the exact sentence that still needs to become concrete before it passes.

## Industry personalisation hooks

The five-component design document structure is sector-neutral, but the most common challenge types and capability maps vary significantly by industry vertical. For trades and construction businesses, the most commonly chosen challenges involve quoting and job documentation — specification extraction from supplier PDFs (file uploads, Module 5) combined with a quoting Project (Module 4) and a prompt template sequence (Module 2). For agency and creative services, proposal and brief writing is the dominant challenge type, with Projects holding brand voice guides and past work samples as the knowledge base. For allied health and professional services, client summary and appointment documentation is the most frequent challenge, where structured note-taking prompts (Module 2) feed into a client-comms Project (Module 4) that enforces consistent format and length. For retail and hospitality, stock and performance reporting using file uploads (Module 5) from POS exports is a common, achievable challenge. For finance and property, the challenge often centres on client-facing document consistency — where the capability map almost always includes file uploads for reference documents, a Project for holding compliance or style constraints, and prompt templates for the drafting sequence. When personalising this lesson for industry cohorts, swap the worked examples (particularly Examples 1 and 3) to reflect the dominant business model in the cohort, and adjust the specificity criterion feedback language to reference the typical time costs and task frequencies for that sector.
