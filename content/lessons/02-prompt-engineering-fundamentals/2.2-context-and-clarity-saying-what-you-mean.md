# Lesson 2.2 — Context and clarity: saying what you mean

**Module:** 02 · Prompt Engineering Fundamentals
**Estimated time:** 30 minutes
**Format:** Read + Practice + Graded deliverable

## Pre-requisites
- Lessons 1.1–1.6 — Claude Essentials (all six)
- Lesson 2.1 — How Claude reads a prompt

## Learning objectives
By the end of this lesson you'll be able to:
- Explain why context is the single biggest variable in whether a prompt produces useful output
- Identify and supply all three types of context: who you are, what the situation is, who the output is for
- Rewrite a vague prompt into a specific one that produces a usable first response
- Include at least one clear "don't" or exclusion in every prompt that has a strong style or format requirement
- Apply the minimum viable context principle to keep prompts efficient without stripping them of what matters

## Lesson script

### Why Claude can't read the room

When you sit down with a capable colleague and explain a task, you're doing something you don't notice: you're filling in context. You mention who the client is. You reference the email they sent last week. You say "keep it light — this person doesn't like formal language." All of that happens in a few seconds, without effort.

Claude doesn't get that. It has no memory of your business, your clients, your tone, or your history unless you write it down. Every prompt starts with a blank sheet. Claude is highly capable at following instructions, drawing on information, and producing structured output — but it can only work with what you give it. Nothing more.

This is the most important thing to understand about how prompts fail. The output isn't usually wrong because Claude made a mistake. It's usually generic because you didn't give Claude enough to work with. The fix isn't a smarter AI. It's more context from you.

Context is the single biggest lever you have on prompt quality. It outranks clever phrasing, fancy structures, and any prompt "hack" you've seen online. A well-contextualised prompt in plain English will beat a framework-compliant prompt with no context, every single time.

This lesson is about how to provide context efficiently — not by writing paragraphs of background for every task, but by knowing exactly which details matter and including those.

### The three types of context

Context in a prompt falls into three categories. Think of them as three lenses, each one focusing Claude's response differently.

**1. Who you are**

This is your role, your business, and the relevant parts of your professional situation. It tells Claude which world you operate in.

A generic prompt says: "Write a quote follow-up email."

A prompt with who-you-are context says: "I run a 6-person bookkeeping firm in Auckland. Most of our clients are in the trades or hospitality sectors. We send quotes after discovery calls, and our typical engagement is $400–$800 per month."

Claude now knows the industry, the business size, the client base, and the price bracket. Every word in the response can be calibrated to that reality instead of written for a generic business in a generic context.

You don't need to write your full CV. Two or three sentences that situate you clearly is enough for most tasks. The goal is for Claude to understand whose problem it's solving.

**2. What the situation is**

This is the specific task at hand — not just the task type, but the circumstance behind it.

"Write a difficult email" tells Claude the task type. It doesn't tell Claude what makes this one difficult, who it's going to, what the dynamic is, or what outcome you're trying to produce.

"I need to email a long-term client — we've worked with them for three years — to let them know we're increasing our monthly fee by 20%, effective next quarter. We've absorbed rising software costs for two years and can't hold the line. The increase is fair and I don't want to lose this client. They're a good payer and easy to work with." That's the situation. Now Claude can actually help.

The situation context is the part people most often skip. They name the task ("write a proposal," "summarise this document," "draft a response") but don't say anything about the circumstances that make this specific instance worth thinking about. Those circumstances are where all the nuance lives.

**3. Who the output is for**

This is the audience — who will read it, what they already know, what they care about, and what tone will land with them.

The same information written for a 60-year-old tradesperson who's never touched accounting software should read completely differently from the same information written for a CFO who processes reports daily. Claude can calibrate for either — but only if you say who's on the receiving end.

Audience context doesn't have to be long. "For a client who's not comfortable with financial terminology" or "for my business partner who has a finance background and wants the short version" — one sentence is often enough to shift the entire register of the response.

When you're drafting anything that's going to another person, always include at least a one-line audience note. It is one of the highest-value lines you can add.

### Vague versus specific: what actually changes

Here is a direct comparison. Same task, different amount of context.

**Vague:**

```
Write a proposal for a new client.
```

Claude will produce something generic. It may have good bones — an intro, a scope section, pricing, a close. But it won't sound like you. It won't address this client's specific situation. It will be usable as a template, not as a deliverable.

**Specific:**

```
I run a graphic design studio in Perth with two designers. We've just had
a discovery call with a cafe chain that wants brand collateral across three
locations — menus, signage, and a social media template pack. They mentioned
they're "design-literate" but cost-conscious, and the owner asked twice about
timelines.

Write a proposal introduction and scope section. The tone should be confident
but not salesy — I want them to feel like they're hiring a specialist, not
being pitched to. Keep the scope section to dot points. Don't include pricing
in this section — that comes later in the document.
```

That prompt produces a usable first draft. Every element of it — the studio size, the client type, the client's stated concerns, the tone instruction, the format instruction, the explicit scope exclusion (no pricing) — narrows Claude's response from the possible range to the useful range.

The output from the vague prompt takes 15 minutes of editing to make useful. The output from the specific prompt might need one pass.

### Saying what you don't want

Most people write prompts that describe what they want. Fewer write prompts that describe what they don't want. Both matter.

"Write a proposal introduction" tells Claude what to produce. It doesn't tell Claude what to avoid. So Claude might write an opening that starts with "We are delighted to present..." which you hate. Or it might use industry jargon that your client wouldn't understand. Or it might include a company history paragraph that you never put in proposals.

Adding one or two negative instructions — things to leave out, tones to avoid, formats to skip — often does more work than adding an extra paragraph of positive context.

Good exclusions are specific. "Don't include a company history section." "Avoid any phrase like 'we are pleased to offer' or 'we look forward to working with you' — I hate that language." "No bullet points — this should read as prose." "Don't make assumptions about the client's budget."

A vague exclusion ("don't make it too formal") is better than no exclusion at all, but it leaves Claude guessing about where the line is. A specific exclusion ("avoid words like 'pursuant to,' 'herewith,' or 'in accordance with'") removes all the guessing.

Think of your prompt as a brief. A good brief tells the writer what to produce and what to steer away from. Both halves are necessary.

### The minimum viable context principle

There is a point of diminishing returns. You don't need to write three paragraphs of background for a task that needs one sentence. You don't need to explain your whole business history before asking Claude to summarise a document. Over-contextualised prompts are rare — most people err in the opposite direction — but they do happen, and they waste your time.

Minimum viable context means: enough detail that Claude can produce a useful response without asking clarifying questions, but no more. The test is simple. Read your prompt back. Is there anything Claude would need to know to complete this task that you haven't included? If yes, add it. Is there anything you've included that doesn't actually affect what you need? Cut it.

For short tasks — quick email replies, brief summaries, simple conversions — two or three sentences of context is usually enough. For complex outputs — proposals, policies, client-facing documents — you might write ten sentences of context, and that's fine. The length isn't the issue. Whether every sentence is doing useful work is.

Here is a rough guide. Ask yourself three questions before sending:

- **Has Claude got enough to know who I am for this task?** If you're a business owner in a specific industry, say so. If you're a team leader writing for your manager, say so.
- **Has Claude got enough to understand this specific situation?** Name the context that makes this task different from a generic version of the same thing.
- **Has Claude got enough to write for the right audience?** Name the person or group who'll receive this, and what matters to them.

If the answer to all three is yes, you have minimum viable context. Send it.

### Common mistake → better approach

**Mistake:** "Write me a social media post about our new service."

This prompt is almost information-free. Claude knows you want a social media post and that you have a new service. That's it. The output will be something like: "Excited to announce our latest offering! [Service] is now live. Contact us today to find out more." Publishable. Worthless.

**Better approach:**

```
I own a 3-person HR consulting firm in Brisbane. We've just launched a new
service: a half-day compliance audit for small businesses (under 20 staff)
who need to check whether their employment contracts, leave policies, and
onboarding processes are legally sound. It costs $595.

Write a LinkedIn post announcing this service. The audience is small business
owners — not HR professionals. They're worried about compliance but don't know
where to start. Write it in plain language. One paragraph, no bullet points.
Lead with the problem the business owner has, not with us. Don't write "Excited
to announce" — I want it to sound like an actual person wrote it.
```

The second prompt takes about 90 seconds to write. The output is ready to post with at most one edit pass. The difference is not the length — it's that every sentence gives Claude something specific to work with: the business size, the service details, the price, the audience mindset, the format, and an explicit "don't" that prevents the most common lazy opener.

## Worked examples

### Example 1: Onboarding email for a new team member

**Scenario:** A small construction company needs a welcome email for a new site supervisor starting Monday.

**Prompt:**
```
I run a residential construction company in Christchurch with 18 staff — mostly
licensed tradespeople and site supervisors. We have a new site supervisor
starting Monday. Her name is Priya. She's coming from a commercial construction
background and hasn't worked in residential before.

Write a welcome email from me (the owner, Dan) that feels warm but professional.
Tell her what Monday looks like: arrive at the office at 7:30am, meet the team,
then a site walkthrough in the afternoon. Let her know she can text my mobile
if she gets stuck.

Keep it under 150 words. Don't make it formal — this is a team that uses
first names and communicates directly.
```
**Why it works:** All three context types are present. Who Dan is (owner, residential construction, Christchurch). What the situation is (new hire from a commercial background, specific first-day logistics). Who the output is for (Priya, and implicitly Dan's direct communication style). The "don't make it formal" constraint plus the team-culture note ("first names, communicates directly") prevents a stiff corporate tone.

### Example 2: Client complaint response for a retail store

**Scenario:** A homewares retailer needs to respond to a customer complaint about a damaged delivery.

**Prompt:**
```
I own a small homewares store in Melbourne — we sell premium locally made
ceramics and textiles online and in-store. A customer ordered a ceramic vase
($185) that arrived with a hairline crack. She emailed to complain; her
message was polite but clearly disappointed.

Write a response email from me. Apologise genuinely without over-apologising
or being sycophantic. Offer a full replacement at no cost and ask for a photo
of the damage for our records. Let her know the replacement will ship within
3 business days.

Tone: warm and direct, not corporate. Under 120 words. Don't start with
"Thank you for reaching out" — I find that phrase hollow and I've told my
team not to use it.
```
**Why it works:** The business context (premium homewares, local ceramics) tells Claude the brand register. The situation (polite complaint, specific product, specific price) gives Claude the facts to work with. The audience note (a disappointed but reasonable customer) guides the tone. The explicit ban on "Thank you for reaching out" removes the most common response opener and forces a more genuine start.

### Example 3: Summary for a business partner who's time-poor

**Scenario:** A physiotherapy practice owner needs to brief her business partner on a supplier proposal.

**Prompt:**
```
I co-own a physiotherapy practice in Sydney with a business partner who is
also a practising physio — she sees patients full-time and has very little
admin time. We've received a proposal from a software vendor for a new
practice management system. The proposal is 14 pages.

Summarise the proposal into a one-page brief she can read in 3 minutes.
Pull out: the core features relevant to a physio practice, the pricing
tiers, implementation timeline, and any lock-in clauses or exit conditions
I should flag to her before we decide.

Write it for someone who knows healthcare but not software procurement.
No jargon. Use short bullet points under each heading, not paragraphs.
Don't include the vendor's case studies or testimonials — she won't care.

[paste proposal here]
```
**Why it works:** The audience description ("practising physio, no admin time, knows healthcare but not software procurement") is precise enough to guide both the selection of what to include and the language level. The explicit exclusion ("don't include case studies or testimonials") cuts the vendor padding that would fill a quarter of the page. The three-minute reading time constraint gives Claude a useful length target without a rigid word count.

### Example 4: Position description for a growing services business

**Scenario:** A marketing agency is hiring its first account manager and needs a job ad.

**Prompt:**
```
I run a 5-person digital marketing agency in Adelaide. We mostly work with
regional Australian businesses — agriculture, tourism, and local government.
We're hiring our first dedicated account manager. The role is responsible for
managing relationships with around 15 clients, running monthly reporting calls,
and coordinating deliverables between clients and our internal team.

Write a position description for the role. Include: role overview, key
responsibilities (6–8 dot points), who this role reports to (directly to me,
the founder), and what we're looking for in a candidate.

Plain language — this isn't a corporate HR document, it's something I'll post
on Seek. Don't list a degree as a requirement — I care about communication
skills and organisation, not credentials. Under 400 words total.
```
**Why it works:** The who-you-are context (5-person agency, regional clients, specific sectors) stops Claude from producing a generic corporate PD. The situation context (first account manager, 15 clients, defined scope) makes the responsibilities specific rather than invented. The two exclusions (not a corporate HR document, no degree requirement) shape the document before a word is written.

### Example 5: Staff policy for a hospitality business

**Scenario:** A cafe owner needs a short mobile phone policy for front-of-house staff.

**Prompt:**
```
I own a busy cafe in Brisbane with 9 staff — mostly casuals aged 18–25.
We've had increasing issues with staff checking their phones while on the
floor. I want a clear written policy, but I don't want it to feel punitive
or create resentment. My team is good — I just need something in writing
so expectations are clear.

Write a one-page mobile phone policy for front-of-house staff. Cover:
when phones are allowed (breaks only, in the back), when they're not
(on the floor, during service), and what happens if the policy isn't
followed (first: reminder, second: formal warning).

Write it in plain, human language — not legalese. It should feel like
something I wrote myself, not something downloaded from an HR website.
Don't include a signature section or reference to employment law — I'll
have my accountant review it separately.
```
**Why it works:** The who-you-are context (owner, 9 casuals, hospitality) sets the register. The situation context (good team, a real problem, explicit goal of avoiding resentment) guides the tone. The who-it's-for context (18–25 year old casuals) keeps the language plain. The two exclusions ("not legalese," no signature section or legal references) prevent the two most common failures on this type of document: stiffness and legal padding.

## Graded deliverable

**Title:** Write a context-rich prompt for a real task

**Brief:** Think of a task you currently do manually, have tried with AI and got mediocre results, or know you should be delegating but haven't. It needs to be something with a real output — a document, an email, a summary, a post, a policy, a report. Something where "good" has a clear meaning for you.

Your job is to write a context-rich prompt for that task — one that includes all three context types from this lesson — and then run it in Claude. Submit your work into the **Submission box** at the bottom of this lesson on the Claude Mastery platform. The AI grading engine returns your grade card within 60 seconds, scored against the rubric below.

**What to submit:**
1. A one-paragraph description of the task and why your current approach (manual, no AI, or basic AI prompts) falls short — what's missing or costing you time
2. The context-rich prompt you wrote — must include all three context types (who you are, what the situation is, who the output is for) and at least one clear exclusion ("don't," "avoid," or "leave out")
3. Claude's full response — paste it in completely, do not summarise or cut it
4. A reflection of approximately 100 words identifying which of the three context types made the biggest difference to the output and why

**Where to submit:** Paste all four parts into the **Submission box** at the bottom of this lesson on the Claude Mastery platform. The AI grading engine returns your grade card within 60 seconds.

**Length guide:** Task description: 2–5 sentences. Prompt: no minimum or maximum — write what the task needs, but every sentence should be earning its place. Claude's response: paste in full. Reflection: approximately 100 words.

**Pass mark:** 70 / 100. If you score below 70, the platform shows your grade card with feedback per criterion and activates a **Revise & Resubmit** button after a 24-hour cooldown.

**Rubric (100 points total — pass at 70):**
- **Specificity (25 pts)** — Does the prompt include all three context types with concrete, real detail? Who the student is must be grounded in an actual industry and role, not generic. The situation must describe this specific task instance, not just the task category. The audience must name a real person or group and say something about them. A prompt that could have been written by anyone in any industry scores low. A prompt where you can picture the student's actual business scores high.
- **Structure (25 pts)** — Is context established before the task instruction, so Claude has what it needs before it reads what to do? Does the prompt flow logically from setup to request? Are format instructions included — what the output should look like, not just what it should contain?
- **Constraint clarity (25 pts)** — Does the prompt include at least one specific "don't," exclusion, or negative instruction? Is it concrete (a named thing to avoid) rather than vague ("not too formal")? Strong submissions often have two or three exclusions that clearly came from real experience of what generic AI output gets wrong for this task.
- **Outcome focus (25 pts)** — Does the reflection go beyond "the output was good"? Does it specifically name which of the three context types (who you are, the situation, the audience) made the biggest visible difference to Claude's response, and explain why that element mattered for this particular task?

## Notes for the AI grading engine

These notes are read by the grading engine when scoring submissions for this lesson. They are not shown to the student.

- **Reward (high marks):**
  - Prompt contains all three context types with specific, non-generic detail — grader can identify the student's actual industry, the real task situation, and the real audience from reading the prompt alone
  - At least one exclusion is concrete and clearly drawn from experience — it names a specific word, phrase, section, or format to avoid, not just "keep it professional"
  - Context is established before the task instruction — setup precedes request
  - Reflection names one specific context element (by type: who they are / the situation / the audience) and explains what difference it made to the output, with reference to something in Claude's actual response
  - Task description genuinely identifies a current workflow problem — not invented or hypothetical

- **Penalise (dock points):**
  - Prompt is generic or could have been written by anyone — no industry, no real situation, no named audience
  - Context types are missing: any of the three absent or present only as a vague label ("I'm a small business owner" with no further detail)
  - Exclusions are absent entirely, or present only as vague adjectives ("not too technical," "keep it simple") with no specific named thing to avoid
  - Reflection evaluates only positively — "it was great, very useful" with no comparison across context types or no identification of which element mattered most
  - Claude's response is paraphrased or partially pasted rather than submitted in full

- **Common 60–69 patterns (near-pass):** The task is real, and two of the three context types are present, but the third is missing — most often the audience note. The exclusion exists but is too vague to count as specific. The reflection is honest but identifies "specificity in general" rather than naming a particular context type. In feedback, name the missing or weak element, quote where it should appear in their prompt, and give a one-line example of what a stronger version would look like.

- **Common 80+ patterns (excellent):** All three context types are present with enough real detail that the grader can picture the student's actual work situation. At least one exclusion is precise — a named phrase, a named section, a specific thing that generic AI output tends to produce that this student doesn't want. The reflection is analytical: it names the specific context type that moved the needle most, points to evidence in Claude's response, and explains why that element was the key variable for this task. The task description shows genuine self-awareness about where their current process falls short.

- **Feedback tone:** Direct, specific, and kind. Quote the student's own words when identifying wins or fix points — do not give abstract advice. If a context type is missing, quote the prompt and show exactly where to add it. If an exclusion is too vague, give a concrete example of a stronger version based on the student's own task. Always end with one concrete next step: the single most important addition or change that would lift their submission to 70+ or, for those already passing, the one thing that would take them from 70 to 85.

- **Resubmission gating:** If the student resubmits, compare against their first submission stored in the platform. If they added a missing context type, name the improvement explicitly and quote both versions. If they strengthened a vague exclusion into a specific one, acknowledge that directly. If the reflection now names a specific context type where it was previously generic, say so. If the revised submission is substantially unchanged — same context gaps, same vague exclusion, same surface-level reflection — say so plainly and name the one thing still missing before the gate can open.

## Industry personalisation hooks

The five worked examples span construction (onboarding email), retail (complaint response), healthcare (partner summary), professional services (job ad), and hospitality (staff policy). In personalised lesson variants, examples should be redrawn for the learner's own industry sector, while keeping the structural teaching — three context types, specificity vs. vagueness, minimum viable context — unchanged. The deliverable brief phrase "a task you currently do manually, have tried with AI and got mediocre results, or know you should be delegating" is intentionally broad and universal; personalised variants can append two or three industry-specific task examples in parentheses to help the student identify their real task faster (e.g. for a trades business: "quoting follow-up email, end-of-job report, or supplier dispute response").
