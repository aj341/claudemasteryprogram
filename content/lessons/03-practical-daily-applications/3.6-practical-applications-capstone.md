## Lesson script

### This is where Module 3 ends — and your working pattern begins

Over the past five lessons you have covered a lot of ground. In Lesson 3.1 you learned how to brief Claude on a writing task and get a draft that sounds like your business. In 3.2 you used Claude to do structured research — not open-ended browsing, but focused extraction that produces a usable output. In 3.3 you brought Claude into a real decision, supplying the criteria and constraints that let it pressure-test your thinking rather than just agree with it. In 3.4 you used Claude as an accelerated learning tool — pulling out the specific knowledge you need without reading a textbook. And in 3.5 you mapped where Claude fits inside your existing workflows: the tasks, handoffs, and repeating jobs where it can cut real time.

That is a concrete set of capabilities. This lesson is about making them stick.

### What you can now do — a real inventory

Before you build the habit, take stock of what you have. This is not motivational framing. It is a practical checklist you can return to whenever you are trying to remember which Claude capability fits the task in front of you.

**Writing and communication.** You can draft client-facing content — emails, proposals, summaries, social posts, event copy — with a properly specified prompt. You can control tone, length, and format. You can revise targeted sections without restarting the whole session. You know how to feed Claude a sample of your voice and ask it to match the style.

**Research and synthesis.** You can extract specific information from a topic, document, or market area using a structured brief. You can ask Claude to compare options, identify gaps, or pull out the specific facts you need — rather than producing a wall of background text you then have to read through. You know how to constrain the research scope so you get what is useful rather than what is comprehensive.

**Decision support.** You can use Claude to stress-test a decision before you make it. You can give it your options, your constraints, and your criteria and ask it to challenge your assumptions. You know the difference between using Claude as a rubber stamp ("does this sound like a good idea?") and using it as a structured challenge ("here are the three options I am considering — what am I not seeing?"). The second use produces real value. The first does not.

**Learning on demand.** You can use Claude to get up to speed on a topic you do not know well — a regulation, a technology, an industry you are pitching to — without spending an afternoon reading. You know how to ask for explanation at the right level, how to ask follow-up questions without losing the thread, and how to turn a learning conversation into a reference document you can keep.

**Workflow integration.** You have identified the recurring tasks in your work where Claude can be used as part of a regular process, not a one-off experiment. You know which tasks belong on the regular list, which tasks are not worth the setup, and which tasks are borderline — good with the right prompt, not worth it with a vague one.

That is your inventory. Keep it somewhere you will look at it.

### What a Claude week looks like

Most people who start using Claude well do not use it constantly. They use it consistently. There is a difference.

Constant use means reaching for Claude every time you have a task, whether it is suited to Claude or not. This leads to frustration — bad outputs on tasks Claude is not suited for, prompt fatigue, and a general sense that it is more effort than it is worth.

Consistent use means building Claude into the specific parts of your week where it reliably saves you time. The tasks are predictable. The context is already loaded or easy to load. The prompt patterns are familiar. You open Claude, run the session, get a usable output, and move on.

Here is what a consistent Claude week might look like for a small business owner in a service business:

**Monday morning.** Before the week starts, you open Claude and ask it to help you draft the one email you have been putting off — a difficult message to a client about a project delay, a follow-up on an unpaid invoice, a quote for a job that needs careful framing. Context first. Task second. Ten minutes, done.

**Mid-week.** You need to prepare for a meeting with a supplier or a potential client. You use Claude to research the company — not everything, just the three or four things that will make you sound prepared. Or you use it to draft the agenda. Or to turn your scattered meeting notes into a clean summary you can send afterwards.

**End of week.** You have a recurring content commitment — a newsletter, a LinkedIn post, a quarterly update for clients. You open Claude, paste in the brief you have already saved, update the variable details (this week's topic, this week's product, this week's news), and get a first draft in two minutes. You edit it. It goes out.

That is three Claude sessions across a week. None of them are long. All of them replace a task that would have taken meaningfully longer without Claude. That is a consistent pattern, and consistent patterns compound.

### Finding your highest-value use cases

Here is the test that matters: not what Claude is most impressive at, but what saves you the most time on tasks that actually need to get done.

Impressive does not equal valuable. Claude can do things that feel remarkable but do not actually change your working week. It can also do things that feel unremarkable — drafting a routine email, extracting five bullet points from a long article, turning your messy notes into a readable summary — that save you thirty minutes of mental work on tasks you do every single week.

Thirty minutes a week is twenty-six hours a year. That is a full week of working time, recovered from tasks that are necessary but not your highest-value work. That is the number to think about when you are deciding which use cases to prioritise.

To find your highest-value use cases, answer three questions:

**What takes you longer than it should?** Not what is hard or complex — what is slow? The task where you sit down, write three words, delete them, check your email, come back, write three more words. Claude is most valuable on tasks where the friction is in starting or structuring, not in the thinking itself. Drafting is a common one. Research that requires you to read before you can synthesise is another.

**What repeats?** A Claude use case that runs once a week is worth ten times a use case that runs once a month. The recurring tasks — weekly communications, meeting prep, quote follow-ups, content production — are where prompt templates pay off. You build the prompt once, store it, and the next fifteen uses cost you almost nothing in setup time.

**What do you avoid?** The tasks you put off — not because they are unimportant but because they feel unpleasant or effortful — are often ideal Claude tasks. An email you are dreading because you are not sure how to frame it. A document you have not started because you do not know where to begin. Claude is good at overcoming the blank page problem. If a task is on your list and keeps staying there, ask yourself whether Claude could at least give you a starting point.

Your highest-value use cases sit at the intersection of all three: tasks that are slow, recurring, and easy to avoid. Those are the ones to build proper prompt templates for and run every week without exception.

### Building the habit: the daily trigger

A skill that only activates when you consciously remember to use it is not a working habit. It is a special-occasion tool. And special-occasion tools get used occasionally.

The difference between people who integrate Claude effectively and people who use it once a month is not intelligence or technical skill. It is a trigger. A specific, predictable moment in the working day that reliably leads to opening Claude.

Triggers work because they attach a new behaviour to an existing one. You do not have to decide to use Claude — the existing behaviour prompts it automatically.

Here are trigger patterns that work for small business owners. Pick one and test it for two weeks before deciding whether to keep it, adjust it, or swap it for another.

**The blank-page trigger.** Any time you open a new document or email and face a blank screen, you open Claude first. You spend two minutes writing the prompt — context, task, format — and use Claude's output as your starting point. You are not committing to using what Claude produces. You are committing to not staring at a blank screen alone.

**The Monday-morning trigger.** The first thing you do on Monday after checking your calendar is open Claude and draft the one difficult communication you have been carrying from the previous week. It does not matter what it is. Pick the one piece of writing you have been avoiding and run a ten-minute Claude session on it. One task, every Monday, before anything else.

**The meeting-prep trigger.** Any time you have a meeting with someone you have not met before, or a meeting where you want to show up prepared, you run a five-minute Claude research and agenda session the morning before. You do not have to use everything Claude produces. The act of running the session forces you to think about what you want from the meeting — and Claude often surfaces one thing you had not considered.

**The end-of-session trigger.** Any time you finish a significant piece of client work — a project, a proposal, a complex email thread — you open Claude and ask it to help you draft the follow-up or next-step communication before you close the file. The context is already in your head. It takes five minutes. It means the follow-through happens immediately instead of sitting on a list.

None of these triggers require a habit of daily Claude use. They require attaching Claude to something you already do: opening a document, starting Monday, preparing for a meeting, finishing a piece of work. The trigger is already there. You are just adding Claude to what happens next.

### Common mistake → better approach

**Mistake:** Treating Claude as a tool you use when you feel like it, with no consistent trigger or pattern — and then wondering why you are not getting value from it.

This is the most common failure mode after completing a module like this one. The student has the skills. They have run successful sessions during the lessons. They know what Claude can do. But they return to their normal working week and Claude just does not come to mind. Two weeks later they open it, run a prompt, get a reasonable output, and then forget about it again for another two weeks.

The problem is not motivation. It is architecture. There is no trigger that reliably puts Claude in front of them at the moment a task is appropriate for it.

**Better approach:** Pick one trigger from the list above. Write it down as a specific rule, for example:

```
When I open a new email or document, I open Claude first.
```

Apply that rule for two weeks without evaluating whether it is the right rule. At the end of two weeks, you will have used Claude six to ten times in a consistent pattern. That is enough data to know whether the trigger is working and what to adjust. Habit design is not about willpower. It is about the conditions you create. One trigger, applied consistently, will do more for your Claude practice than all the capability in this module combined.

## Worked examples

### Example 1: Writing task — client project update email

**Scenario:** A graphic designer needs to update a retail client on a delayed brand refresh project.

**Prompt:**
```
I run a two-person graphic design studio in Brisbane. Our clients are
small and mid-size retail businesses. We're known for being direct and
reliable — not corporate.

I need to send a project update to a retail client. Their brand refresh
is running two weeks behind because a key supplier delayed the print
samples we needed for approval. The client is generally reasonable but
gets anxious when they don't have visibility.

Draft an update email that:
- Acknowledges the delay clearly and takes ownership
- Explains the reason briefly without blaming the supplier by name
- States the new timeline and the next step they will see from us
- Closes warmly

Tone: direct, honest, reassuring. Under 200 words.
```

**Why it works:** The prompt specifies the business voice ("direct and reliable — not corporate"), the client's emotional state (gets anxious without visibility), and the four structural elements the email needs to contain. Claude is not guessing at tone or format — both are explicit. The word limit prevents padding and keeps the output immediately usable.

### Example 2: Research task — preparing for a supplier meeting

**Scenario:** A café owner is meeting a new coffee roaster and wants to show up informed.

**Prompt:**
```
I own a single-location café in Melbourne's inner north. We're known for
specialty coffee and minimal waste. I'm meeting a coffee roaster next
week — they're a mid-size Australian roaster who supplies around 80 cafés
nationally and are expanding into the Victorian market.

Before the meeting I need to understand:
1. The key questions I should ask about their sourcing and traceability
   practices — my customers care about this
2. The commercial terms that matter most when entering a supply agreement
   with a roaster (exclusivity, minimum volumes, pricing review periods)
3. Two or three things that often go wrong in new café-roaster relationships
   and how to raise them without seeming difficult

Give me a one-page briefing. Use plain English. No jargon.
```

**Why it works:** The prompt names the specific output (a one-page briefing, plain English), the three research areas in order of priority, and the constraint on how sensitive topics should be handled. The student does not need to know anything about roaster contracts before the meeting — they just need to know the right questions, and this prompt surfaces them.

### Example 3: Decision support — choosing between two pricing models

**Scenario:** A bookkeeper is deciding whether to switch from hourly billing to fixed monthly retainers.

**Prompt:**
```
I run a solo bookkeeping practice in Auckland. Most of my clients are
trades and hospitality businesses — 12 active clients, all hourly-billed,
rates between $75 and $95 per hour. I'm considering moving to fixed
monthly retainers.

I'm worried about:
- Under-quoting on complex clients who have messy records
- Losing clients who prefer to pay per job
- How to set the right monthly rate for each client's workload

I'm leaning toward retainers because I want predictable income.

Challenge my thinking. What am I not seeing? What would make retainers
the wrong call for my specific situation? What conditions would make it
clearly the right call?
```

**Why it works:** The prompt explicitly asks Claude to challenge the student's existing preference — not validate it. This is the critical difference between decision support and a rubber stamp. By naming the three specific worries, the student gets targeted pressure-testing rather than a generic pros-and-cons list.

### Example 4: Learning on demand — understanding a new regulation

**Scenario:** A physiotherapy practice owner needs to understand new privacy rule changes that affect patient records.

**Prompt:**
```
I run a small physiotherapy practice in Sydney with 3 practitioners.
I am not a lawyer and I have no compliance background.

The Australian Privacy Act is being updated with new rules around health
records. I need to understand:
1. What is actually changing — in plain terms, not legal language
2. What a small health practice like mine specifically needs to do or change
3. What the realistic risk is if we do not act

Explain it as if I am competent but not a specialist. Tell me where my
understanding might be wrong rather than just confirming what I think I know.
Give me a list of the three most important actions to take in the next
30 days.
```

**Why it works:** The "explain as if I am competent but not a specialist" instruction prevents over-simplification without inviting jargon. The instruction to flag where the student's understanding might be wrong adds active challenge rather than passive explanation. The 30-day action list forces Claude to prioritise rather than comprehensively list every possible consideration.

### Example 5: Workflow integration — recurring weekly client report

**Scenario:** A marketing consultant sends a weekly performance summary to a retail client and has turned it into a Claude template.

**Prompt:**
```
I'm a marketing consultant. Each Monday I send a weekly performance
summary to a retail client who runs an e-commerce homewares store.

This week's data:
- Website sessions: 4,280 (up 12% on last week)
- Conversion rate: 2.4% (up from 2.1%)
- Revenue: $18,600 (up 18% on last week)
- Top traffic source this week: email campaign sent Thursday
- One issue: cart abandonment rate increased from 61% to 68%

Write a 150-word performance summary in the following structure:
1. Headline result (one sentence)
2. What drove it (two to three sentences)
3. One issue to watch (two sentences)
4. What we are doing about it (one to two sentences)

Tone: confident, clear, no jargon. This client is smart but not a
digital marketing specialist.
```

**Why it works:** This is a template-style prompt — the structure is fixed every week, only the data changes. The student stores this prompt, updates the numbers each Monday, and runs it in under five minutes. The format instructions are precise enough that Claude produces a consistent structure week after week without the student having to rewrite or reformat anything.

## Graded deliverable

**Title:** Your Claude week

**Brief:** This is the Module 3 capstone. Instead of a single Claude session, you are submitting evidence of five real Claude uses across a real working week. The tasks must come from your actual work this week — not hypotheticals, not examples from the lesson, not tasks you imagine you might do. Real prompts you actually sent. Real outputs you actually received.

The purpose of this deliverable is to show that you can apply Module 3 skills across different task types and evaluate which ones produce the most value for your business. The AI grading engine is looking for specificity, completeness, and honest business judgment — not perfect prompts. Paste everything into the **Submission box** at the bottom of this lesson on the Claude Mastery platform. The AI grading engine returns your grade card within 60 seconds.

**What to submit:**

1. **Five task entries.** Each entry must include all four of the following components:
   - **Task type** — which Module 3 skill area does this fall into? (Writing, Research, Decision support, Learning, Workflow integration)
   - **The prompt** — exact text as you sent it to Claude, copied from Claude.ai. Do not clean it up or rewrite it.
   - **Claude's response** — full response, or a summary if the response was very long (aim for the key output content, not just "Claude gave me a good answer")
   - **Outcome note** — one sentence on what you did with the output and whether it was useful

2. **A ranked list of your top 3 most valuable Claude applications** from the five tasks, with a one-to-two sentence explanation for each — why was this one valuable to your business specifically? Not what was most impressive. Most time-saving, most decision-relevant, or most reliably repeatable.

3. **A 150-word reflection** on what Module 3 changed about how you work. What are you doing differently this week compared to the week before Module 3? What has not changed yet, and why?

**Where to submit:** Paste all three items into the **Submission box** at the bottom of this lesson on the Claude Mastery platform. The AI grading engine returns your grade card within 60 seconds.

**Length guide:** Each task entry: 100–300 words depending on prompt and response length. Ranking: 3–4 sentences total. Reflection: 150 words (not a strict limit, but this should be substantive — not a paragraph of general positivity).

**Pass mark:** 70 / 100. If you score below 70, the platform shows your grade card with feedback per criterion and activates a **Revise & Resubmit** button after a 24-hour cooldown.

**Rubric (100 points total — pass at 70):**

- **Specificity (25 pts)** — Are all five tasks real and from this week — not hypothetical, not recycled from lesson examples? Does each entry name a real task with enough detail that the grader can picture the actual business situation? Generic entries ("I asked Claude to help with an email") with no specifics score in the bottom third of this criterion regardless of other qualities.

- **Structure (25 pts)** — Does every entry include all four components: task type, prompt (exact text), response or summary, and outcome note? Entries missing any of the four components lose marks proportionally. The ranking and reflection must both be present and clearly labelled. Submissions that run everything together as unbroken prose rather than organised sections score in the bottom half of this criterion.

- **Constraint clarity (25 pts)** — Do the prompts across the five entries show Module 2 prompt engineering skills applied in practice? Look for: context included before the task, specific format instructions, tone or audience instructions, word count or length constraints, and explicit exclusions where relevant. Casual questions with no structure ("what should I do about X?") indicate the student has not applied what they learned. At least three of the five prompts should show clear constraint and format specification.

- **Outcome focus (25 pts)** — Does the ranking show genuine business judgment — not just enthusiasm? Does the student evaluate value in concrete terms (time saved, decision made, task completed) rather than just saying it was useful or interesting? Does the reflection show honest assessment of what has and has not changed in their working pattern? Reflections that are purely positive with no acknowledgment of what still needs work score in the bottom third of this criterion.

## Notes for the AI grading engine

(Not shown to student)

- **Reward (high marks):**
  - All five task entries are clearly from the student's actual business — names, industries, task specifics, and outcomes are plausible and specific, not generic
  - Prompts show Module 2 skills applied: context before task, format instructions, at least one constraint per prompt (word count, tone, audience, exclusion)
  - The ranking uses concrete business logic — time saved, frequency of the task, whether the output was used directly — not just "it was impressive" or "it was helpful"
  - The reflection distinguishes between what has genuinely changed in the student's working pattern and what is still aspirational — honest assessment, not a success summary
  - The five entries cover genuinely different task types — not five writing tasks or five research tasks, but visible spread across the Module 3 skill areas
  - Outcome notes are specific: "I sent it with minor edits" or "I used it to make the decision the same afternoon" rather than "it was useful"

- **Penalise (dock points):**
  - Any entry that is clearly hypothetical or that reproduces an example from the lesson — these fail the Specificity criterion immediately
  - Prompts that are casual questions with no structure ("what's the best way to handle this?") — these fail Constraint clarity and suggest Module 2 skills were not applied
  - Missing components in any entry — a task type label alone with no prompt text, or a prompt with no outcome note — dock Structure marks per missing component
  - A ranking that does not explain business value — "this was my favourite" or "this one worked best" without explaining why it was valuable to the business
  - A reflection that is purely positive and forward-looking with no honest assessment of current gaps or limitations in the student's Claude practice

- **Common 60–69 patterns:**
  The student has done the work — five real entries, mostly structured — but the prompts lack constraint specification. They sent reasonably clear task descriptions but skipped format instructions, word counts, or tone guidance. Claude produced something workable but generic, and the student used it anyway. The ranking says the entries were useful without explaining why they saved time or mattered to the business. The reflection is a paragraph of general positivity. In feedback, quote one of the student's prompts and show exactly one constraint they could add to make it produce a better output. Then ask: in your ranking, can you name how much time each task saved? That is the question the reflection needs to answer.

- **Common 80+ patterns:**
  The five entries are specific enough that the grader can picture the actual business. The prompts are clearly structured — context, task, format, constraint — across all or most of the five entries. The ranking evaluates value in time or decision terms: "this one runs every Monday and takes three minutes instead of twenty" or "this one changed which option I chose." The reflection names something that has not yet changed and why — honest, not just grateful. The student has already identified their trigger or is close to doing so. Where appropriate, acknowledge the specific entry that showed the strongest prompt engineering and explain why. End with one concrete thing to build on in Module 4.

- **Feedback tone:** Direct, specific, kind. Quote the student's actual prompt text when identifying a strength or a gap — do not speak in generalities. This is the module capstone: students who get here have done substantial work and deserve specific recognition of what they have built. Always end with one concrete next step: a named improvement to a specific prompt, a trigger to try, or a task type to prioritise in the next module.

- **Resubmission gating:** Compare the resubmission to the original across three specific dimensions: (1) did any prompts gain constraint or format specification that was missing? (2) did the ranking gain concrete business-value language? (3) did the reflection become more honest about gaps and not just more positive? If all three improved, name each. If only one or two improved, acknowledge the progress and name the remaining gap. If the resubmission is essentially unchanged, say so directly and name the specific thing that still needs to change to reach 70.

## Industry personalisation hooks

The five-entry format of this capstone means personalisation is built into the deliverable — students draw from their own working week, so the task types, prompts, and outcomes automatically reflect their industry. For facilitated cohorts or personalised course variants, the worked examples in this lesson can be swapped to reflect the student's sector without changing any structural content: the five examples here (graphic design, hospitality/café, professional services/bookkeeping, allied health, marketing consulting) cover common AU/NZ small business industries, but could be substituted with trades, property management, retail, education, or legal services examples depending on enrolment profile. The ranking and reflection components are universally applicable — business-value judgment is the same skill regardless of industry.
