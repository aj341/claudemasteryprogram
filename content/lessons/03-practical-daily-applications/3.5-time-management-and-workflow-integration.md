## Lesson script

### The integration question you should actually be asking

Most people who use Claude regularly still treat it like a tool they pick up when they think of it. A prompt here, a draft there, a quick question when something comes up. It works. But it doesn't compound. You're not saving an hour a week — you're saving five minutes at a time, randomly.

The question that changes this is not "should I use AI?" You've answered that already. The useful question is: where in my day does AI replace manual work?

That's a different question. It forces you to look at your actual schedule — not a hypothetical version of it — and find the specific moments where you are doing something by hand that Claude could do in a fraction of the time. Not categories. Moments. "Every Tuesday morning I write four client update emails that take me 40 minutes total." That's a moment. "I spend time on communication" is not.

This lesson is about making that map concrete, turning it into a habit, and understanding what stops most people from getting there.

### Finding your friction points

Friction points are the tasks you do every week that cost you more time than they should. They're the ones where, if you're honest, you procrastinate for a few minutes before starting. They feel heavier than their actual importance. Not complex, high-judgment work — the other kind. The repetitive, time-consuming kind that doesn't require your best thinking, but still pulls from your energy budget.

Here's how to find them. Look back at the last two weeks of your actual work. Not what you planned to do — what you actually did. Which tasks took 20 minutes or more that are essentially the same every time you do them? Which ones do you leave until the end of the day or the end of the week because you don't want to start? Which ones would you immediately delegate if you had a capable person to hand them to?

Make a short list — five to eight tasks. Then for each one, ask: is this task primarily text-based input and output? Does it follow a recognisable pattern from one instance to the next? Could someone who understood the task well produce a solid first draft if I gave them the background?

If the answers are yes, yes, and yes — that is a Claude task. Not a task you can theoretically use Claude for. A task you should be using Claude for right now.

For example: the follow-up email you send every client after a first meeting. Every time, you're covering similar ground — confirming what was discussed, next steps, a warm close. The specifics change but the structure doesn't. That email currently takes you 20 minutes. With a good prompt and a brief of the meeting notes pasted in, Claude produces a first draft in under two minutes. You spend three more minutes editing. You're done in five. You've taken back 15 minutes — and you'll do that again every time you have a first meeting.

That's one friction point. Most people have six to ten of them.

### Building a batching habit

The biggest mistake people make when they start integrating Claude is one-off use. They use Claude when a specific task comes up, as a reactive response to the moment. This works, but it produces inconsistent results — sometimes you've thought through the prompt carefully, sometimes you haven't — and it doesn't build any skill or efficiency over time.

The better approach is batching. Set aside one or two dedicated times each week — 20 to 30 minutes each — and run all your similar Claude tasks in one session. Your draft emails go in together. Your social media posts for the week go in together. Your meeting summaries from yesterday go in together. You're in the same headspace, using the same prompt structure, and you get faster with each one.

Batching does two things. First, it means you're not deciding on the fly whether to use Claude — you've already decided, and you've scheduled the time. Second, it means you're comparing your prompts directly against each other, which surfaces what's working and what isn't faster than scattered use ever does.

Here's what a simple batching system looks like in practice:

```
Monday morning — 20 minutes:
- Draft follow-up emails from last week's client meetings (paste notes, one prompt per email)
- Write this week's social posts (one prompt for all three)

Friday afternoon — 15 minutes:
- Summarise the week's completed work for my project log
- Draft next week's team briefing note
```

That's 35 minutes a week of deliberate Claude use replacing close to two hours of manual effort. Those 35 minutes also build your skill — you'll find better prompts, discover new task types to add, and get faster at producing usable output on the first or second attempt.

Start with two sessions. Same days, same times. Treat them like meetings with yourself that don't get cancelled.

### The context cost: Projects versus standalone conversations

Every time you open a new conversation with Claude, you start from zero. Claude has no memory of any previous conversation you've had. It doesn't know you run a 12-person construction company. It doesn't know your client is a stickler for formal language. It doesn't know you've already decided on a particular direction for a proposal. You have to tell it again, from scratch, every time.

This is called the context cost. And it compounds. If you're using Claude for ten different tasks across a week, and each one starts with two minutes of context-setting, that's 20 minutes of overhead per week. Across a year, that's substantial. More importantly, prompts without adequate context produce weaker outputs — Claude is guessing at your situation rather than knowing it.

Claude Projects solves this. A Project is a persistent workspace where you set your context once. You describe your business, your tone, your preferences, the common situations you deal with. Every conversation you start inside that Project inherits all of that context automatically. You never re-explain who you are.

Use a Project when:
- You have a set of recurring tasks that all require the same business context
- You've built up a prompt structure that works and you want to build on it rather than start fresh each time
- You're working on something ongoing — a proposal that spans multiple sessions, a content series for the month ahead, a set of policy documents you're updating

Use a standalone conversation when:
- It's a one-off task with its own unique context that doesn't relate to your usual work
- You're experimenting with a new prompt approach and don't want to pollute your Project history
- The task is short, self-contained, and you don't expect to return to it

Most small business owners should have one Project that represents their core work — their business context, their clients, their typical task types. Everything else is a standalone. That distinction alone removes most of the context cost from your week.

### Common mistake → better approach

**Mistake:** Keeping Claude use completely spontaneous — opening a new conversation each time a task comes up, re-explaining your business context each time, and never building on prompts that worked previously.

**Better approach:** Create a Project for your core business context. Inside it, keep a pinned note with the standard background Claude needs: your business name, what you do, who your clients are, your usual tone preferences, and any standing constraints (e.g. "always write in Australian English," "avoid pricing commitments in client-facing drafts"). Now every conversation in that Project starts informed. You write shorter prompts. You get better output faster. And when you find a prompt that works particularly well for a recurring task, you save it — either inside the Project or in a simple notes file — so you're not rebuilding it from scratch next time.

### What good Claude integration actually looks like

Three examples from AU/NZ small businesses — described the way their owners would describe their weeks.

**Example 1: Kate, sole-trader bookkeeper, Auckland**

Kate has 18 small business clients. Every month she produces a one-page summary for each client covering their P&L highlights, any outstanding issues, and her recommendations. She used to write these from scratch — usually 25–30 minutes each, 7.5 hours total per month.

Now she has a Project set up with her practice context and her preferred report format. Each Monday she pastes one client's month-end figures and a short note of any issues into Claude and gets a structured first draft in under two minutes. She spends five minutes editing and personalising. She's doing 18 reports in under two hours. She's taken back five and a half hours a month — and the reports are more consistent than they used to be.

**Example 2: Marcus, owner of a landscaping company, Brisbane**

Marcus has four staff and eight to twelve active residential clients at any time. The part of his job he least enjoyed was writing quotes — not calculating the numbers, but writing the accompanying letter that explained the scope and justified the price. He'd been putting them off for days.

He now dictates his quote notes into his phone immediately after a site visit — materials, scope, any constraints, relevant client preferences — and pastes the transcript into Claude with a prompt that produces a professional quote letter. The process takes eight minutes instead of 45. He sends quotes the same day, every time. His quote-to-acceptance rate went up because clients stopped hearing from competitors in the gap.

**Example 3: Nina, director of a marketing agency, Melbourne**

Nina's agency has seven staff and multiple active client accounts. The weekly bottleneck was internal briefing notes — the documents that explain to the team what work needs doing for each client that week and why. Nina was spending 90 minutes writing them every Monday.

She now does them in a Project that already knows her agency's client accounts and her preferred briefing format. She writes rough notes — "retail client, autumn social campaign, need three concepts by Thursday, lead is Priya" — and Claude turns that into a structured brief in the right format. She reviews, edits, sends. Monday morning briefing time: 20 minutes instead of 90.

None of these business owners changed their core work. They identified where the manual drafting work was, built a repeatable system around it, and now that time is theirs again.

## Worked examples

### Example 1: Identifying friction points

**Scenario:** A financial planner in Canberra wants to pinpoint which recurring tasks are best suited for Claude.

**Prompt:**
```
I'm a self-employed financial planner in Canberra with 40 active clients. My recurring tasks include:

- Writing file notes after every client meeting (usually 15–20 minutes each, three meetings per day on average)
- Drafting cover letters for Statements of Advice (usually 30–45 minutes each, around 6 per month)
- Writing my quarterly newsletter to all clients (usually 3 hours)
- Responding to routine client enquiries about account balances and contribution limits (usually 5–10 minutes each, maybe 10 per week)

I want to identify which of these tasks has the highest integration potential with Claude — meaning: where can I get a solid first draft in under 3 minutes that I can review and send within 10?

For each task, tell me: integration potential (high/medium/low), main bottleneck, and what I'd need to include in my prompt to make it work.
```
**Why it works:** The prompt names four concrete tasks with real time estimates rather than categories. The "integration potential" framing — a solid draft in under 3 minutes, send-ready in 10 — gives Claude a clear definition of success. The three-part output request (potential, bottleneck, prompt ingredients) means the answer is immediately actionable.

### Example 2: Setting up a batching system

**Scenario:** A property manager in Sydney wants to batch her recurring Claude tasks into two weekly sessions.

**Prompt:**
```
I manage a portfolio of 35 residential properties in Sydney for a small property management firm — just me and one admin assistant. My recurring tasks that are text-heavy and follow similar patterns each time include:

- Routine maintenance request responses to tenants (around 8 per week)
- Monthly condition report summaries for landlords (around 6 per month)
- Renewal offer letters when a fixed-term tenancy is ending (around 4 per month)
- End-of-month summary emails to all landlords covering rent collection status and any outstanding issues

Help me design a batching system for these tasks. I want two weekly Claude sessions — one on Tuesday morning, one on Thursday afternoon — and I want to know: which tasks go into which session, what I need to prepare in advance to make each session efficient, and what my Project context should include so I'm not re-explaining my role every time.
```
**Why it works:** The student has done the thinking — she knows she wants two sessions and has named the days and times. Asking Claude to allocate tasks and specify the prep work needed means the answer builds a complete system, not just a concept. The request to specify Project context makes the output immediately implementable.

### Example 3: Writing a Project context note

**Scenario:** A boutique HR consultant in Perth wants to set up a Claude Project that covers her most common work.

**Prompt:**
```
I run a small HR consultancy in Perth — just me. My typical clients are AU businesses with 10–50 employees, usually in professional services, retail, or healthcare. My work includes:

- Drafting HR policies (leave, performance management, code of conduct)
- Writing position descriptions for client hiring
- Preparing scripts for difficult workplace conversations (e.g., performance improvement discussions, redundancy notifications)
- Writing template letters for common HR situations (warnings, acknowledgement of resignation, end-of-probation outcomes)

I'm setting up a Claude Project for my core work. Write a Project context note I can paste in as my Project instructions. It should tell Claude who I am, who my clients are, the types of tasks I use Claude for, my tone preferences (professional but plain — no HR jargon where plain language works), and any standing rules I always want applied (Australian employment law context, use Australian English spelling, never make specific legal advice claims in any document).

Keep it under 200 words. It needs to be something I can paste directly.
```
**Why it works:** Rather than asking Claude to explain Projects conceptually, this prompt asks for the actual output — a paste-ready context note. The standing rules (Australian law, Australian English, no legal advice claims) are exactly the kind of constraints that save repeated re-prompting later. The 200-word limit is practical: Project instruction notes that are too long dilute the key instructions.

### Example 4: Calculating time savings before committing

**Scenario:** A retail business owner in Adelaide wants to assess whether switching to Claude for a specific task is worth the setup time.

**Prompt:**
```
I own a homewares retail shop in Adelaide — physical store plus online. I currently spend about 2 hours every Sunday writing product descriptions for the 15–20 new products we list each week on our website. Each description is around 80–100 words.

Before I invest time in setting up a Claude workflow for this, I want to understand the realistic time saving. Assume I'll need 30 minutes upfront to write a good prompt template, and that Claude will produce a first draft in under 1 minute per description that takes me 3 minutes to review and adjust.

Calculate: how many weeks before I break even on the setup time? What does the weekly time saving look like in practice? And what's the one thing most likely to make this workflow take longer than expected?
```
**Why it works:** The prompt gives Claude the real numbers — current time, assumed prompt time, assumed review time, upfront setup cost — and asks for a practical ROI calculation rather than a general endorsement of AI use. Asking for the most likely failure mode ("what's most likely to make it take longer") prompts a realistic rather than optimistic answer.

### Example 5: Diagnosing why Claude integration has stalled

**Scenario:** An accounting firm manager in Wellington started using Claude but has drifted back to doing tasks manually and wants to understand why.

**Prompt:**
```
I'm the practice manager at a 6-person accounting firm in Wellington. I started using Claude about two months ago for drafting client communications and internal meeting summaries. I had a good run for three weeks, then I drifted back to writing everything manually. I'm still using Claude occasionally but not consistently.

I think I know some of the reasons but I want you to help me diagnose what's likely happening. Here's what I've noticed:
- I keep re-explaining the same context about our firm every time I open Claude
- My prompts are inconsistent — sometimes I get great output, sometimes mediocre
- I forget to use Claude when I'm busy, which is exactly when I most need it

Based on these three symptoms, what's the likely diagnosis for each, and what's the specific fix? Be direct — I don't need a general overview of Claude productivity, I need to fix these three things.
```
**Why it works:** The student has already done the diagnostic work — she knows what the symptoms are. Asking for a diagnosis and specific fix per symptom (not a general answer) keeps the response targeted. The instruction to "be direct" and skip the general overview signals she wants a practical answer, which is the prompt behaviour worth modelling.

## Graded deliverable

**Title:** Your Claude integration map

**Brief:** You've now learned the difference between reactive, one-off Claude use and deliberate weekly integration. This deliverable makes that concrete. You're going to look at your actual working week, find three specific tasks where Claude can replace or significantly reduce manual effort, and attempt one of them right now.

This is not a planning exercise. The table you build below is a working document. The task you attempt in step 2 should produce a real output — something you'd actually use or adapt for your work. Paste everything into the **Submission box** at the bottom of this lesson on the Claude Mastery Platform. The AI grading engine returns your grade card within 60 seconds.

**What to submit:**
1. **A table with four columns and three rows** — one row per task. Column headers: Task | Current time | Estimated Claude time | What Claude does. Each task must be named specifically — not "emails" but "writing follow-up emails after client discovery calls." Time estimates should be realistic, not aspirational. The "What Claude does" column should describe the exact step in the task Claude handles, not just "helps me write it."
2. **One attempted task** — pick one of your three tasks, write a prompt using what you've learned in this module, and paste both the prompt and Claude's response. The prompt must use proper structure (context, task, format constraints) — not a quick question.
3. **An 80-word reflection** — identify one specific integration challenge you expect to face (not "it takes time to get used to") and describe concretely how you'll deal with it. Specific friction, specific response.

**Where to submit:** Paste everything into the **Submission box** at the bottom of this lesson on the Claude Mastery Platform. The AI grading engine returns your grade card within 60 seconds.

**Length guide:** Table: 3 rows × 4 columns — a few words to one sentence per cell. Attempted task: prompt (typically 80–200 words) plus Claude's full response (paste without trimming). Reflection: exactly around 80 words — tight is better than loose here.

**Pass mark:** 70 / 100. If you score below 70, the platform shows your grade card with feedback per criterion and activates a **Revise & Resubmit** button after a 24-hour cooldown.

**Rubric (100 points total — pass at 70):**
- **Specificity (25 pts)** — Are all three tasks named concretely, at the level of a specific recurring task rather than a category? "Writing the end-of-week project update email to our four active clients" scores high. "Email" or "client communication" scores low. Do time estimates look realistic for the named task? Does the "What Claude does" column describe a precise step, not a vague role?
- **Structure (25 pts)** — Does the table contain all four columns with content in every cell? Are the three rows genuinely distinct tasks — not variations of the same task type? Is the attempted task in step 2 clearly separate and complete: both prompt and Claude's response included?
- **Constraint clarity (25 pts)** — Does the attempted task prompt use proper structure learned in the module — context, task, format constraints — rather than a short or casual question? Is there at least one specific format constraint (length, tone, structure, or an exclusion)? Does the prompt reflect what the student has learned, or does it look like a first-day attempt?
- **Outcome focus (25 pts)** — Does the reflection name a specific integration challenge — not a vague observation like "it takes time" or "I need to build the habit"? Does the response to that challenge describe a concrete action — specific day, specific system, specific fix — rather than a general intention?

## Notes for the AI grading engine

These notes are read by the grading engine when scoring submissions for this lesson. They are not shown to the student.

- **Reward (high marks):**
  - Table tasks are named at the sub-task level — the grading engine can identify the student's industry, their role, and the specific communication or document type in each row
  - Time estimates in the table are internally consistent — current time is plausibly longer than estimated Claude time, and the estimated Claude time is realistic (not "30 seconds" for a complex report or "3 hours" for a short email)
  - "What Claude does" column describes a step in the process — "drafts the body paragraphs from my bullet notes" rather than "writes the email"
  - The attempted task prompt includes all three structural elements: context (who the student is, their business, the situation), task (a specific verb and output type), and at least one format constraint — and the prompt matches the lesson module's prompt engineering standards
  - The attempted task is a real task from the student's table — not a generic example
  - The reflection names a specific friction point (e.g. "I won't have my meeting notes ready before I sit down to batch prompts on Tuesday") and a specific planned response ("I'll record a 2-minute voice memo immediately after each client call and paste the transcript")

- **Penalise (dock points):**
  - Table rows are categories rather than tasks — "emails," "social media," "admin" without further specification
  - All three table rows are essentially the same task type with minor variation
  - The attempted prompt is a short question without context, task structure, or format constraints — indicates the student has not applied Module 2 learning
  - The attempted task is clearly not from the student's own work — it uses a generic business or a different industry from the one implied in the table
  - The reflection says "it takes time to get used to" or "I just need to make it a habit" without naming a specific obstacle or a specific countermeasure — this is the most common way reflections fail and should be flagged directly
  - Claude's response to the attempted task is trimmed or summarised rather than pasted in full

- **Common 60–69 patterns (near-pass):** The table has three distinct, named tasks but the specificity is moderate — task names are recognisable but not precise enough to know the student's industry without guessing. Time estimates exist but the "What Claude does" column is thin (just "writes a draft"). The attempted prompt has some structure but is missing a format constraint or has a vague task instruction. The reflection identifies a challenge that is real but states the solution in general terms ("I'll make time"). In feedback, quote one table cell and show what a more specific version would contain. Quote the reflection's solution sentence and ask: what day, what time, what exactly will you do differently?

- **Common 80+ patterns (excellent):** The table reads like a real person's week — the grading engine can clearly identify the student's role, their clients or stakeholders, and the exact task type in each row. Time estimates are specific (e.g. "40 minutes" not "a while") and the Claude time estimate shows the student has thought through the workflow, not just halved the original. The attempted prompt is structured, uses real business context, and produces a Claude response that is clearly better than a generic first attempt would have achieved. The reflection is precise: a named friction point (e.g. "I batch on Monday but I often don't have notes ready from Friday meetings") with a named solution (e.g. "I'll write a quick 3-line debrief immediately after each Friday call before I leave the office"). The submission reads as a working document the student will actually use.

- **Feedback tone:** Direct, specific, kind. Quote the student's words — their table entries, their reflection — when identifying wins or gaps. Never give generic feedback. If a table task is a category, quote it back and show exactly what a specific version would say. If the reflection is vague, quote the solution sentence and ask: on what day, at what point in your week, doing what exactly? End with one concrete next step — the single most useful action the student can take in the next 48 hours to begin real integration.

- **Resubmission gating:** Compare against the previous submission explicitly. If the student has made table entries more specific, name the cell that improved and quote the new version. If the reflection now includes a named friction point but the solution is still general, say: "You've identified the right problem now. The solution still needs a specific trigger and action — when exactly, and what exactly will you do?" If the attempted prompt has improved in structure, acknowledge the specific addition (e.g. "you've added a format constraint this time — that's the right move"). If the resubmission is essentially unchanged, say so clearly and name the one thing they still need to fix.
