## Lesson script

### What Claude can and cannot read

Claude can work with a range of file types — but they are not all equal. Understanding the hierarchy saves you a lot of frustration.

**Text files (.txt, .md, .csv)** are the cleanest input. No formatting to interpret, no layout to reconstruct. Claude reads the raw text and gets to work immediately.

**Word documents (.docx)** work well. The text content comes through clearly. Formatting — headers, bold text, tables — is usually preserved or at least interpretable. If you're sharing a contract, a supplier proposal, or a long brief, a Word document is a solid choice.

**PDFs** are common and mostly workable, with one important caveat: it depends entirely on how the PDF was made. A PDF created by exporting from Word or Google Docs contains machine-readable text. Claude reads it directly. A PDF created by scanning a physical page is an image — it contains no text, only pixels. Claude can attempt to read scanned PDFs, but the accuracy drops significantly and can fail entirely on poor scans or handwritten material.

**Spreadsheets (.xlsx, .csv)** are read-only. Claude can read the data, analyse it, summarise it, and extract patterns from it. It cannot write back to your spreadsheet or push data anywhere. If you need Claude to process spreadsheet data, CSV format is the most reliable — export the relevant sheet as CSV before pasting or uploading.

**Scanned images of documents** are the least reliable input. If you have a scanned invoice or a photographed contract, Claude will attempt to read it, but errors increase. Where you can, convert scans to proper text-based formats first. Many scanners and phone apps (like Adobe Scan or Apple's built-in Notes scanner) now offer OCR — optical character recognition — which converts scanned text into machine-readable text. Use that output, not the raw image, when you can.

The format hierarchy in practice: **text > Word/PDF (digital) > scanned image.** When you have a choice, use the format highest on that list.

### The paste versus upload question

Claude gives you two ways to get a document into a conversation: paste the content directly, or upload the file. The choice matters.

**Paste** when the content is short enough to select and copy — typically anything under two or three pages. Pasting is faster, and it keeps the content visible in the conversation thread so you can reference it later. It also works in every interface, including the mobile app.

**Upload** when the document is long or when copying and pasting the content would be cumbersome. PDFs, multi-page Word documents, and large CSV files are all candidates for upload. The upload feature is available on Claude.ai (in the browser and the desktop app) — you'll see a paperclip or attachment icon in the input area.

One thing to know: uploading doesn't give Claude any special powers the paste approach doesn't. Both methods put the text in front of Claude's context window. The difference is convenience and the handling of complex formatting. For a heavily formatted document — one with tables, headers, numbered lists, and footnotes — uploading usually preserves structure better than a raw paste.

**Common mistake → Better approach**

Mistake: Pasting a 40-page contract as raw text. The formatting collapses, tables become unreadable rows of data, and you spend five minutes wondering why the output is chaotic.

Better approach: Upload the PDF or Word version of the contract directly. Claude processes the file, the formatting is preserved, and you get a coherent output. If you only need one section — the termination clause, the payment terms, the indemnity provisions — copy just that section and paste it. You don't need the whole document if you have a specific target.

### Working with long documents

Here's a limitation that trips people up: Claude has a context window. That means there's a ceiling on how much text it can hold in active memory at once. For most everyday documents — contracts up to 30 or 40 pages, reports, proposals — you'll be well within the limit. But for very long documents — a 200-page government tender, a multi-year audit report, a compiled email archive — you may hit the ceiling.

When that happens, you have two practical options.

**Option 1: Reduce the input.** Extract only the sections you need. You don't need to give Claude a 200-page tender if your question is about the evaluation criteria in Section 5 and the pricing schedule in Appendix B. Copy those sections and work with them directly.

**Option 2: Process in passes.** Feed the document to Claude in sections, one at a time. Ask Claude to produce a structured summary of each section. Once you have all the section summaries, feed those into a fresh conversation and ask Claude to synthesise across them. This is more work, but it's reliable for very long documents.

Here's what a pass-by-pass prompt looks like:

```
This is Section 3 of a supplier tender document — Methodology and Approach.
It runs from pages 18 to 34. Please do two things:
1. Summarise the methodology in plain language — no more than 150 words.
2. List any specific commitments the supplier makes in this section — exact
   promises, not vague claims. Format as a bullet list.

I will send you each section separately and then ask you to compare them.

[Section 3 pasted below]
```

The closing line — "I will send you each section separately and then ask you to compare them" — is important. It tells Claude what's coming and orients it toward the larger task. Claude can't hold earlier sections in memory once the conversation grows long, so you're asking it to produce a structured output each time — one that you'll feed back in later.

### Extracting structured data from messy documents

This is one of the most practical things Claude does with documents, and most people never try it.

Messy documents are everywhere in business. An invoice that lists line items across a paragraph of prose instead of a table. A supplier proposal that buries key terms in narrative. A long email thread where the action items are scattered across fifteen messages. A contract where the obligations are mixed in with definitions and whereas clauses.

Claude can extract structured data from all of these — provided you write the right prompt.

There are two reliable extraction patterns.

**Table extraction.** Use this when the document contains records — items, rows, transactions — that should be in a table but aren't. Tell Claude exactly which columns you want.

```
Below is an invoice from a building supplies company. The line items are
described in running prose, not a table. Please extract every line item
and present the results in a table with these five columns:
Item description | Quantity | Unit price (AUD) | Total (AUD) | GST included (Y/N)

If any field is not stated in the invoice, write "not stated" in that cell.
Do not estimate or calculate — only include what is explicitly written.

[Invoice text pasted below]
```

The instruction "do not estimate or calculate — only include what is explicitly written" is doing real work here. Without it, Claude may fill gaps with plausible-looking numbers. That's the wrong behaviour when you're checking financial data.

**Key-field extraction.** Use this when the document has specific terms, dates, parties, or obligations you need to pull out — but the document isn't structured around rows. Contracts are the classic case.

```
Below is a services agreement. I need to extract the following specific fields.
For each field, quote the relevant text from the document exactly, then state the
page or section reference where it appears. If a field is not addressed in the
agreement, write "not found."

Fields to extract:
- Contract start date
- Contract end date or notice period for termination
- Payment terms (when payment is due after invoice)
- Late payment provisions (interest, penalties)
- Intellectual property ownership clause (who owns work created under this agreement)
- Dispute resolution process
- Governing law (which state or jurisdiction applies)

[Agreement pasted below]
```

Asking Claude to quote the relevant text exactly — rather than paraphrase it — is a critical constraint. Paraphrases can drift. An exact quote gives you something you can verify against the original and show to a solicitor if needed.

### Real business use cases

These are the scenarios where this lesson pays off most directly.

**Reading a supplier contract before you sign.** You don't need a solicitor to do a first pass. Paste the contract and ask Claude to extract the key commercial terms (payment, termination, liability, IP) using the key-field extraction pattern above. Then identify anything that looks unusual or missing relative to what you agreed verbally. Take that list to a solicitor for the final review. You show up to that conversation better prepared, which saves time and fees.

**Extracting line items from an invoice.** When a supplier invoice arrives as a PDF or pasted text, use the table extraction prompt to pull it into a structured format. Compare it against your purchase order. Flag discrepancies. This is faster than reading line by line, and you're less likely to miss a duplicate charge or a quantity error.

**Summarising a long report.** Your industry association releases a 60-page industry outlook report. You have 10 minutes. Paste the executive summary and the conclusions section into Claude and ask it to produce: (1) a three-sentence overview, (2) the five most relevant findings for a business of your type and size, (3) any actions or recommendations aimed at businesses like yours. You get what you need without reading the whole document.

**Reviewing a tender or proposal.** A subcontractor sends you a 25-page proposal. You need to understand what they're offering, what they've committed to, and what's vague. Use the section-by-section pass approach from earlier. Or, if it's short enough, upload the whole thing and ask Claude to: (1) summarise what's included in scope, (2) list explicit commitments, (3) list anything described vaguely that you'd need to clarify before signing.

**Processing a long email thread.** Copy and paste the whole email thread — including headers, dates, and names — and ask Claude to produce: a timeline of decisions made, a list of open action items with the person responsible for each, and any commitments one party made to another. This turns a 30-message thread into a usable document in about 30 seconds.

### Accuracy is your responsibility

One more thing before the worked examples.

Claude is very good at extraction. It is not infallible. On a complex, dense, or poorly formatted document, it will occasionally miss a field, misread a number, or overlook a clause buried in a footnote.

This is why every extraction task should include a verification step. After Claude returns the extracted data, spot-check a sample. Pick three line items and confirm them against the original. Pick two contract terms and confirm the quote is verbatim. Pick two dates and confirm they match.

A spot-check takes two minutes. It tells you whether Claude's accuracy on this particular document is high enough to rely on. If you find multiple errors in your sample, go back to Claude with corrections and ask it to review the full extraction again. Most of the time, a spot-check will show the output is clean — and knowing that is worth the two minutes.

Never submit an extraction to a client, forward it to a colleague as a source of truth, or make a financial decision based on it without checking a sample first. Claude extracted it. You verified it. That's the workflow.

## Worked examples

### Example 1: Extracting key terms from a supplier agreement

**Scenario:** A café owner receives a new supplier agreement from a coffee roaster and needs to understand the key commercial terms before signing.

**Prompt:**
```
I run a café in Adelaide. My coffee roaster has sent me a new supply agreement
to sign. Before I sign anything, I need to understand the key commercial terms.

Below is the full agreement. Please extract the following fields. For each one,
quote the relevant text from the document word-for-word, then note which clause
or section it appears in. If a field is not addressed, write "not found."

Fields to extract:
- Minimum order quantities or purchase commitments
- Pricing and any provisions for price increases
- Contract length and how to end the agreement
- Notice period required by either party to terminate
- Exclusivity — am I prevented from buying coffee from other suppliers?
- What happens if they can't supply (force majeure or supply failure clause)
- Governing law (which state)

[Agreement pasted below]
```

**Why it works:** The specific fields map to the business owner's actual concerns — not generic contract terms. The instruction to quote word-for-word prevents paraphrasing drift. Asking for the clause reference means the output is auditable: the owner can go back to the original and check each quote. The "not found" instruction means Claude flags gaps rather than guessing.

### Example 2: Pulling line items from a messy invoice

**Scenario:** A building contractor receives an invoice from a materials supplier where the charges are described in paragraph form rather than an itemised table.

**Prompt:**
```
I run a building contracting business in Brisbane. I've received an invoice from
a materials supplier but the line items are written as a block of text, not a
table. I need to check it against my purchase order.

Please read the invoice below and extract every line item into a table with
these columns:
Item description | Quantity | Unit | Unit price (AUD, ex-GST) | Line total (AUD, ex-GST)

Rules:
- Do not calculate or estimate. Only include figures explicitly stated.
- If quantity or unit price is not stated for an item, write "not stated."
- Include a final row at the bottom: "Invoice total (as stated)" — copy the
  total exactly as it appears in the document.
- If you notice anything that looks like a discrepancy (e.g. quantity × unit
  price does not match the stated line total), note it below the table.

[Invoice pasted below]
```

**Why it works:** The explicit columns define exactly what the output should look like. The no-calculation rule prevents Claude from filling gaps with plausible figures — which would undermine the purpose of checking the invoice. Asking Claude to flag apparent discrepancies adds a quality check that serves the real task: catching billing errors before payment.

### Example 3: Summarising a long industry report

**Scenario:** A financial planning practice receives a 55-page annual industry benchmarking report. The principal needs a briefing before a partner meeting, not a full read.

**Prompt:**
```
I run a financial planning practice in Perth. Our industry association has
released its annual benchmarking report — 55 pages covering revenue, staffing,
technology adoption, client demographics, and fee structures across practices
in Australia.

I've pasted the executive summary and the conclusions chapter below. I need
a briefing before a partner meeting this afternoon. Please produce:

1. A three-sentence overview of the report's main argument or finding.
2. Five findings most relevant to a practice of our size (8 advisers,
   fee-for-service, primarily pre-retiree clients aged 50–65).
3. Any specific recommendations or actions the report directs at practices
   in our segment — quote the recommendation exactly, then translate it
   into plain language.
4. One thing the report says that surprises you or that contradicts common
   assumptions in this industry.

Stick to what the pasted sections say. Do not draw on general knowledge
about financial planning.

[Executive summary and conclusions pasted below]
```

**Why it works:** The practice profile — 8 advisers, fee-for-service, 50–65-year-old clients — filters the findings to the relevant ones. The four-part output is shaped around what the principal needs for the meeting, not what the report offers. "Stick to what the pasted sections say" keeps Claude within the source material. Asking for something that "surprises" Claude draws out a finding the principal might not have flagged as notable — useful for a meeting discussion.

### Example 4: Processing a long email thread

**Scenario:** A project manager at a small construction firm needs to reconstruct a timeline of decisions from a 25-email thread about a subcontractor dispute.

**Prompt:**
```
I manage projects for a construction firm in Melbourne. I have a long email
thread about a subcontractor dispute — approximately 25 emails spanning
six weeks. I need to understand what was agreed, what changed, and where
the disagreement sits now.

The full thread is pasted below, in chronological order with headers
showing sender, recipient, and date. Please produce:

1. A timeline of key decisions or agreements — date, what was decided or
   agreed, and who agreed to it. Bullet point format.
2. A list of commitments made by our firm to the subcontractor — exact
   quote, date of the email, name of the person who made the commitment.
3. A list of commitments made by the subcontractor to us — same format.
4. A one-paragraph summary of where the disagreement currently sits,
   based only on the most recent three emails in the thread.
5. Any requests or questions that were asked but never answered —
   note the date asked and who asked.

Do not interpret intent or assign blame. Stick to what is written.

[Email thread pasted below]
```

**Why it works:** The four sections map to exactly what a project manager needs before a dispute conversation: what was agreed, who said what, where things stand, and what was never answered. The instruction "do not interpret intent or assign blame" keeps the output factual — essential when the output might be used in a formal context. Asking for exact quotes and dates on commitments makes the output directly usable as a chronology.

### Example 5: Section-by-section processing of a long tender

**Scenario:** A consultancy is responding to a government tender and needs to understand the evaluation criteria before writing their response.

**Prompt:**
```
I run a management consultancy in Sydney. We're preparing a response to a
government tender and I need to fully understand the evaluation criteria
before we start writing.

Below is Section 4 of the tender document — Evaluation Criteria and Weighting.
This is one section of a longer document I'll be sending you separately.

Please do the following for this section only:
1. List every evaluation criterion named in this section. For each one,
   state: the criterion label (as written), its weighting (percentage or
   points), and a plain-language description of what assessors will
   be looking for.
2. Note any criterion where the document is vague about what "good"
   looks like — flag it explicitly so I know where to seek clarification
   before we submit.
3. Identify which criterion carries the most weight, and which one is
   most often underestimated by tenderers (based on your understanding
   of government procurement).

Format: numbered list for items 1 and 2, short paragraph for item 3.

I will send you the pricing schedule section next.

[Section 4 pasted below]
```

**Why it works:** The scope instruction — "this section only" — keeps Claude from trying to process a document it doesn't have in full. The final line — "I will send you the pricing schedule section next" — primes Claude for the multi-pass workflow. Asking Claude to flag vague criteria is practical: those are the sections where a pre-submission clarification question to the agency can change the quality of the response significantly.

**Brief:** Bring a real business document — a contract, a supplier proposal, a report, a long email thread, or an invoice — and use Claude to extract something genuinely useful from it. 2. The extraction prompt: exact text, copied from Claude.ai
3. Claude's extracted output: paste the full output, do not summarise or shorten it
4. Verification note: one sentence stating which items you checked manually and what you found — did Claude's output match the source?

**Where to submit:** Paste all four components into the **Submission box** at the bottom of this lesson on the Claude Mastery platform. The AI grading engine returns your grade card within 60 seconds.

**Length guide:** Item 1: one to three sentences. Item 2: your exact prompt, however long it is. Item 3: Claude's full output. Item 4: one sentence — specific about what you checked and what you found.

**Pass mark:** 70 / 100. If you score below 70, the platform shows your grade card with feedback per criterion and activates a **Revise & Resubmit** button after a 24-hour cooldown.

**Rubric (100 points total — pass at 70):**

- **Specificity (25 pts)** — Is the document real and the extraction task genuinely useful to the student's business — not a generic summarisation task? A prompt that says "summarise this article" scores low. A prompt that extracts named fields from an actual contract, or pulls line items from a real invoice, scores high. The document description should make the business context clear.
- **Structure (25 pts)** — Does the prompt specify the exact fields, columns, or items to extract? A prompt that asks Claude to "get the important bits" or "find the key terms" scores low. A prompt that names each field, or each column in a table, scores high. The output format should be explicit — table, bullet list, quoted-field format — not left to Claude's discretion.
- **Constraint clarity (25 pts)** — Does the prompt tell Claude what to do when information is missing or ambiguous — for example, "write not stated" rather than estimating? Does it include at least one explicit constraint on what Claude should or should not do? Absent constraints leave gaps for Claude to fill with plausible-looking guesses — which is the wrong behaviour in a document extraction task.
- **Outcome focus (25 pts)** — Does the verification note show the student actually checked Claude's output against the original document? A note that says "it looked accurate" or "I trust it" scores low. A note that names the specific items checked and states whether they matched — or where they didn't — scores high. The point of this criterion is whether the student treated Claude's output as a draft to verify, not a final answer to accept.

## Notes for the AI grading engine

(Not shown to student)

- **Reward (high marks):**
  - Document is real and specific — the description names the document type, context, and purpose clearly
  - Prompt names exact fields or columns to extract — not vague categories
  - Prompt includes explicit output format (table with named columns, bullet list, quoted-field format)
  - Prompt includes at least one missing-data instruction (e.g. "write not stated," "write not found," "do not estimate")
  - Verification note names specific items checked and reports what was found — including any discrepancy, even a minor one
  - Student chose a task with real business stakes — a contract before signing, an invoice before payment, a tender before a submission deadline

- **Penalise (dock points):**
  - Document appears hypothetical, sample, or online — description lacks specificity or the task has no obvious business stakes
  - Prompt asks for a general summary rather than a targeted extraction — "summarise this contract" instead of extracting named fields
  - No output format specified — Claude is left to choose how to present the data
  - No missing-data constraint — Claude is implicitly allowed to estimate or fill gaps
  - Verification note is absent, vague, or clearly not a genuine check ("it looked fine," "seemed accurate," "I trust Claude")
  - Student pastes only a portion of Claude's output rather than the full response

- **Common 60–69 patterns:**
  - The document is real and the task is relevant, but the prompt asks for "the key commercial terms" without naming which terms — leaving Claude to decide what matters. In feedback: quote the prompt, explain that named fields produce auditable output, and give a two-line example of a field list they could have used for their document type.
  - The prompt specifies fields but omits a missing-data instruction. Claude fills in gaps with plausible text and the student accepts it. In feedback: explain why the no-estimation constraint exists, and suggest adding "if not stated, write 'not found'" to their prompt.
  - Verification note says "I checked it and it was correct" without naming what was checked. In feedback: explain that a useful verification note names three specific items, what was expected, and what Claude returned. Ask the student to do this and resubmit.

- **Common 80+ patterns:**
  - Prompt includes named fields or columns, explicit output format, missing-data instruction, and at least one scope constraint ("based only on what is written — do not infer")
  - Student chose a document with complexity — dense formatting, multiple parties, mixed text and tables — and the prompt anticipates that complexity
  - Verification note is specific: "I checked line items 3, 7, and 11 against the original invoice. Items 3 and 7 matched exactly. Item 11 had the unit price listed as $42.50 in Claude's output but it read $42.80 in the original — a transcription error. I corrected it."
  - Student noted the transcription error and described how they would adjust the prompt or process to catch similar issues in future

- **Feedback tone:** Direct, specific, kind. Quote the student's prompt or verification note when pointing out wins or gaps. If the prompt lacks named fields, show exactly where a field list would go and give two example fields from their document type. If the verification note is vague, explain what a specific verification note looks like and ask for one. End with one concrete next step — either the single prompt addition most likely to improve accuracy on their document type, or a suggestion for another document in their business this workflow could replace a manual process.

- **Resubmission gating:** Compare the revised submission against the original. If they added named fields where there were none, acknowledge that explicitly. If they added a missing-data constraint, name it. If the verification note went from vague to specific, say so. If the revision changes the wording without changing the structure — still no field list, still no format instruction — say so clearly and name the one remaining gap. Do not pass a resubmission that has the same structural gaps as the original.
