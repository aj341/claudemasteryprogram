## Lesson script

### This is not another lesson. It's an accounting.

You've spent five modules building something. Not a skill in isolation — a stack. A coherent set of capabilities that compound on each other. Before you move into Module 6, it's worth stopping to name exactly what that stack contains and how it connects.

This lesson is not about new mechanics. There's nothing to install or configure. It's about taking stock of what you have built so you can use it deliberately — rather than reaching for whatever tool you happen to remember when a task arrives.

Most people who study something seriously never do this step. They absorb information, complete the exercises, pass the assessments. But they don't stop to map what they know into a usable reference that reflects their actual behaviour. This lesson is that map.

### What you now have — the full stack

Let's name it plainly.

**Module 1 — Claude Essentials.** You started with the fundamentals: what Claude is and isn't, how to write a prompt that gives Claude something to work with, how to audit your existing AI habits, and how to load your business context into a conversation. That foundation is what made every subsequent lesson possible. Without a clear sense of your context, your industry, and the specific tasks you need help with, even the best prompt techniques produce generic outputs.

**Module 2 — Prompt Engineering.** You built the craft of writing effective prompts. Anatomy, context, clarity, templates, advanced techniques, iteration. You learned the difference between a vague instruction ("write me an email") and a structured brief that constrains output, specifies format, names the audience, and sets the right tone. You now write prompts that other people don't. That gap matters more than most people realise.

**Module 3 — Practical Applications.** You applied prompting skills to real task categories: writing and communication, research and synthesis, decision support, learning on demand, and workflow integration. The capstone asked you to document a real Claude week — five actual tasks, real prompts, real outputs. That was not an exercise. That was your working pattern, documented.

**Module 4 — Claude Projects.** You built infrastructure. Not just prompts, but a persistent environment: Projects with knowledge bases, calibrated instructions, and clear domain boundaries. You moved from starting every conversation from scratch to arriving in a space where Claude already knows your business. The four-element architecture — name, purpose, knowledge base, when to use — gave you a system, not a collection of tools.

**Module 5 — Extended Capabilities.** You added capabilities on top of that infrastructure. Vision: sending Claude images, screenshots, and scanned documents to extract, interpret, and analyse visual information. Files: uploading real business documents — contracts, invoices, reports, spreadsheets — and getting Claude to work through them structuredly. Automation: understanding how the Claude API and no-code tools connect Claude to triggers, so work happens without you manually initiating every session.

That is your stack. Five modules, each one building on the one before it. You are not a beginner.

### Your Claude Stack audit

Knowing what the stack contains is different from knowing how you actually use it. The audit is honest about that gap.

Most people who complete five modules of a course end up with a distribution that looks roughly like this: some things they use every week without thinking about it, some things they tried once and set aside, and some things they completed the lesson on but haven't applied yet. All three are normal. The problem is treating them as equivalent — listing everything as "things I know how to do" without distinguishing between active use and theoretical knowledge.

The audit has three categories:

**Active.** You use this regularly. It's part of your working week. You'd notice if it disappeared. You could run it without re-reading the lesson.

**Tested, not adopted.** You ran through the lesson, completed the exercise, got a useful output — but it hasn't become a regular part of your workflow. Maybe the trigger wasn't obvious. Maybe the setup felt like overhead. Maybe you planned to return to it and didn't. It's not a failure. It's a flag.

**Not yet tried.** You completed the lesson, passed the assessment, but haven't applied it to real work. This is the category most people underestimate. Knowing the concept is not the same as having the capability.

The deliverable for this lesson asks you to populate all three columns honestly. A submission that puts everything in the "active" column is almost certainly inaccurate. The grading engine is not rewarding completion — it's rewarding honest self-assessment.

Here's why the audit matters beyond self-knowledge: it tells you where your highest-opportunity gap is. The things in the "tested, not adopted" column are the ones closest to your reach. You already understand them. The barrier is usually a trigger — a specific task or moment in your week where you could apply the tool but haven't built the habit yet. Once you identify those, one targeted change in your workflow closes the gap without any additional learning.

### The opportunity cost of complexity

Here is something the course has not said directly but which becomes visible at this stage: more capability is not always the right answer.

You have vision, file processing, and automation tools available to you. That does not mean every task should use all of them. It means you now have the judgment to know when they're worth the overhead — and when a well-structured plain-text prompt does the job faster.

The clearest way to think about it is this: a task that takes you two minutes to brief Claude on with a plain prompt and returns a useful output in thirty seconds does not benefit from being wrapped in an automation. The automation would take longer to build, test, and maintain than the manual sessions it replaces over a month. For a task that runs three hundred times a year, the automation pays off. For a task that runs four times a year, it doesn't.

The same logic applies to file uploads versus pasting text. Uploading a PDF gives Claude access to the full document — but if the relevant section is two paragraphs you could copy and paste in ten seconds, uploading adds friction without adding value. Use the file upload when the document is too long to paste, when you need Claude to navigate between sections, or when the document will be used across multiple sessions in a Project. Don't use it as a reflex.

**Common mistake → Better approach**

Mistake: Reaching for the most sophisticated tool available by default. You have automation set up, so you route tasks through the automation even when a quick manual prompt would be faster. You upload documents as a habit rather than as a choice. You add complexity because you built it, not because the task calls for it.

```
# Checking your tool choice before you start
Is this task manual or recurring?
  Recurring more than weekly → consider automation
  Manual or occasional → plain prompt is almost always faster

Is this document long or short?
  Long (can't paste cleanly) → upload or paste key sections
  Short (two paragraphs) → paste directly, don't upload
```

Better approach: Before you decide how to run a Claude task, ask one question: what is the simplest approach that produces a useful output? Start there. Add complexity only when the simple approach falls short — not before. This is not about being conservative with the tools you've built. It's about using them with judgment instead of using them automatically. The tools are most powerful when they're chosen for a reason.

### What Module 6 is — and why this moment matters

Module 6 is the capstone module. It is different in structure from Modules 1–5.

In Modules 1–5, the curriculum determined what you worked on. Each lesson taught a specific capability, gave you examples, and asked you to practise it in a bounded way. The skills were defined. The tasks were guided.

Module 6 is self-directed. You bring the project. You define the scope. You apply whichever tools from your stack are the right ones for the job — not because a lesson told you to, but because you can now evaluate which tool fits which task. The result you're submitting in Module 6 is not a lesson exercise. It's a real piece of work produced for your actual business using the full range of what you've built.

That's why this capstone matters. It's not an end point — it's a bridge. You're mapping your stack now so that when Module 6 asks you to select your tools deliberately, you know exactly what's in your toolkit, what you reliably use, and where your gaps are.

There's something else worth naming. By the time you complete this lesson, you will have built more structured Claude capability than most small business owners in Australia and New Zealand have. Not because the course is exceptional — because almost no one does this work. They try a tool, get inconsistent results, and conclude that AI is either not useful or too complicated. You now know why the results are inconsistent: no context, no structure, no persistent system. You've built the alternative.

That doesn't mean the work is finished. Module 6 will surface gaps. There will be tasks you attempt in your real project where your current stack doesn't have a clean answer. That's expected. The difference between where you started in Lesson 1.1 and where you stand now is not that you know everything — it's that you know how to approach what you don't know yet. You have the prompt craft, the Projects infrastructure, and the capability layer. You know how to interrogate a task and match it to the right tool. That is a durable skill, not a fixed body of knowledge.

One prompt, written well, with the right context loaded and the right tool applied — that's the whole thing. Five modules to understand it. Module 6 to prove it in your own work.

## Worked examples

### Example 1: Claude Stack audit — service business

**Scenario:** A freelance HR consultant audits her Claude Stack three months into active use.

**Prompt:**
```
I've completed Modules 1–5 of the Claude Mastery Program and I want to
do an honest audit of my Claude Stack before starting Module 6.

Here's my current picture:

Active (I use this regularly):
- Writing client-facing documents using my Client Comms Project
- Drafting difficult emails with context-loaded prompts
- Research briefs before stakeholder meetings

Tested but not adopted:
- File uploads for contract review — tried it once, it worked, but I
  haven't made it a habit. I still read contracts manually first.
- Vision prompts for screenshotting competitor materials

Not yet tried:
- Any automation — I completed Lesson 5.3 but haven't connected
  Claude to Make or Zapier yet

Given this picture, what is my highest-opportunity gap — the one thing
I could add to my active stack with the least friction, based on what
I've already tested? Give me a specific, actionable first step for that
one gap only. Don't give me a plan for all three categories.
```

**Why it works:** The prompt structures the audit into all three categories before asking a question, which means Claude has real data to work with rather than an abstract question. Asking for the highest-opportunity gap — singular, not a prioritised list — keeps the output focused and actionable. The instruction "don't give me a plan for all three categories" prevents Claude from producing an overwhelming response that's hard to act on.

### Example 2: Multi-capability showcase task — contract plus comparison

**Scenario:** A property manager uploads a supplier contract and combines file processing with decision support to evaluate whether to renew.

**Prompt:**
```
I manage a portfolio of 24 rental properties in Christchurch. I'm deciding
whether to renew with my current property maintenance supplier when our
contract expires in 6 weeks.

I'm uploading our current contract. After reading it, I need you to do
two things:

1. Extract the six most commercially important terms — rates, notice
   periods, exclusivity clauses, liability caps, payment terms, and
   renewal conditions. Present these as a table with three columns:
   term name, current contract language (verbatim), and plain-English
   translation.

2. Based on those terms and the following context about our experience
   with this supplier, give me three reasons to renew and three reasons
   not to — then a recommendation. I am the decision-maker. I want your
   analysis, not a neutral summary.

Supplier context: Response times have been consistently under 4 hours
(which is excellent). Pricing is 8% above the market average we got from
two competing quotes last month. They've never missed a job or created a
liability issue in 3 years. The relationship with their lead technician
is strong and he knows all 24 properties well.

[Attach: maintenance-supplier-contract-2024.pdf]
```

**Why it works:** This prompt uses two capability tiers intentionally: file processing (extract terms from the contract) and decision support (evaluate the renewal using both the contract terms and the supplied context). The output structure is explicit — table with three named columns for the extraction, then a for-and-against with a recommendation for the decision support. Asking for a recommendation rather than a neutral analysis ensures the output is decision-ready rather than research-level.

### Example 3: Vision plus Project — analysing a competitor's marketing

**Scenario:** A café owner uses her Marketing Project and a screenshot to benchmark her own Instagram post quality.

**Prompt:**
```
[Opening the Marketing Content Project, which contains brand voice guide,
target audience description, and past top-performing post examples]

I'm attaching a screenshot of a competitor café's Instagram post. They're
a specialty café in Newtown, Sydney — similar positioning to us (quality
coffee, community feel, no chains).

Looking at the screenshot:
1. What is the copy structure — hook, body, call to action? Quote the
   actual text from the image.
2. What engagement signals can you see — likes, comments, shares visible
   in the screenshot?
3. Compare the post structure to the tone guide and post examples in this
   Project. What is this competitor doing that we're not? Be specific —
   name the technique, not just the effect.

I want one concrete thing I could test in our next three posts based on
this comparison.

[Attach: competitor-post-screenshot.png]
```

**Why it works:** Opening this task in a Project means Claude has brand voice and past performance context before reading the screenshot — it's a genuine comparison, not an isolated analysis. The three-part output structure keeps the vision analysis and the comparison analysis separate, which makes the output easier to act on. Asking for one concrete test rather than a list of improvements ensures the response ends in a decision, not a catalogue.

### Example 4: Automation assessment — when not to automate

**Scenario:** A bookkeeper evaluates whether a weekly client report should be automated.

**Prompt:**
```
I send a weekly bookkeeping summary to each of my 9 clients every Friday
afternoon. Each summary is based on that client's transaction data from
the week — different figures, different clients, different commentary.

I've been considering automating this using Make and Claude's API, based
on what I learned in Lesson 5.3. Before I invest the setup time, help me
evaluate whether this is actually worth automating.

Current state:
- Each report takes me 15–20 minutes to write from scratch
- I write 9 reports every Friday, so roughly 2.5–3 hours total
- Reports have a consistent structure: 3 financial highlights, 1 issue to
  watch, next week's focus area
- Client data lives in Xero — I currently export a PDF and look at it
  manually

Automation requirements if I go ahead:
- Would need a Xero integration to pull data automatically
- Claude would need to generate commentary, not just format numbers
- Would need review step before sending — I can't send unreviewed financials

Given the time savings, the setup complexity, and the review requirement,
should I automate this, partially automate it, or keep it manual? Give me
a clear recommendation with the reasoning.
```

**Why it works:** This prompt demonstrates the lesson's core judgment point — evaluating whether automation is worth the overhead before building it. The student has quantified the current time cost, identified the integration requirements, and flagged the non-negotiable constraint (review before sending). Asking for a clear recommendation rather than a pros-and-cons list forces an honest output. The answer may well be "partially automate" — which is itself a sophisticated response that requires understanding where Claude's value is real.

### Example 5: Reflection framing — before-and-after at the module boundary

**Scenario:** A retail shop owner uses Claude to structure her Module 5 capstone reflection before writing it herself.

**Prompt:**
```
I'm writing a 150-word reflection for my Module 5 capstone. The prompt
asks me to describe how my relationship with Claude has changed from
Lesson 1.1 to now.

I don't want you to write the reflection for me — I want to write it
myself. But I'm finding it hard to identify the specific before-and-after
differences.

Here's what I can remember about Lesson 1.1: I wrote my first prompt,
which was basically just asking Claude a question. It was vague. Claude
gave me something generic. I had to ask three follow-up questions to get
anything useful.

Here's what I do now: I have a Client Comms Project and an Operations
Project. I upload supplier quotes as PDFs and get structured comparisons.
I use a saved prompt template for my weekly staff update.

Give me five specific "before vs. after" statements based on what I've
described. Use plain language. Don't be motivational. Just name the
actual differences so I can choose which two or three to build the
reflection around.
```

**Why it works:** The student has correctly identified that they don't want Claude to write the reflection — that would undermine the grading exercise, which is evaluating their own self-assessment. Using Claude to surface contrast points they can then select from is a legitimate use of the capability that still keeps the authorship with the student. The instruction "don't be motivational" prevents Claude from returning a list of encouragements rather than a list of functional differences.

## Graded deliverable

**Title:** Your Claude Stack

**Brief:** This is the Module 5 capstone — and the last major submission before Module 6, where you apply your full stack to a self-directed business project. The purpose of this deliverable is to produce a one-page reference that accurately maps how you use Claude across all five modules. Not how you plan to use it. Not the ideal version. The actual current state.

Three components make up the submission. First, your Claude Stack map: four sections covering your prompting approach, your practical applications, your Project system, and which extended capabilities you actively use. Second, one showcase task you complete now — not a historical example, but a task you run specifically for this submission, using at least two different capability tiers from your stack in combination. Third, a 150-word reflection on how your working relationship with Claude has changed between Lesson 1.1 and today. Paste all three components into the **Submission box** at the bottom of this lesson on the Claude Mastery platform. The AI grading engine returns your grade card within 60 seconds.

**What to submit:**

1. **Your Claude Stack map** — four labelled sections:
   - **Prompting approach** — How do you write prompts now? Describe your approach in 3–5 sentences. Name specific techniques from Module 2 that you apply consistently (context-first, format instructions, constraints, templates). Don't describe what the techniques are — describe how you personally apply them in your work.
   - **Practical applications** — Your top 3 Claude uses from your working week. For each: the task name, how often you run it, and a one-sentence description of what the output replaces or saves.
   - **Project system** — List the Projects you have active. For each Project: its name and one sentence on its purpose. If you have none active, name the one Project you would build if you started today and why.
   - **Extended capabilities** — Which of the Module 5 capabilities (vision, file processing, automation) do you actively use? For each one you actively use: name the task it applies to and what it produces. For capabilities you haven't adopted, name one specific task in your business where you could apply it.

2. **One showcase task** — A single task completed now, using at least two capability tiers from your stack working together. Submit: (a) the prompt(s) you sent, in full as typed; (b) Claude's response(s), in full or summarised if very long; (c) a one-paragraph note identifying which capability tiers you used and why you combined them for this task rather than using one alone.

3. **150-word reflection** — How has your relationship with Claude changed from Lesson 1.1 to now? Name specific before-and-after differences — concrete skills or habits that are different. The reflection should answer: what do you do differently now, and what does that difference produce in your work? 140–160 words.

**Where to submit:** Paste everything into the **Submission box** at the bottom of this lesson on the Claude Mastery platform. The AI grading engine returns your grade card within 60 seconds.

**Length guide:** Claude Stack map: 200–400 words total across the four sections. Showcase task: as long as the prompt(s) and response(s) require — do not truncate the prompt. Reflection: 140–160 words strictly.

**Pass mark:** 70 / 100. If you score below 70, the platform shows your grade card with feedback per criterion and activates a **Revise & Resubmit** button after a 24-hour cooldown.

**Rubric (100 points total — pass at 70):**

- **Specificity (25 pts)** — Is the Claude Stack map populated with real, current usage — not aspirational descriptions of what the student might use someday? Each section should name specific tasks, specific Projects, specific prompt techniques they apply today. Phrases like "I plan to use file uploads for contracts" score in the 6–10 range for this section. Phrases like "I upload supplier quotes as PDFs every week and use extraction prompts to pull price, lead time, and terms into a comparison table" score 20+. Specificity is verifiable: a specific claim describes a task with enough detail that a reader could picture the student doing it.

- **Structure (25 pts)** — Does the stack map cover all four sections with concrete, named examples? Does the showcase task include all three required components: prompt(s) in full, response(s), and a capability-tier note? Is the reflection present and within the word count? Missing sections cost points proportionally. A submission with three strong sections and one section missing scores no higher than 18/25 on this criterion regardless of the quality of the other three.

- **Constraint clarity (25 pts)** — Does the showcase task demonstrably use two or more capability tiers — and is the multi-tier use intentional rather than coincidental? The capability-tier note is the key evidence here: the student should be able to name which tiers they used and explain why combining them produced a better result than using one alone. A task that uses a Project and a file upload but doesn't explain why they were combined scores in the 12–16 range. A task where the student articulates the connection — "the Project provides the context Claude needs to evaluate the contract terms against our known business situation" — scores 20+.

- **Outcome focus (25 pts)** — Does the reflection name specific before-and-after differences — concrete skills or habits, not vague improvements in confidence? The reflection should answer two questions: what do I do differently now, and what does that difference produce in my work? A reflection that says "I'm more confident using Claude and it feels more natural" scores in the 6–10 range. A reflection that says "In Lesson 1.1 I sent a single-sentence question and rewrote it four times to get something useful. Now I open my Client Comms Project, write a structured brief in two minutes, and send the first draft with minor edits" scores 20+.

## Notes for the AI grading engine

(Not shown to student)

- **Reward (high marks):**
  - Claude Stack map sections are populated with named, specific tasks — the grader can picture the student's working week from the description, not just their intentions
  - All four sections are present and distinct — prompting approach is not the same content as practical applications, Project system names real active Projects rather than aspirational ones
  - Showcase task shows a clear and intentional multi-tier combination — the capability-tier note explains the logic of why two tiers were used together, not just lists them
  - Reflection names specific before-and-after states at the task or habit level — "I used to do X, now I do Y, which produces Z" — rather than reporting a general improvement in fluency or confidence
  - Honest audit present: the student's stack map acknowledges capabilities they haven't fully adopted alongside ones they use actively — this honesty is a signal of genuine self-assessment rather than a completed-course summary

- **Penalise (dock points):**
  - Any Claude Stack section that describes what a capability is rather than how the student uses it — this indicates the student summarised the lesson rather than audited their own behaviour
  - A showcase task where the multi-tier combination is coincidental or unexplained — two tiers present but no rationale for combining them; the task could have been done with one tier
  - A prompt submitted as a paraphrase rather than the exact text sent to Claude — the grading engine cannot evaluate prompt quality without seeing the actual prompt
  - A reflection composed entirely of positive statements with no named before-state — "I use Claude much more confidently now" with no reference to what using Claude looked like at the start
  - A Project system section that lists Projects by name with no purpose statement — names without purpose descriptions cannot be evaluated for whether the system is meaningfully structured

- **Common 60–69 patterns:**
  The student has done the work — all four sections present, a real showcase task, a written reflection — but the specificity is thin. The Claude Stack map describes capabilities in general terms ("I use file uploads for documents") without naming the specific tasks, formats, or frequency. The showcase task includes the prompt and response but the capability-tier note says only "I used my Project and a file upload" without explaining why that combination was the right choice for this task. The reflection notes improvement without naming a specific starting point. In feedback, quote one general statement from the stack map and ask: "Can you name the specific task this refers to, and how often you run it?" Then quote the capability-tier note and ask: "What would the output have missed if you'd only used the Project without the file upload?" Those two questions are the concrete next steps.

- **Common 80+ patterns:**
  The stack map reads like a working document the student would actually refer to — specific tasks, named Projects, frequencies, clear descriptions of what each capability replaces. The showcase task makes the multi-tier combination feel necessary rather than incidental — the grader can see why combining the tiers produced something one tier alone couldn't. The reflection has a specific before-state ("In Lesson 1.1 I...") and a specific after-state ("Now I...") and names what changed in between. The student may have included an honest note about a capability they haven't yet adopted — this is a strong signal. Acknowledge the specific showcase task choice and explain what made the multi-tier combination effective. End with one concrete observation about what this student's stack is set up to do well in Module 6, based on what they've built and described.

- **Feedback tone:** Direct, specific, kind. Quote the student's words — their stack map descriptions, their capability-tier note, their reflection language. Never respond to a general pattern without reference to what the student actually wrote. Completing five modules is a meaningful milestone and deserves acknowledgment — but name what specifically they've built, not just that they've built it. End with one concrete next step or forward observation about Module 6 that connects to their actual stack.

- **Resubmission gating:** Compare the resubmission against the original on three dimensions: (1) did any general descriptions in the stack map become specific — named tasks, named frequencies, named outputs? (2) did the capability-tier note in the showcase task gain a rationale — an explanation of why the combination was necessary? (3) did the reflection gain a before-state that anchors the comparison? If all three improved, name each change. If one or two improved, acknowledge the progress and name the remaining gap precisely. If the resubmission is essentially the same document with added words but no added specificity, say so directly and name the exact sentence that still needs to become concrete.

## Industry personalisation hooks

The four-section Claude Stack structure adapts naturally across AU/NZ small business industries without changing the format. For trades and construction businesses, the prompting approach section typically highlights job quoting and subcontractor communication, while the extended capabilities section most commonly involves file processing for supplier invoices and vision for site photos used in scope documentation. For health and allied health practices, the Project system section commonly describes a patient-comms Project and a compliance or administration Project, and the showcase task often involves combining file uploads with decision support for clinical admin decisions. For retail and hospitality, automation is the most common gap in the "tested not adopted" column — students have the knowledge from Lesson 5.3 but haven't yet connected Claude to a booking or inventory trigger. The reflection component is universally applicable: the before-and-after comparison between Lesson 1.1 and Module 5 completion is the same exercise regardless of industry, and the specificity criterion is calibrated to the same standard across all sectors.
