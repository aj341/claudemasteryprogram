## Lesson script

### Why "I should use AI more" doesn't work

Most accountants and bookkeepers who've used Claude a few times fall into the same pattern. They use it when they remember to, for whatever task is in front of them at that moment, without any real system. A few weeks in, they've had some wins and some misses, and they can't quite explain why.

The problem isn't the tool. The problem is the approach. "Using AI more" is not a strategy. It's an intention, and intentions don't survive a busy lodgement week.

The fix is simple: know exactly which tasks you want Claude to handle. Not in theory — in your actual practice work, in the specific tasks you do every week. When you have that list, using Claude becomes a decision you've already made, not a decision you have to make fresh each time.

This lesson is where you build that list, {{firstName}}. You'll identify your recurring tasks at {{businessName}}, run them through a simple framework to see which ones are good candidates for Claude, and leave with a concrete task map — something you'll actually use, not something you file away and forget.

There's a second reason this matters. From Lesson 1.5 onward, this course personalises examples and exercises to your actual work. The task map you build here is how the platform knows what your work looks like. The more specific you are in this lesson, the more relevant every subsequent lesson becomes.

### A 2×2 framework for categorising your tasks

Not every task is equally suited to Claude. The useful question isn't "can Claude help with this?" — it usually can, in some way. The useful question is "is this one of the tasks where Claude saves me meaningful time or improves the quality of what I produce?"

A simple framework helps answer that. Plot your tasks on two axes: how often you do them, and how much skill or judgment they require from you.

**Axis 1 — Frequency:** Is this task something you do every week, or something you do occasionally — once a month, once a quarter, when a particular client situation comes up?

**Axis 2 — Skill level:** Does this task require significant professional judgment, deep knowledge of a client's tax history, or are the parameters fairly fixed and the main challenge is time and effort?

This gives you four quadrants:

**High-frequency, lower-skill tasks.** These are your quick wins. You do them often, they're fairly routine, and the main cost is time. BAS reminder emails, formatting working-paper notes, drafting standard responses to common client queries, filling in engagement-letter templates — Claude can handle these well, and the cumulative time saving across a week adds up faster than you'd expect. Start here.

**High-frequency, higher-skill tasks.** These are your highest-value opportunities. You do them constantly, and they're the tasks that actually require your expertise — writing fee proposals, producing analysis for a year-end client letter, drafting tax-position memos, creating client-facing content that has to sound like you. These are harder to hand to Claude wholesale, but used as a co-writer or thinking partner, Claude can shorten a 2-hour task to 45 minutes. This quadrant is where most accountants find the biggest long-run gains.

**Low-frequency, lower-skill tasks.** These tasks come up occasionally and don't take much judgment. Use Claude when they arise — there's no urgency to build a system around them, but it's worth knowing you have the option. Meeting agendas for an FY-planning workshop, expense summaries for a one-off project, onboarding checklists for a new client.

**Low-frequency, higher-skill tasks.** These require your professional judgment, but they don't happen often enough to be your focus. Use Claude for research and early drafting — it can get you 60% of the way there — but these tasks need more careful human review than the others. Submissions or voluntary disclosures to the ATO, complex Div 7A or trust resettlement queries, anything with significant compliance implications.

You don't need a perfect categorisation. The goal is to quickly sort your tasks so you know where to direct your attention in the next four lessons.

### What makes a task "Claude-suitable"

Before you build your list, here are four signals that a task is a good fit for Claude.

**The input and output are both text.** Claude reads text and writes text. If a task involves reading something and producing something written, Claude can help. If it involves a live ATO phone call, a face-to-face client meeting, or a real-time ledger reconciliation inside Xero, Claude is the wrong tool.

**The task benefits from drafting or summarising.** Writing a first draft, summarising a long document, extracting key information from a report — these are Claude's natural habitat. If a task is currently costing you time because you're staring at a blank page or wading through long working papers, Claude is likely to help.

**The task doesn't depend on information Claude doesn't have.** Real-time data — today's ATO determinations, current super contribution caps that may have changed last week, this morning's news — is outside Claude's reach unless you're using a version with web access enabled. Anything requiring information that's private to your practice (client ledgers, internal practice management data) needs to come from you, pasted in or described in the prompt.

**Professional sign-off is still possible.** Claude producing a draft doesn't mean the draft goes out. For tasks where accuracy matters — a client-facing document, a tax return, a formal letter of advice — Claude helps you produce the first version faster, and you review it before it leaves your desk. That's fine. Where it doesn't work is when the task requires a registered tax agent's judgment and there's no review step in the process. That's a boundary, not a failing of the tool.

### Identifying quick wins versus strategic uses

When you look at your task list, you'll probably notice two types of opportunity.

Quick wins are tasks you can try with Claude today, with a basic prompt and minimal preparation. These tasks are clearly text-based, the parameters are well-defined, and the output doesn't carry high stakes if it needs a revision or two. A BAS reminder email, a meeting summary from a client review, a draft FAQ for your practice website, a first pass at a junior bookkeeper job ad. These are the tasks to try in this week's practice.

Strategic uses take a bit more setup. They're often your most important recurring tasks — the ones where the quality of the output really matters. To use Claude well on these, you need a good prompt, probably a few passes to refine the output, and a clear review step. But once you've figured out the prompt that works for, say, your end-of-FY client letter, you have a template you can reuse every June. The upfront time pays back quickly.

The mistake people make is trying to start with their hardest, most high-stakes task — because that's where the potential upside is biggest. But the first time you use Claude for something that really matters, you haven't built the skill yet to get the best out of it. Start with a quick win. Build confidence in the tool and in your prompting. Then bring Claude into the work that matters most.

Here is what that looks like in practice. Before you try to use Claude to draft your tax-planning memo for a top-10 client, try it for the summary email you send a small business client after each quarterly review meeting. Same writing skill required. Lower stakes. You'll learn what works for your style, your context, and your client base — and you'll be ready to tackle the planning memo with a prompt that actually produces something useful.

```
Before you build your task map, run this quick check prompt in Claude:

"I run a [type of practice — accounting, bookkeeping, financial planning,
mortgage broking, tax agent] and I'm mapping the tasks I want to use Claude
for. Here are five tasks I do regularly: [list them]. For each one, tell me:
1. Whether it's a strong, moderate, or weak candidate for Claude
2. The main thing that would make it work or not work in an AU practice

Give me a clear answer for each — no hedging."
```

This check takes under two minutes. It often surfaces considerations you hadn't thought of — and it's a useful first exercise in being specific about your work.

### Common mistake → better approach

**Mistake:** Listing tasks at too high a level. "Client communication" is on the list. "Report writing" is on the list. These entries look like tasks, but they're actually categories. When you sit down to use Claude for "client communication," you still have to decide which client, which communication, what it should say, and what format it should take. The high-level entry doesn't help you act.

**Better approach:** Write each task as you'd write it in a practice management tool — specific enough that someone reading the list could start on the task without asking a clarifying question. "Write the weekly BAS-due reminder email to our 35 monthly-BAS clients" is a task. "Draft a holding response for prospects who enquire about fees before we've sent the engagement letter" is a task. "Summarise the meeting notes from our fortnightly partner ops review into action points for the team" is a task.

When your task map is specific at this level, it becomes genuinely useful. You can look at it on a Monday morning and decide which of your Claude tasks to work on first. Vague entries on a list produce no action. Specific ones produce exactly the prompt you need to write.

## Graded deliverable

**Title:** Your task map

**Brief:** Document your actual recurring work, categorise it using the frequency &times; skill framework, and identify where to start with Claude. Don't write hypothetical tasks — write the tasks you actually do, in the words you'd use to describe them to a colleague. The specificity you put in here shapes how useful the rest of this course is to you.

**What to submit:**

1. **Your task list (10–15 tasks)** — List 10–15 recurring tasks from your work. For each task, note: specific description · frequency (weekly/fortnightly/monthly) · quadrant (HF-LS / HF-HS / LF-LS / LF-HS). One per line works well.

2. **Your top 3 Claude opportunities** — The three tasks from your list you'll try with Claude first. For each, a one-sentence rationale — what makes it Claude-suitable (text-based, benefits from drafting, clear parameters). Then close the section with two short notes that apply across all three: (a) the voice or tone Claude needs to write in to sound like you / your business when it produces this work — describe how your brand sounds when it speaks; and (b) the goal you'd count as a real win across these three tasks once you've worked the rest of the course — what success looks like for you by the end.

3. **One task you're explicitly ruling out** — A task you've decided Claude is not right for. 2–3 sentences on why: real-time data dependency? need for professional sign-off without review? physical action required?

**Where to submit:** Paste your submission into the Submission box at the bottom of this lesson on the Claude Mastery platform. The AI grading engine returns your grade card within 60 seconds.

**Pass mark:** 70 / 100. If you score below 70, the platform shows your grade card with feedback per criterion and activates a **Revise & Resubmit** button after a 24-hour cooldown.

**Rubric (100 points total — pass at 70):**
- **Specificity (25 pts)** — Tasks are written with enough detail that the grader can identify your industry and role. Generic entries like "emails" or "reports" score low. "Draft the monthly performance summary I send to our 6 franchise partners" scores high.
- **Structure (25 pts)** — All three components are present and clearly labelled: full task list with frequency and quadrant notes, top 3 opportunities with rationale, and the ruled-out task with explanation. 10–15 items in the list.
- **Constraint clarity (25 pts)** — The ruled-out task names a specific reason Claude is unsuitable (real-time data, sign-off without review, physical action). Top 3 rationales name specific Claude-suitability criteria — not just "it takes a lot of time."
- **Outcome focus (25 pts)** — The map is grounded in your real work. Top 3 are genuinely the best starting points — not the easiest or most impressive. The ruled-out task shows you understand Claude's limits in your specific context, not just in general.

## Notes for the AI grading engine

These notes are read by the grading engine when scoring submissions for this lesson. They are not shown to the student.

- **Reward (high marks):**
  - Task list entries are specific enough that the grader can picture the student's actual job — role, industry, and type of work are all visible from reading the list
  - Each task has a frequency note and a quadrant assignment — the student has done the categorisation work, not just listed tasks
  - Top 3 opportunity rationales reference specific Claude-suitability criteria from the lesson (text-based, benefits from drafting, clear parameters, no real-time data required) rather than vague positives ("it takes a long time" or "Claude would be good at this")
  - The ruled-out task explanation names a precise reason tied to the student's specific context — not a generic "Claude isn't reliable enough"
  - Task variety is evident — not 10 variations of the same type of task

- **Penalise (dock points):**
  - Task list has fewer than 10 items, or items are category labels rather than named tasks ("writing" instead of "write the weekly project update to our clients")
  - No frequency or quadrant notes — the student has listed tasks but not categorised them
  - Top 3 rationales are generic ("this is text-based" applied to all three) or repeat the same reason across all three opportunities
  - Ruled-out task is absent, or the reason given is vague ("Claude might get it wrong") without a specific explanation tied to the task or the student's context
  - Task list appears hypothetical or generic — tasks that could belong to any small business in any industry, with no visible industry or role context

- **Common 60–69 patterns (near-pass):** The student submits all three components but task descriptions remain vague — "write emails to clients" rather than "write the weekly check-in email to our 5 active project clients." Frequency is noted but quadrant assignments are missing or generic. The top 3 rationale says something like "this one takes a lot of time" without connecting to a specific Claude-suitability criterion. The ruled-out task explanation is present but thin — one sentence where two or three were needed. In feedback, quote one of their vague task descriptions back to them and show them what a specific version would look like. Ask: who is this email to? How often? What does it typically cover?

- **Common 80+ patterns (excellent):** The task list reads like a real person's working week — you can tell the student's industry, their role, and the type of clients or colleagues they work with. Every task has a frequency and a quadrant. The top 3 rationales are different from each other and each names a distinct reason why that task suits Claude. The ruled-out task explanation is thoughtful — it addresses the specific reason that task is unsuitable and shows the student understands what that means for their workflow. The submission as a whole feels like a genuine working document the student will refer back to.

- **Feedback tone:** Direct, specific, and kind. Quote the student's own task descriptions when identifying wins or fix points — never generic. If task entries are vague, quote one back and show exactly what a more specific version would say. Always end with one concrete next step: identify which of their top 3 Claude opportunities to try first in the next lesson, and name the specific prompt element they should focus on to make that task work.

- **Resubmission gating:** If the student resubmits, compare against the original stored in the platform. The most common resubmission fix is adding specificity to task descriptions and rationales. If they've done this — more specific task descriptions, quadrant assignments where there were none, richer rationale for top 3 — name that improvement explicitly and reference what changed. If the resubmission is essentially the same list with a few extra words but the task descriptions are still vague, say so directly: "Your second submission has more words, but the task entries are still at the category level. The fix is precision, not length." Then quote one entry and show the specific version.
