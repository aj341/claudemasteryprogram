## Learning objectives
By the end of this lesson you'll be able to:
- Describe your current AI tool use honestly — what you use, for what, and how satisfied you actually are
- Distinguish between low-value AI habits and high-value recurring use cases

- Pinpoint one specific opportunity where Claude is likely to outperform your current approach

## Lesson script

### Why you should audit before adding anything new

Most people who describe themselves as "using AI" have a patchwork of habits — a tool they tried once, a feature they half-set-up, a workflow they use every second day when they remember to. That's not a criticism. It's how new tools get adopted in a busy practice: messily, inconsistently, and often for the first use case that came to mind rather than the most valuable one.

Before you add Claude to that mix, {{firstName}}, it's worth taking stock of what's already there. Not because your current setup is wrong, but because you can't design something better until you can see clearly what you're working with.

The audit this lesson asks you to do has three purposes. First, it shows you your actual AI use — not the version you'd describe to another consultant over coffee, but the real picture. Second, it reveals where you're getting genuine value versus where you're going through the motions. Third, it gives you a starting point for deciding exactly where Claude should sit in your work at {{businessName}}.

This lesson is not about convincing you to use more AI. If your current tools work for you, that's a good outcome. What the audit reveals, though, is that most people are using AI at about 30% of what it could do for them — and in the wrong 30% at that.

### The three questions that structure a useful audit

A useful AI audit doesn't need to be complicated. Three questions cover almost everything that matters.

**Question one: What are you using?**

List every AI tool you've used in the past three months — including tools you've only used once or twice. This includes stand-alone tools like Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity, or Otter.ai. It also includes AI features built into software you already use: the Copilot button in your Microsoft apps, the AI draft feature in Gmail, the AI assistant in your practice management platform, the chatbot on a software vendor's website.

Most consultants and advisers undercount this by 30–50%. Once you start listing, more surfaces. Include everything — even the AI summary suggestion you accept inside your meeting notes app.

**Question two: What tasks do you actually use AI for?**

Not the tasks you intend to use AI for. The actual recurring ones.

Be honest about this. If you open ChatGPT twice a month to brainstorm a workshop outline but forget about it for weeks at a time, that's a once-a-fortnight brainstorming habit — not a core workflow. If you use the AI draft in Gmail every day for routine status updates to clients, that's a real workflow.

Tasks worth listing: anything you do at least once a week, or anything where AI saves you more than 15 minutes each time you use it. Don't count tasks you use AI for once out of curiosity and never again.

**Question three: How satisfied are you, honestly?**

Rate each tool and each task. Not in terms of "it's impressive," but in terms of actual work value. Does it produce something you can use with minimal editing before it goes into a client deliverable? Does it reduce the time or mental load of a task you'd otherwise find tedious — like writing post-discovery recaps? Or does it require so much prompting, correcting, and editing that you sometimes wonder if it's faster to draft it yourself?

A tool that impresses you but doesn't actually save you time is not a workflow asset. A tool that quietly handles 40 status updates a month is — even if it never produces anything that feels clever.

### The two patterns you'll probably find

When you do this audit honestly, most consultants and advisers find they fall into one of two patterns.

**Pattern one: Generic use.** AI shows up for brainstorming, first drafts of things you don't care about, and testing — but almost never for the specific, recurring tasks that actually take up your billable hours. The brainstorming is fine. The real opportunity is in those repeating tasks. A scope of work that needs writing every time a prospect asks for one. A summary of every discovery call you run. A weekly status update that goes out to a long-running engagement client. Pattern-one users have often proven AI works in principle but haven't connected it to their real workflow.

**Pattern two: Narrow use.** You use AI regularly, but for one or two tasks only. It works well for those tasks, and you haven't looked further. The audit won't tell you you're wrong — your current use is probably genuinely useful. What it will tell you is whether there are obvious adjacent tasks that should also be on AI but aren't yet — for instance, you draft engagement letters with AI but still hand-write every weekly status update.

Neither pattern is a problem. They're just different starting points. The audit makes visible what's working, what's being ignored, and what's worth changing.

### Identifying gaps and opportunities

After listing what you use, what you use it for, and how satisfied you are, look for three things.

**Where do you feel friction?** These are the tasks where you use AI but it feels more like managing the AI than using it. You're re-prompting repeatedly. You're getting outputs that need heavy editing before they could go to a client. You're not sure the tool understands the difference between a discovery brief and a final deliverable. That friction usually means one of two things: either your prompts need work (covered in Lesson 1.2), or the tool isn't matched to the task.

**Where have you given up?** Tasks you tried AI for and abandoned. This is worth examining because sometimes the abandonment was warranted (wrong tool, wrong task) and sometimes it was an early bad experience with a tool you were using wrong. If you gave up on AI for drafting scopes of work six months ago because the output was generic and missed your engagement structure, you may have given up on a prompt problem, not a tool problem.

**Where are your heaviest recurring tasks with no AI at all?** These are the opportunity zones. Any task you do at least weekly that involves writing, summarising, extracting, or structuring information — and where you're not yet using AI — is worth examining. Not because AI always belongs there, but because it very often does. Post-meeting status updates. Discovery brief recaps. Scope clarification emails. Fee-increase notifications.

### Why treating all AI tools as interchangeable is the common mistake

This one matters, so it gets its own section.

The tools are not the same. They behave differently. They're trained differently, they have different strengths on different task types, and they produce noticeably different results for the same prompt.

The practical implication: using "AI" as a category when deciding what to try doesn't tell you much. Using ChatGPT because it's what you know, when a tool purpose-built for your task exists and works better, is a real cost — not in money necessarily, but in output quality and time spent re-editing.

Claude has particular strengths: long documents and complex reading tasks (think a 40-page discovery brief or a fresh policy document a client has shared), instructions that require holding multiple constraints in mind simultaneously, nuanced writing that needs to sound like a trusted adviser rather than a template, and tasks where accuracy in following a detailed brief matters more than creativity. These are not universal AI strengths. They're specific to how Claude was built and trained.

The relevant question isn't "should I use AI for this?" but "which tool does this task well?" The audit makes that question possible to answer, because you'll know what you're actually doing and what results you're getting.

### Common mistake → better approach

**Mistake:** Opening a new AI tool and asking it the same kind of prompt you've always used, then judging it by whether the output feels roughly as good as the one you're used to.

This approach treats all tools as interchangeable and penalises any tool that behaves differently from the last one you used. You end up anchored to the first AI behaviour you learned, rather than learning what each tool does best.

**Better approach:** Before testing a new tool, write down the specific task you're testing it on, the specific output you consider acceptable, and how long your current approach takes. Then test the new tool on that same task with a specific, well-structured prompt. Compare the outputs on the same criteria, not on general impression.

When you do the audit in this lesson, you'll gather exactly this kind of information about your current tools. That gives you an honest basis for deciding where Claude fits — and where it probably doesn't.

### What to do with what you find

After completing the audit, you should have four clear things:

1. A list of every AI tool you currently use, what you use it for, and how satisfied you are.
2. Your three strongest current use cases — the places where AI is genuinely saving you time.
3. Your three biggest frustrations or gaps — the places where AI has let you down or where you've stopped trying.
4. One specific opportunity where you think Claude might do better than your current approach, and one sentence of reasoning for why.

That fourth item is the one that matters most for the rest of this course. It's the specific task you'll use to test Claude properly in the next session — not a hypothetical, not a brainstorm, but a task you actually need done where you have enough context to judge whether the output is useful in your practice.

The audit is not the end of the process. It's the beginning of a more deliberate one. When you know what you're using, for what, and how well it's working, every subsequent decision — which tool for which task, how to build a prompt, whether to add a new tool or fix your use of an existing one — has a real foundation underneath it.

## Graded deliverable

**Title:** Your AI use audit

**Brief:** An honest audit of where AI sits in your work right now — whether you've been using it heavily, dabbling, or holding off. The audit works at either end. Don't manufacture experience you don't have, and don't downplay what you've already tried. The point is to know your starting line so the rest of the course can build from it.

**What to submit:**

1. **Your AI starting point** — What AI tools (if any) you've actively used in the last three months. Include AI features inside software you already use (Gmail Smart Reply, Microsoft Copilot, ChatGPT, etc.), not just stand-alone AI apps. **If you haven't used any AI yet**, write a few sentences on what's held you back — what made you wary, what you tried once and stopped, or why you've left it alone. Either path is a valid starting point.

2. **What is (or could be) helping** — **If you've found something that genuinely helps:** name the tool, the specific task, and rough time saved per use. One example is enough; up to three is fine. **If you haven't yet:** name the single task you're most hoping Claude can help with, why it matters in your week, and what (if anything) you've tried. Pick whichever is honest.

3. **Three recurring tasks where AI is underperforming or hasn't been tried** — List three specific recurring tasks from your week — different in nature, not three flavours of the same thing — where AI has either let you down OR has never been involved. For each task: name it in the language you'd use to describe it to a colleague (a sentence is plenty), then briefly say what you've tried (if anything) and what the actual problem is. "I haven't tried AI on this" is a valid answer — describe the task and why it feels like a candidate. Three is the right number: it forces you off the obvious one.

4. **One specific opportunity for Claude** — One real task in your actual work where you think Claude could earn its keep. Name the task, your current approach (or "no current approach"), and why you think Claude could help. Tie it back to a Claude strength from the earlier lessons (long context, holding instructions, drafting from a brief, summarising, thinking through a problem). This is the task you'll act on.

**Where to submit:** Paste your submission into the Submission box at the bottom of this lesson on the Claude Mastery platform. The AI grading engine returns your grade card within 60 seconds.

**Pass mark:** 70 / 100. If you score below 70, the platform shows your grade card with feedback per criterion and activates a **Revise & Resubmit** button after a 24-hour cooldown.

**Rubric (100 points total — pass at 70):**
- **Specificity (25 pts)** — When tools are named, they're real and specific (not "an AI tool"). Tasks name the actual task, not a category. The opportunity item names one specific task, not a vague area. If the answer is "I haven't used AI yet," the reasoning behind that is specific too.
- **Structure (25 pts)** — All four sections are present and clearly separated. Each answers its own question and stands on its own. The opportunity is distinct from the use case / frustration sections.
- **Constraint clarity (25 pts)** — The audit reflects the student's actual current situation, not aspirational use. AI newcomers are honest about it. Frustrations describe specific problems, not vague dissatisfaction. Both heavy users and newcomers can score full marks here.
- **Outcome focus (25 pts)** — The opportunity item identifies a specific task with a clear case for why Claude could help — connecting a Claude strength from the lesson (long context, holding instructions, drafting, summarising, thinking through problems) to the task's actual requirements. Reads like a working document the student could act on.

## Notes for the AI grading engine

These notes are read by the grading engine when scoring submissions for this lesson. They are not shown to the student.

- **Reward (high marks):**
  - Tool inventory names real, specific tools by name — including AI features embedded in business software (ServiceM8, Xero, Gmail, Microsoft 365, HubSpot, etc.) not just stand-alone AI apps
  - Use cases describe a specific recurring task with enough context to picture it: "summarising the project brief from each new client before our kickoff call" scores higher than "document summarising"
  - Frustrations describe a specific problem with a specific task — what they tried, what happened, what the output was missing — rather than vague disappointment
  - The opportunity item connects a specific task's requirements to a specific Claude capability — not just "Claude is more advanced"
  - Satisfaction ratings are contextualised: the student explains why they gave a rating, not just the number

- **Penalise (dock points):**
  - Tool inventory contains only one tool or lists tools without tasks or ratings
  - Use cases or frustrations are vague categories ("I use it for emails," "it wasn't very useful for writing")
  - The opportunity item is generic ("I think Claude could help with my writing") without naming a specific task
  - Submission is clearly hypothetical — tools and tasks don't match the student's actual business context, or the student admits they don't currently use AI but writes use cases anyway
  - Sections are missing or merged — for example, frustrations folded into use cases without distinction

- **Common 60–69 patterns (near-pass):** All four sections are present but the tool inventory is thin (one or two tools, no ratings, no task detail) or the opportunity item names a category rather than a task. Or the use cases are real but lack enough specificity to distinguish a recurring workflow from a one-off experiment. In feedback, quote the vague item back — for example: "You listed 'helping with emails' as a use case — which emails specifically? Who are you sending them to, and what do they typically cover? That level of detail is what turns a vague use case into a real workflow you can build on."

- **Common 80+ patterns (excellent):** The tool inventory reads like a proper software audit — multiple tools including embedded AI features, real tasks, and ratings with brief justifications. Use cases include enough operational detail that you can picture the student's working week. Frustrations name a specific problem — sometimes including a prompt they tried that didn't work, or an output characteristic that made the result unusable. The opportunity item names a task, explains the current approach, articulates why it falls short, and makes a reasoned case for Claude — connecting a specific Claude capability to the task's actual requirements.

- **Feedback tone:** Direct, specific, and kind. Quote the student's own words — name what's specific enough and what isn't. Never give generic "be more specific" advice without showing exactly where the vagueness sits. Always end with one concrete next step: for most students, this is the opportunity item they identified — tell them that's the task to use in their next Claude session, and if possible, give them one sentence on what their opening prompt should include.

- **Resubmission gating:** Compare resubmits against the original. The most common fix is adding specificity to the tool inventory (ratings, task descriptions) or replacing a vague use case with a concrete one. If the student did this, name the improvement explicitly: "In your first submission, you listed 'drafting' as a use case. In this version, you named 'writing the fortnightly project update to our three main clients' — that's the level of detail that makes this actionable. That's the improvement." If the resubmit adds length without adding precision, say so and point to the one item that still needs work.
