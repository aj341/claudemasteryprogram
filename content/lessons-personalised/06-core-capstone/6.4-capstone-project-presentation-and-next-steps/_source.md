## Lesson script

### You have built something. Now name it.

Over four lessons you designed a Claude-powered solution for a real problem in your business. You scoped it. You built it. You ran it under real conditions and measured what happened. You have done more structured AI implementation work than most small business owners in Australia and New Zealand will do this year.

This lesson is the last step: synthesis. Turning everything that happened across Lessons 6.1 through 6.3 into a document that captures what you built, what you learned, and where you go next. Not a report filed and forgotten. A reference you will actually use.

Synthesis is different from summary. A summary says what happened in each phase. Synthesis extracts the meaning. It answers the questions: what was the real problem, what did we actually build, what did it do, and what do we know now that we didn't know at the start? Those four questions, answered clearly and in sequence, produce a document worth keeping.

That is what this lesson asks you to write.

### The executive summary: one page that does the work

The executive summary is the most important thing you will write in this lesson. It is the thing a future hire reads to understand your project. It is the thing you read in three months when you can't remember exactly what you built or why. It is the thing you use to explain your AI work to a business partner or accountant who wasn't there when you did it.

One page. Roughly 200–250 words. Four elements in sequence.

**The challenge.** What was the problem before this project started? Be specific. "Our client onboarding took too long" is a summary. "New clients waited an average of four days for their welcome pack and first appointment instructions, which led to three complaints last quarter and one client who walked before starting" is a challenge statement. The difference is specificity: the first tells you something happened, the second tells you what it cost.

**The solution.** What did you build? Name it plainly. What does it do, in one or two sentences, for someone who was not in Lesson 6.2 with you? If you built a Claude Project with a knowledge base and a prompt template that generates client onboarding documents in under five minutes, say that. Don't generalise it to "an AI-powered process improvement."

**The build.** A sentence or two on what the build actually involved. The key decisions you made in Lesson 6.2 and why you made them. What you set up, configured, or wrote. This section is brief — it's evidence that the solution was real work, not a one-prompt shortcut.

**The results.** What did the evaluation in Lesson 6.3 find? Numbers if you have them. Time saved, quality improvement, frequency of use, error reduction — whatever was specific to your project. At least two concrete data points. If you found that the solution worked but needed adjustment, say that too. Honest results are more useful than polished ones.

Written in that order, those four elements make the case for your project without the reader needing any prior context. That is the test: can someone who didn't do this course read your executive summary and understand what you built and why it mattered?

```
# Executive summary structure
Challenge:   What was the problem — specific, with costs or consequences named
Solution:    What you built — named clearly, described in two sentences maximum
Build:       What the build involved — key decisions, brief
Results:     What the evaluation found — at least two concrete data points
```

### The project journey: what actually happened

The executive summary is the argument. The project journey is the evidence.

Three paragraphs — one each for design, build, and evaluation. Each paragraph answers a different question.

**Design paragraph:** What was the scope you set in 6.1? Why that scope, not something broader or narrower? What constraint or decision shaped the project most? The design phase often produces the most important decision in the whole project — the choice to focus on one specific thing rather than trying to solve everything. Name that choice.

**Build paragraph:** What did you actually make in 6.2? What surprised you during the build — what was harder than expected, or easier? What did you change from the original design once you got into it? The build paragraph is where the project gets real, because real builds always involve at least one change from the plan. If yours didn't, be specific about why the plan held.

**Evaluation paragraph:** What did you measure in 6.3? What did the results show — and what did they not show? What would you do differently if you ran this project again from the start? The evaluation is where most people learn the most, because measurement always reveals something the build couldn't. Name what you learned.

Three paragraphs at roughly 100 words each. Specific, honest, written in plain language. If you find yourself writing a paragraph that could apply to any project — any AI project, any business project — it is not specific enough. Return to your actual notes from Lessons 6.1, 6.2, and 6.3 and pull out something concrete.

### Reflection on the Foundation tier: what you can do now

You started this course at Lesson 1.1. That lesson asked you to write your first real prompt for a real task. Most students arrive there writing prompts that are essentially questions — a sentence or two, no context, no format guidance, no constraint. Claude produces something generic. The student rewrites it two or three times and eventually gets something usable. The whole process takes twenty minutes.

That is not where you are now. Let's be precise about what has changed.

In Lesson 1.1, you didn't have a persistent environment. Every conversation started from zero. Claude didn't know your business, your clients, your tone, your industry. Every prompt had to carry all the context on its own, and most prompts didn't.

Now you have Projects. Claude arrives in your domain already knowing who you are and what you need. You spend your time on the task rather than re-explaining the context.

In Lesson 1.1, you couldn't write a structured brief. You knew what you wanted but couldn't translate it into instructions Claude could act on reliably.

Now you write prompts with context, task, format, and constraints in sequence. You know what each element does and what happens when you leave one out. You write prompts that other people don't, and you notice the difference in output quality.

In Lesson 1.1, you had no evaluation framework. You couldn't tell the difference between a good Claude output and a mediocre one except by gut feel.

Now you have measured a real project against real criteria. You have data on what Claude produces in your business under real conditions — not a demo, not a classroom exercise. You know where the outputs are strong and where they need your judgment before they go anywhere.

That is a real shift in capability. Not in Claude — in you. Claude is the same tool it was in Lesson 1.1. What changed is the structure you built around it: the Projects, the prompt craft, the evaluation habit. That structure is what makes the tool reliable instead of occasionally impressive.

For the deliverable in this lesson, you need to name three specific learnings from the Foundation tier. Not "I got better at prompting." That is true but useless. What specifically did you learn that changed how you work? Which lesson produced the single most useful thing you now do regularly? What did you believe about AI before this course that you now know is wrong? Concrete. Personal. Specific.

### Common mistake → Better approach

**Mistake:** Writing a project presentation that reads like a lesson summary instead of a project record. The student describes what each lesson covered rather than what actually happened in their project. "In Lesson 6.1 I designed my solution. In Lesson 6.2 I built it. In Lesson 6.3 I evaluated it." That is three sentences about the course, not one sentence about the project.

```
# Lesson summary (wrong)
In Lesson 6.2 I built a Claude Project following the steps in the lesson.
I followed the recommended approach and set up a knowledge base.

# Project record (right)
I built a Client Comms Project with three uploaded reference documents:
our service menu, a past proposal we're proud of, and a one-page tone
guide I wrote specifically for this project. The setup took 90 minutes.
The first time I ran the onboarding prompt against a real new client,
the output needed two edits — both minor — before I sent it.
```

**Better approach:** When writing each section, ask: "Could this paragraph describe someone else's project?" If yes, it is not specific enough. Replace any general description with the actual thing you built, the actual numbers you measured, the actual decision you made. The test is whether a reader who knows nothing about your business can picture exactly what you built and what happened when you ran it.

### What comes next: the Professional tier and your next 30 days

Completing this lesson passes the Foundation tier. That is a real thing. You have covered 34 lessons and built a real project. The Professional tier is available when you are ready — it covers advanced prompt architecture, multi-Project workflow design, Claude for team use, and more sophisticated automation patterns. It assumes Foundation as a prerequisite and builds directly on everything you have done here.

But the most important thing about the next 30 days is not the Professional tier. It is staying in motion with what you have built.

AI capability compounds when it is used regularly and intentionally. The Foundation tier gave you the tools and the structure. The next 30 days determine whether those tools become habits or drift back toward occasional use. Three specific habits, each with a trigger, will do more for your capability development than any amount of additional learning.

For the deliverable, you will name three specific habits you will build in the next 30 days. Each habit needs a trigger — a specific moment or event that will cause you to use Claude in that way. "I will use Claude more often" is not a habit. "Every Monday morning when I open my calendar for the week, I will write one difficult email in my Client Comms Project before I do anything else" is a habit. The trigger (Monday morning, opening calendar) makes the behaviour automatic rather than optional.

Your three habits should connect directly to your capstone project. If the project showed that your Claude-assisted client onboarding saves you 40 minutes per new client, one habit should be: make sure you always run that process in the Project, not in a fresh conversation. If the evaluation showed that outputs need a quality check before they go to clients, one habit should be: build that check into the workflow as a named step, not an afterthought. The habits should close the gap between what the project proved was possible and what you actually do every week.


## Graded deliverable

**Title:** Capstone Project: Final Presentation

**Brief:** This is the final graded submission of the Core tier. Everything from Lessons 6.1–6.3 comes together here: a capstone presentation that records your project clearly enough to be useful to you, your team, or anyone you bring into your work later. It is both a grade submission and a working reference you keep. Five components: executive summary, project journey, quantified outcomes, three Core tier learnings, and three next-30-days habits with triggers.

**What to submit:**

1. **Executive summary (200–250 words)** — The full four-element summary in sequence: challenge (specific, with consequences), solution (named clearly, two sentences maximum), build (key decisions, brief), results (at least two concrete data points from your Lesson 6.3 evaluation).

2. **Project journey — design, build, evaluation** — Three paragraphs of approximately 100 words each. Design: scope and key decisions. Build: what you made, what surprised you, what changed from the plan. Evaluation: what you measured, what the results showed, what you'd do differently.

3. **Quantified outcomes — at least 2 concrete results from Lesson 6.3** — Bullet points with numbers. Time saved, quality change, error reduction, frequency of use, cost saved. "It worked well" is not a quantified outcome.

4. **Top 3 Core tier learnings — three short paragraphs (150–300 words total)** — Three paragraphs (50–80 words each). Each learning must be personal and concrete — the specific thing that changed in how you work, not a general observation about AI. If a learning could apply to any student in any course, it is not specific enough.

5. **Next 30 days — three habits with triggers (50–150 words total)** — Three habits in the trigger format. Format: "When [specific trigger], I will [specific action] using [specific Claude capability or Project]." Habits without triggers score in the bottom half of Constraint clarity.

**Where to submit:** Paste your submission into the Submission box at the bottom of this lesson on the Claude Mastery platform. The AI grading engine returns your grade card within 60 seconds.

**Pass mark:** 70 / 100. If you score below 70, the platform shows your grade card with feedback per criterion and activates a **Revise & Resubmit** button after a 24-hour cooldown.

**Rubric (100 points total — pass at 70):**
- **Specificity (25 pts)** — Does the executive summary name the specific project, the specific problem, and specific results — or does it describe a generic "AI process improvement"? Are the three learnings personal and named — the specific thing that changed in how the student works — or generic ("I learned to write better prompts")? Language that could apply to any project scores in the bottom third.
- **Structure (25 pts)** — Are all five components present and in the correct form? Executive summary in the four-element sequence. Project journey in three paragraphs labelled by phase. Quantified outcomes as bullet points with numbers. Learnings as paragraphs, not dot points. Next 30 days in the trigger format. Missing components cost marks proportionally.
- **Constraint clarity (25 pts)** — Does the project journey describe the actual work done — specific decisions, specific builds, specific surprises — rather than summarising what each lesson covered? Do the next-30-days habits have named triggers, not just intentions? A habit without a trigger ("I will use Claude for client communication more regularly") scores in the bottom third.
- **Outcome focus (25 pts)** — Are the quantified outcomes real and specific — numbers from Lesson 6.3 — or placeholders like "significant time savings"? Does the full document read like something the student would actually return to and use — not just a grade submission? Real findings, honest evaluation results, and specific future habits read as a working reference.

## Notes for the AI grading engine

These notes are read by the grading engine when scoring submissions for this lesson. They are not shown to the student.

- **Reward (high marks):**
  - Executive summary is clearly written about a specific real project — the challenge names a consequence, the solution is named (not described generically), the results include at least two numbers or concrete changes
  - Project journey paragraphs describe what actually happened — specific decisions, specific surprises, specific findings — and could not plausibly describe someone else's project
  - Quantified outcomes include real measurements from the 6.3 evaluation, including any mixed or negative results, which signals genuine assessment rather than polished reporting
  - Three learnings are personal and named — they reference a specific moment, lesson, or realisation that changed a specific working behaviour, not a general statement about AI capability
  - Next-30-days habits are in trigger format and connect logically to what the capstone project demonstrated — the habits close the gap between what was proven possible and what the student will do regularly
  - The full document reads like a working reference — useful beyond the submission, something the student would actually return to

- **Penalise (dock points):**
  - Executive summary describes a generic "AI-powered workflow improvement" without naming what was built, what problem it solved, or what it produced — this fails Specificity immediately
  - Project journey paragraphs summarise what each lesson covered rather than what happened in the student's project — penalise under Constraint clarity
  - Learnings that use only general language: "I learned to use Claude more effectively," "I learned that AI can save time," "I got better at prompting" — dock Specificity and flag in feedback with a direct prompt for the student to name what specifically changed
  - Quantified outcomes presented as vague improvements: "my workflow is faster," "quality improved" — these fail Outcome focus; require at least one number or specific change
  - Next-30-days habits without triggers: "I will keep using Claude for onboarding documents" — this is an intention, not a habit; dock Constraint clarity and name the trigger format in feedback

- **Common 60–69 patterns:**
  The student has completed all five sections and the submission is clearly about a real project. The executive summary is in four parts but the results section is vague — no numbers, just a general assessment of success. The project journey reads as a description of what they did rather than what happened — no surprises, no changes from plan, no honest findings. The learnings are structured as paragraphs but generalised: they name the topic of the learning (prompting, Projects, evaluation) without naming the specific change in working behaviour. The next-30-days habits are intentions without triggers. In feedback: quote the results section and ask "what number from Lesson 6.3 belongs here?" Quote one learning and ask "what specific task do you do differently now, and what did you do before?" Quote one habit and ask "when exactly — what triggers this?"

- **Common 80+ patterns:**
  The executive summary reads as a clean, useful record of a real project — specific enough that a reader who knows nothing about the course or the student's business understands exactly what was built and what it produced, including any limitations or mixed findings. The project journey paragraphs include at least one honest complication — something that didn't go as planned, or a finding from the evaluation that was unexpected. The learnings are personal: they reference a specific moment or turning point and name the behaviour that changed, not just the concept learned. The next-30-days habits are in trigger format, plausible given the student's described business, and connected to the capstone project's findings. The document as a whole is something the grader can see the student actually using. Acknowledge the specific project clearly — name what they built and what result stood out. End with one concrete observation about what the next 30 days look like if the habits take hold, drawn from the student's own trigger statements.

- **Feedback tone:** Direct, specific, kind. This is the final lesson of a substantial program. Acknowledge the completion genuinely — one sentence that names what they built, not a general congratulation. Quote the student's own words from the submission when identifying strengths and gaps. End with one concrete encouragement for the next 30 days drawn directly from their trigger habits — not a next step toward another lesson.

- **Resubmission gating:** Compare resubmission against original on four dimensions: (1) did the executive summary results section gain at least one number? (2) did any project journey paragraph gain a specific detail — a decision, a surprise, a change from plan — that wasn't in the original? (3) did any learning become personal and named rather than generic? (4) did any next-30-days habit gain a trigger? Name each improvement that occurred. If the resubmission is substantially the same document with added words but no added specificity, say so directly and name the exact section and sentence that still needs to become concrete.
