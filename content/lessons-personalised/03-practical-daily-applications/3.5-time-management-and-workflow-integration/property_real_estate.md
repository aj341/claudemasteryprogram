## Lesson script

### The integration question you should actually be asking

Most agents who use Claude regularly still treat it like a tool they pick up when they think of it. A prompt here, a draft there, a quick question when something comes up. It works. But it doesn't compound. You're not saving an hour a week — you're saving five minutes at a time, randomly.

The question that changes this is not "should I use AI?" You've answered that already. The useful question is: where in my agency week does AI replace manual work?

That's a different question. It forces you to look at your actual schedule — not a hypothetical version of it — and find the specific moments where you are doing something by hand that Claude could do in a fraction of the time. Not categories. Moments. "Every Monday morning I write four vendor updates that take me 40 minutes total." That's a moment. "I spend time on communication" is not.

This lesson is about making that map concrete, turning it into a habit, and understanding what stops most agents from getting there.

### Finding your friction points

Friction points are the tasks you do every week at {{businessName}} that cost you more time than they should. They're the ones where, if you're honest, you procrastinate for a few minutes before starting. They feel heavier than their actual importance. Not complex listing presentations or critical buyer negotiations — the other kind. The repetitive, time-consuming kind that doesn't require your best thinking, but still pulls from your energy budget.

Here's how to find them. Look back at the last two weeks of your actual work. Not what you planned to do — what you actually did. Which tasks took 20 minutes or more that are essentially the same every time you do them? Which ones do you leave until the end of the day or the end of the week because you don't want to start? Which ones would you immediately delegate if you had a capable EA to hand them to?

Make a short list — five to eight tasks. Then for each one, ask: is this task primarily text-based input and output? Does it follow a recognisable pattern from one instance to the next? Could someone who understood the task well produce a solid first draft if I gave them the background?

If the answers are yes, yes, and yes — that is a Claude task. Not a task you can theoretically use Claude for. A task you should be using Claude for right now.

For example: the vendor update email you send every Monday after a Saturday open home. Every time, you're covering similar ground — number through the open, qualified buyer feedback, action plan for the week ahead, a confident close. The specifics change but the structure doesn't. That email currently takes you 20 minutes per vendor. With a good prompt and your open home notes pasted in, Claude produces a first draft in under two minutes. You spend three more minutes editing in the buyer comments and personalising. You're done in five. You've taken back 15 minutes per update — and you'll do that again every Monday for every active campaign.

That's one friction point. Most agents have six to ten of them.

### Building a batching habit

The biggest mistake agents make when they start integrating Claude is one-off use. They use Claude when a specific task comes up, as a reactive response to the moment. This works, but it produces inconsistent results — sometimes you've thought through the prompt carefully, sometimes you haven't — and it doesn't build any skill or efficiency over time.

The better approach is batching. Set aside one or two dedicated times each week — 20 to 30 minutes each — and run all your similar Claude tasks in one session. Your vendor updates go in together. Your owner reports for the month go in together. Your prospecting letters from yesterday's appraisal list go in together. You're in the same headspace, using the same prompt structure, and you get faster with each one.

Batching does two things. First, it means you're not deciding on the fly whether to use Claude — you've already decided, and you've scheduled the time. Second, it means you're comparing your prompts directly against each other, which surfaces what's working and what isn't faster than scattered use ever does.

Here's what a simple batching system looks like in practice:

```
Monday morning — 20 minutes:
- Draft vendor updates from Saturday's open homes (paste notes,
  one prompt per campaign)
- Write this week's social tiles for sold and just-listed posts
  (one prompt for all three)

Friday afternoon — 15 minutes:
- Summarise the week's leasing activity for the principal's
  Monday meeting
- Draft next week's prospecting letter to the appraisal
  database
```

That's 35 minutes a week of deliberate Claude use replacing close to two hours of manual effort. Those 35 minutes also build your skill — you'll find better prompts, discover new task types to add, and get faster at producing usable output on the first or second attempt.

Start with two sessions. Same days, same times. Treat them like meetings with yourself that don't get cancelled.

### The context cost: Projects versus standalone conversations

Every time you open a new conversation with Claude, you start from zero. Claude has no memory of any previous conversation you've had. It doesn't know you run a 10-person regional NSW agency. It doesn't know your top vendor is a stickler for formal language. It doesn't know you've already decided on private treaty for the Forest Road campaign. You have to tell it again, from scratch, every time.

This is called the context cost. And it compounds. If you're using Claude for ten different tasks across a week, and each one starts with two minutes of context-setting, that's 20 minutes of overhead per week. Across a year, that's substantial. More importantly, prompts without adequate context produce weaker outputs — Claude is guessing at your situation rather than knowing it.

Claude Projects solves this. A Project is a persistent workspace where you set your context once. You describe your agency, your tone, your service offering, the common situations you deal with — sales, property management, commercial leasing. Every conversation you start inside that Project inherits all of that context automatically. You never re-explain who you are.

Use a Project when:
- You have a set of recurring tasks that all require the same agency context
- You've built up a prompt structure that works and you want to build on it rather than start fresh each time
- You're working on something ongoing — a listing presentation that spans multiple sessions, a 4-week campaign for a marquee property, a set of management agreements you're updating

Use a standalone conversation when:
- It's a one-off task with its own unique context that doesn't relate to your usual work
- You're experimenting with a new prompt approach and don't want to pollute your Project history
- The task is short, self-contained, and you don't expect to return to it

Most agency principals should have one Project that represents their core work — their agency context, their typical vendor and owner profile, their usual task types. Everything else is a standalone. That distinction alone removes most of the context cost from your week.

### Common mistake → better approach

**Mistake:** Keeping Claude use completely spontaneous — opening a new conversation each time a task comes up, re-explaining your agency context each time, and never building on prompts that worked previously.

**Better approach:** Create a Project for your core agency context. Inside it, keep a pinned note with the standard background Claude needs: your agency name, what you do, the suburbs you cover, your typical fee structure, your preferred tone, and any standing constraints (e.g. "always write in Australian English," "never commit to a price guide in vendor-facing drafts"). Now every conversation in that Project starts informed. You write shorter prompts. You get better output faster. And when you find a prompt that works particularly well for a recurring task, you save it — either inside the Project or in a simple notes file — so you're not rebuilding it from scratch next time.

### What good Claude integration actually looks like

Three examples from AU/NZ property and real estate businesses — described the way their owners would describe their weeks.

**Example 1: Karen, strata manager, Auckland**

Karen manages 40 buildings. Every quarter she produces a short report for each owners committee covering levy collection, completed maintenance, outstanding issues, and her recommendations for the upcoming AGM. She used to write these from scratch — usually 25 to 30 minutes each, around 18 hours per quarter.

Now she has a Project set up with her business context and her preferred report format. Each week she pastes one building's quarter-end figures and a short note of any issues into Claude and gets a structured first draft in under two minutes. She spends five minutes editing and personalising. She's doing 40 reports in around five hours. She's taken back over 12 hours a quarter — and the reports are more consistent than they used to be.

**Example 2: Marcus, owner of a Brisbane buyer's agency, three staff**

Marcus represents buyers across Brisbane's inner-east. The part of his job he least enjoyed was writing the post-inspection summaries for clients — not making the calls on which properties to recommend, but writing the accompanying email that explained why each one met or missed the brief. He'd been putting them off until late at night.

He now dictates his inspection notes into his phone immediately after each viewing — bones of the property, what the client would love, what would compromise the brief, and his price take — and pastes the transcript into Claude with a prompt that produces a structured client summary. The process takes eight minutes instead of 45 per inspection. He sends summaries the same evening, every time. His clients say it's the most useful service in the engagement.

**Example 3: Nina, principal of a 12-person agency, Melbourne**

Nina runs a sales and property management business with four PMs and six sales agents. The weekly bottleneck was the Monday morning team briefing — the document that explained which campaigns were doing what, which managements needed escalation, and which prospects were warm. Nina was spending 90 minutes putting it together every Sunday night.

She now does it in a Project that already knows her agency's listings and her preferred briefing format. She writes rough notes — "Maple St auction Saturday, 8 through OFI, two contracts requested, vendor wants update Tuesday" — and Claude turns that into a structured briefing in the right format. She reviews, edits, sends. Sunday night briefing time: 20 minutes instead of 90.

None of these property businesses changed their core work. They identified where the manual drafting work was, built a repeatable system around it, and now that time is theirs again, {{firstName}}.

### Example 6: Turning scattered support replies into one 30-minute batched session with a Project

**Scenario:** SaaS founder, Melbourne · Batching daily support reply drafting

**Prompt:**
```
I'm the founder of ChairFill, a small AU SaaS for hair and beauty salons (4-person team, ~180 paying salons). I'm still doing tier-1 support myself and it's eating my mornings — Intercom pings, I context-switch, half-write a reply, get pulled away, come back and reread the thread. I want to design a batching system using Claude.
      
      Help me set this up:
      
      1. Recommend a daily batching window — when in the day, how long, and why — given I do product work best in the morning.
      2. Help me write a Project context note (under 300 words) that includes: what ChairFill does, our top 5 recurring ticket types, our tone (friendly, no jargon, no exclamation marks), our refund policy, and what to escalate to me vs draft for sending.
      3. Propose a simple workflow: how I'd dump 8–12 tickets into one chat, get drafts back, review, paste into Intercom.
      4. Tell me what I should NOT use Claude for in this workflow (judgment calls, churn-risk customers, anything legal).
      
      Goal is to get tier-1 support down to 30 focused minutes a day. Keep it concrete — I'll be setting it up this Sunday.
```

**Why it works:** This hits three of the lesson 3.5 moves at once — friction-point naming (morning context-switching), batching design (one 30-min window), and writing a Project context note so the same setup isn't re-explained every chat. The final "what NOT to use Claude for" question reinforces the lesson's point about good integration meaning knowing the edges of the tool, not just where it fits.

### Example 7: Mapping the Monday admin pile-up before deciding what Claude touches first

**Scenario:** Dental practice manager, Brisbane · Friction-point audit

**Prompt:**
```
I manage a 4-chair dental practice in Paddington, Brisbane. We have one dentist, one hygienist, two assistants and me on reception. I want to figure out where Claude can save me time without disrupting clinical workflow.
      
      Help me run a friction-point audit. I'll list the recurring admin tasks I do each week. For each one, ask me three things: how long it takes, how often it repeats, and whether it involves patient health information.
      
      My weekly tasks:
      - Drafting recall SMS reminders for 6-month check-ups (about 40 a week)
      - Writing referral letters when our dentist refers to oral surgeons or orthodontists
      - Replying to new-patient enquiry emails (around 15 a week)
      - Updating our new-patient welcome email when fees or policies change
      - Writing the weekly internal note to the team about schedule changes
      
      After we go through each one, rank them from "safest and highest-value to integrate first" to "leave alone for now". Flag anything that should not go through Claude because of patient privacy under the Privacy Act.
```

**Why it works:** This is a textbook friction-point audit from the lesson — listing recurring tasks, scoring each on time, frequency and risk, then ranking integration order. The privacy flag operationalises the lesson's point that integration questions aren't only about time saved; some friction stays because the data shouldn't leave the practice management system.

### Example 8: Designing a Friday batching block for parent comms across 60 students

**Scenario:** Tutoring business owner, Hamilton NZ · Building a batching system

**Prompt:**
```
I run BrightPath Tutoring in Hamilton — six tutors, around 60 students, mostly Year 9–13 NCEA. Right now I write parent update emails as they come up, which means I'm switching contexts ten times a day and each one takes longer than it should.
      
      I want to design a Friday afternoon batching block where I sit down with Claude and knock out the week's parent comms in one go. Help me design it.
      
      Ask me:
      1. What types of parent emails recur (progress updates, missed-session follow-ups, term-end summaries, fee reminders)?
      2. How many of each type per week on average?
      3. What information I need on hand before I start the block (tutor session notes, attendance, any flags)?
      4. How long the whole block should take if it's working well?
      
      Then propose a structure: prep step, the order I should tackle email types in, what I paste in for each, and a finishing checklist before I hit send. Keep in mind I should not paste real student NCEA results or full names into Claude — show me how to anonymise inputs.
```

**Why it works:** The lesson's batching habit isn't "do similar tasks together" — it's a designed block with a prep step, an order, and a finishing checklist. The prompt asks Claude to build that scaffold rather than just listing emails, and folds in the data-handling rule from the same lesson about what doesn't belong in the conversation.

## Graded deliverable

**Title:** Your Claude integration map

**Brief:** Look at your actual working week, find three specific tasks where Claude can replace or significantly reduce manual effort, and attempt one of them right now. Then reflect on one concrete integration challenge you expect to face — and how you'll deal with it. This is not a planning exercise. The table you build is a working document you'll actually use.

**What to submit:**

1. **Integration map table — three rows, four columns** — Columns: Task | Current time | Estimated Claude time | What Claude does. Three distinct tasks. Name each one specifically (not "emails" but "follow-up emails after client discovery calls"). Describe the exact step Claude handles, not just "helps me write it."

2. **One attempted task — prompt, response, and where it fits** — Pick one of your three tasks and actually run it through Claude. Submit three things: **(a)** the exact prompt you sent (using Module 2 fundamentals — context, task, format), **(b)** Claude's full response unedited, and **(c)** a 1–2 sentence integration note: where this Claude step sits in your workflow — what feeds in (what you bring to the prompt), what feeds out (what you do with the response next), and what manual step it replaces.

3. **Reflection (80 words — specific friction, specific response)** — Identify one specific integration challenge you expect to face (not "it takes time to get used to") and describe concretely how you'll deal with it. Specific friction, specific response.

**Where to submit:** Paste your submission into the Submission box at the bottom of this lesson on the Claude Mastery platform. The AI grading engine returns your grade card within 60 seconds.

**Pass mark:** 70 / 100. If you score below 70, the platform shows your grade card with feedback per criterion and activates a **Revise & Resubmit** button after a 24-hour cooldown.

**Rubric (100 points total — pass at 70):**
- **Specificity (25 pts)** — Are all three tasks named concretely — at the level of a specific recurring task, not a category? "Writing the end-of-week project update email to our four active clients" scores high. "Email" or "client communication" scores low. Time estimates realistic. "What Claude does" describes a precise step, not a vague role.
- **Structure (25 pts)** — Does the table contain all four columns with content in every cell? Are the three rows genuinely distinct tasks — not variations of the same task type? Is the attempted task in step 2 clearly separate and complete — both prompt and Claude's response included in full, not trimmed?
- **Constraint clarity (25 pts)** — Does the attempted prompt use proper structure from Module 2 — context, task, format constraints — rather than a short or casual question? Is there at least one specific format constraint (length, tone, structure, or an exclusion)? The prompt should read like someone who has learned this, not a first-day attempt.
- **Outcome focus (25 pts)** — Does the reflection name a specific integration challenge — not a vague observation like "it takes time" or "I need to build the habit"? Does the response to that challenge describe a concrete action — specific day, specific system, specific fix — rather than a general intention?

## Notes for the AI grading engine

These notes are read by the grading engine when scoring submissions for this lesson. They are not shown to the student.

- **Reward (high marks):**
  - Table tasks are named at the sub-task level — the grading engine can identify the student's industry, their role, and the specific communication or document type in each row
  - Time estimates in the table are internally consistent — current time is plausibly longer than estimated Claude time, and the estimated Claude time is realistic (not "30 seconds" for a complex report or "3 hours" for a short email)
  - "What Claude does" column describes a step in the process — "drafts the body paragraphs from my bullet notes" rather than "writes the email"
  - The attempted task prompt includes all three structural elements: context (who the student is, their business, the situation), task (a specific verb and output type), and at least one format constraint — and the prompt matches the lesson module's prompt engineering standards
  - The attempted task is a real task from the student's table — not a generic example
  - The reflection names a specific friction point (e.g. "I won't have my meeting notes ready before I sit down to batch prompts on Tuesday") and a specific planned response ("I'll record a 2-minute voice memo immediately after each client call and paste the transcript")

- **Penalise (dock points):**
  - Table rows are categories rather than tasks — "emails," "social media," "admin" without further specification
  - All three table rows are essentially the same task type with minor variation
  - The attempted prompt is a short question without context, task structure, or format constraints — indicates the student has not applied Module 2 learning
  - The attempted task is clearly not from the student's own work — it uses a generic business or a different industry from the one implied in the table
  - The reflection says "it takes time to get used to" or "I just need to make it a habit" without naming a specific obstacle or a specific countermeasure — this is the most common way reflections fail and should be flagged directly
  - Claude's response to the attempted task is trimmed or summarised rather than pasted in full

- **Common 60–69 patterns (near-pass):** The table has three distinct, named tasks but the specificity is moderate — task names are recognisable but not precise enough to know the student's industry without guessing. Time estimates exist but the "What Claude does" column is thin (just "writes a draft"). The attempted prompt has some structure but is missing a format constraint or has a vague task instruction. The reflection identifies a challenge that is real but states the solution in general terms ("I'll make time"). In feedback, quote one table cell and show what a more specific version would contain. Quote the reflection's solution sentence and ask: what day, what time, what exactly will you do differently?

- **Common 80+ patterns (excellent):** The table reads like a real person's week — the grading engine can clearly identify the student's role, their clients or stakeholders, and the exact task type in each row. Time estimates are specific (e.g. "40 minutes" not "a while") and the Claude time estimate shows the student has thought through the workflow, not just halved the original. The attempted prompt is structured, uses real business context, and produces a Claude response that is clearly better than a generic first attempt would have achieved. The reflection is precise: a named friction point (e.g. "I batch on Monday but I often don't have notes ready from Friday meetings") with a named solution (e.g. "I'll write a quick 3-line debrief immediately after each Friday call before I leave the office"). The submission reads as a working document the student will actually use.

- **Feedback tone:** Direct, specific, kind. Quote the student's words — their table entries, their reflection — when identifying wins or gaps. Never give generic feedback. If a table task is a category, quote it back and show exactly what a specific version would say. If the reflection is vague, quote the solution sentence and ask: on what day, at what point in your week, doing what exactly? End with one concrete next step — the single most useful action the student can take in the next 48 hours to begin real integration.

- **Resubmission gating:** Compare against the previous submission explicitly. If the student has made table entries more specific, name the cell that improved and quote the new version. If the reflection now includes a named friction point but the solution is still general, say: "You've identified the right problem now. The solution still needs a specific trigger and action — when exactly, and what exactly will you do?" If the attempted prompt has improved in structure, acknowledge the specific addition (e.g. "you've added a format constraint this time — that's the right move"). If the resubmission is essentially unchanged, say so clearly and name the one thing they still need to fix.
