## Lesson script

### This is where Module 3 ends — and your working pattern begins

Over the past five lessons you have covered a lot of ground. In Lesson 3.1 you learned how to brief Claude on a writing task and get a draft that sounds like your business. In 3.2 you used Claude to do structured research — not open-ended browsing, but focused extraction that produces a usable output. In 3.3 you brought Claude into a real decision, supplying the criteria and constraints that let it pressure-test your thinking rather than just agree with it. In 3.4 you used Claude as an accelerated learning tool — pulling out the specific knowledge you need without reading a textbook. And in 3.5 you mapped where Claude fits inside your existing workflows: the tasks, handoffs, and repeating jobs where it can cut real time.

That is a concrete set of capabilities. This lesson is about making them stick.

### What you can now do — a real inventory

Before you build the habit, take stock of what you have. This is not motivational framing. It is a practical checklist you can return to whenever you are trying to remember which Claude capability fits the task in front of you.

**Writing and communication.** You can draft client-facing content — emails, proposals, summaries, social posts, event copy — with a properly specified prompt. You can control tone, length, and format. You can revise targeted sections without restarting the whole session. You know how to feed Claude a sample of your voice and ask it to match the style.

**Research and synthesis.** You can extract specific information from a topic, document, or market area using a structured brief. You can ask Claude to compare options, identify gaps, or pull out the specific facts you need — rather than producing a wall of background text you then have to read through. You know how to constrain the research scope so you get what is useful rather than what is comprehensive.

**Decision support.** You can use Claude to stress-test a decision before you make it. You can give it your options, your constraints, and your criteria and ask it to challenge your assumptions. You know the difference between using Claude as a rubber stamp ("does this sound like a good idea?") and using it as a structured challenge ("here are the three options I am considering — what am I not seeing?"). The second use produces real value. The first does not.

**Learning on demand.** You can use Claude to get up to speed on a topic you do not know well — a regulation, a technology, an industry you are pitching to — without spending an afternoon reading. You know how to ask for explanation at the right level, how to ask follow-up questions without losing the thread, and how to turn a learning conversation into a reference document you can keep.

**Workflow integration.** You have identified the recurring tasks in your work where Claude can be used as part of a regular process, not a one-off experiment. You know which tasks belong on the regular list, which tasks are not worth the setup, and which tasks are borderline — good with the right prompt, not worth it with a vague one.

That is your inventory. Keep it somewhere you will look at it.

### What a Claude week looks like

Most people who start using Claude well do not use it constantly. They use it consistently. There is a difference.

Constant use means reaching for Claude every time you have a task, whether it is suited to Claude or not. This leads to frustration — bad outputs on tasks Claude is not suited for, prompt fatigue, and a general sense that it is more effort than it is worth.

Consistent use means building Claude into the specific parts of your week where it reliably saves you time. The tasks are predictable. The context is already loaded or easy to load. The prompt patterns are familiar. You open Claude, run the session, get a usable output, and move on.

Here is what a consistent Claude week might look like for a small business owner in a service business:

**Monday morning.** Before the week starts, you open Claude and ask it to help you draft the one email you have been putting off — a difficult message to a client about a project delay, a follow-up on an unpaid invoice, a quote for a job that needs careful framing. Context first. Task second. Ten minutes, done.

**Mid-week.** You need to prepare for a meeting with a supplier or a potential client. You use Claude to research the company — not everything, just the three or four things that will make you sound prepared. Or you use it to draft the agenda. Or to turn your scattered meeting notes into a clean summary you can send afterwards.

**End of week.** You have a recurring content commitment — a newsletter, a LinkedIn post, a quarterly update for clients. You open Claude, paste in the brief you have already saved, update the variable details (this week's topic, this week's product, this week's news), and get a first draft in two minutes. You edit it. It goes out.

That is three Claude sessions across a week. None of them are long. All of them replace a task that would have taken meaningfully longer without Claude. That is a consistent pattern, and consistent patterns compound.

### Finding your highest-value use cases

Here is the test that matters: not what Claude is most impressive at, but what saves you the most time on tasks that actually need to get done.

Impressive does not equal valuable. Claude can do things that feel remarkable but do not actually change your working week. It can also do things that feel unremarkable — drafting a routine email, extracting five bullet points from a long article, turning your messy notes into a readable summary — that save you thirty minutes of mental work on tasks you do every single week.

Thirty minutes a week is twenty-six hours a year. That is a full week of working time, recovered from tasks that are necessary but not your highest-value work. That is the number to think about when you are deciding which use cases to prioritise.

To find your highest-value use cases, answer three questions:

**What takes you longer than it should?** Not what is hard or complex — what is slow? The task where you sit down, write three words, delete them, check your email, come back, write three more words. Claude is most valuable on tasks where the friction is in starting or structuring, not in the thinking itself. Drafting is a common one. Research that requires you to read before you can synthesise is another.

**What repeats?** A Claude use case that runs once a week is worth ten times a use case that runs once a month. The recurring tasks — weekly communications, meeting prep, quote follow-ups, content production — are where prompt templates pay off. You build the prompt once, store it, and the next fifteen uses cost you almost nothing in setup time.

**What do you avoid?** The tasks you put off — not because they are unimportant but because they feel unpleasant or effortful — are often ideal Claude tasks. An email you are dreading because you are not sure how to frame it. A document you have not started because you do not know where to begin. Claude is good at overcoming the blank page problem. If a task is on your list and keeps staying there, ask yourself whether Claude could at least give you a starting point.

Your highest-value use cases sit at the intersection of all three: tasks that are slow, recurring, and easy to avoid. Those are the ones to build proper prompt templates for and run every week without exception.

### Building the habit: the daily trigger

A skill that only activates when you consciously remember to use it is not a working habit. It is a special-occasion tool. And special-occasion tools get used occasionally.

The difference between people who integrate Claude effectively and people who use it once a month is not intelligence or technical skill. It is a trigger. A specific, predictable moment in the working day that reliably leads to opening Claude.

Triggers work because they attach a new behaviour to an existing one. You do not have to decide to use Claude — the existing behaviour prompts it automatically.

Here are trigger patterns that work for small business owners. Pick one and test it for two weeks before deciding whether to keep it, adjust it, or swap it for another.

**The blank-page trigger.** Any time you open a new document or email and face a blank screen, you open Claude first. You spend two minutes writing the prompt — context, task, format — and use Claude's output as your starting point. You are not committing to using what Claude produces. You are committing to not staring at a blank screen alone.

**The Monday-morning trigger.** The first thing you do on Monday after checking your calendar is open Claude and draft the one difficult communication you have been carrying from the previous week. It does not matter what it is. Pick the one piece of writing you have been avoiding and run a ten-minute Claude session on it. One task, every Monday, before anything else.

**The meeting-prep trigger.** Any time you have a meeting with someone you have not met before, or a meeting where you want to show up prepared, you run a five-minute Claude research and agenda session the morning before. You do not have to use everything Claude produces. The act of running the session forces you to think about what you want from the meeting — and Claude often surfaces one thing you had not considered.

**The end-of-session trigger.** Any time you finish a significant piece of client work — a project, a proposal, a complex email thread — you open Claude and ask it to help you draft the follow-up or next-step communication before you close the file. The context is already in your head. It takes five minutes. It means the follow-through happens immediately instead of sitting on a list.

None of these triggers require a habit of daily Claude use. They require attaching Claude to something you already do: opening a document, starting Monday, preparing for a meeting, finishing a piece of work. The trigger is already there. You are just adding Claude to what happens next.

### Common mistake → better approach

**Mistake:** Treating Claude as a tool you use when you feel like it, with no consistent trigger or pattern — and then wondering why you are not getting value from it.

This is the most common failure mode after completing a module like this one. The student has the skills. They have run successful sessions during the lessons. They know what Claude can do. But they return to their normal working week and Claude just does not come to mind. Two weeks later they open it, run a prompt, get a reasonable output, and then forget about it again for another two weeks.

The problem is not motivation. It is architecture. There is no trigger that reliably puts Claude in front of them at the moment a task is appropriate for it.

**Better approach:** Pick one trigger from the list above. Write it down as a specific rule, for example:

```
When I open a new email or document, I open Claude first.
```

Apply that rule for two weeks without evaluating whether it is the right rule. At the end of two weeks, you will have used Claude six to ten times in a consistent pattern. That is enough data to know whether the trigger is working and what to adjust. Habit design is not about willpower. It is about the conditions you create. One trigger, applied consistently, will do more for your Claude practice than all the capability in this module combined.

## Graded deliverable

**Title:** Your Claude week

**Brief:** Submit evidence of five real Claude uses from your actual work as you've been moving through Module 3 — the past few weeks, however long that took you. Not hypotheticals, not examples from the lesson. Each entry includes the task type, the prompt as sent, Claude's response (or a faithful summary), and a one-sentence outcome note. Then rank your top three and write a 150-word reflection on what has genuinely changed in how you work.

**What to submit:**

1. **Task entry 1 — task type + prompt + response + outcome** — Task type (Writing, Research, Decision support, Learning, or Workflow integration), the prompt as sent, Claude's response (or a faithful summary), and one sentence on what you did with the output.

2. **Task entry 2 — task type + prompt + response + outcome** — A genuinely different task type from entry 1. Across the five entries, spread across the Module 3 skill areas.

3. **Task entry 3 — task type + prompt + response + outcome** — Third entry. Different task type if possible.

4. **Task entry 4 — task type + prompt + response + outcome** — Fourth entry. Still from your actual work over the past few weeks.

5. **Task entry 5 — task type + prompt + response + outcome** — Fifth and final entry. Aim to spread the five entries across the Module 3 skill areas rather than cluster.

6. **Ranking + Reflection (reflection 150 words ± 10)** — Top 3 most valuable entries with one or two sentences explaining why each was valuable to your business specifically — time saved, decision made, or reliably repeatable. Then 150 words on what Module 3 changed about how you work: what you are doing differently now versus before Module 3, and what has not changed yet and why.

**Where to submit:** Paste your submission into the Submission box at the bottom of this lesson on the Claude Mastery platform. The AI grading engine returns your grade card within 60 seconds.

**Pass mark:** 70 / 100. If you score below 70, the platform shows your grade card with feedback per criterion and activates a **Revise & Resubmit** button after a 24-hour cooldown.

**Rubric (100 points total — pass at 70):**
- **Specificity (25 pts)** — Are all five tasks real and from this week — not hypothetical, not recycled from lesson examples? Does each entry name a real task with enough detail that the grader can picture the actual business situation? Generic entries score in the bottom third.
- **Structure (25 pts)** — Does every entry include all four components: task type, prompt (exact text), response or summary, and outcome note? Entries missing any of the four lose marks proportionally. Ranking and reflection must both be present and clearly labelled.
- **Constraint clarity (25 pts)** — Do the prompts show Module 2 prompt engineering applied in practice — context before task, format instructions, tone or audience, word count, explicit exclusions? At least three of five prompts should show clear constraint and format specification.
- **Outcome focus (25 pts)** — Does the ranking show genuine business judgment — concrete terms like time saved, decision made, task completed — not just enthusiasm? Does the reflection show honest assessment of what has and has not changed? Purely positive reflections score in the bottom third.
