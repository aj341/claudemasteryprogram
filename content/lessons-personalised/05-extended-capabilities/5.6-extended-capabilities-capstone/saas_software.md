### Your Claude Stack audit

Knowing what the stack contains is different from knowing how you actually use it. The audit is honest about that gap.

Most founders who complete five modules of a course end up with a distribution that looks roughly like this: some things they use every week without thinking about it, some things they tried once and set aside, and some things they completed the lesson on but haven't applied yet. All three are normal. The problem is treating them as equivalent — listing everything as "things I know how to do" without distinguishing between active use and theoretical knowledge.

The audit has three categories:

**Active.** You use this regularly. It's part of your working week. You'd notice if it disappeared. You could run it without re-reading the lesson.

**Tested, not adopted.** You ran through the lesson, completed the exercise, got a useful output — but it hasn't become a regular part of your workflow. Maybe the trigger wasn't obvious. Maybe the setup felt like overhead. Maybe you planned to return to it and didn't. It's not a failure. It's a flag.

**Not yet tried.** You completed the lesson, passed the assessment, but haven't applied it to real work. This is the category most founders underestimate. Knowing the concept is not the same as having the capability.

The deliverable for this lesson asks you to populate all three columns honestly. A submission that puts everything in the "active" column is almost certainly inaccurate. The grading engine is not rewarding completion — it's rewarding honest self-assessment.

Here's why the audit matters beyond self-knowledge: it tells you where your highest-opportunity gap is. The things in the "tested, not adopted" column are the ones closest to your reach. You already understand them. The barrier is usually a trigger — a specific task or moment in your week where you could apply the tool but haven't built the habit yet. Once you identify those, one targeted change in your workflow closes the gap without any additional learning.

### The opportunity cost of complexity

Here is something the course has not said directly but which becomes visible at this stage: more capability is not always the right answer.

You have vision, file processing, and automation tools available to you. That does not mean every task should use all of them. It means you now have the judgment to know when they're worth the overhead — and when a well-structured plain-text prompt does the job faster.

The clearest way to think about it is this: a task that takes you two minutes to brief Claude on with a plain prompt and returns a useful output in thirty seconds does not benefit from being wrapped in an automation. The automation would take longer to build, test, and maintain than the manual sessions it replaces over a month. For a task that runs three hundred times a year, the automation pays off. For a task that runs four times a year, it doesn't.

The same logic applies to file uploads versus pasting text. Uploading a PDF gives Claude access to the full document — but if the relevant section is two paragraphs you could copy and paste in ten seconds, uploading adds friction without adding value. Use the file upload when the document is too long to paste, when you need Claude to navigate between sections, or when the document will be used across multiple sessions in a Project. Don't use it as a reflex.

**Common mistake → Better approach**

Mistake: Reaching for the most sophisticated tool available by default. You have automation set up, so you route tasks through the automation even when a quick manual prompt would be faster. You upload documents as a habit rather than as a choice. You add complexity because you built it, not because the task calls for it.

```
# Checking your tool choice before you start
Is this task manual or recurring?
  Recurring more than weekly → consider automation
  Manual or occasional → plain prompt is almost always faster

Is this document long or short?
  Long (can't paste cleanly) → upload or paste key sections
  Short (two paragraphs) → paste directly, don't upload
```

Better approach: Before you decide how to run a Claude task, ask one question: what is the simplest approach that produces a useful output? Start there. Add complexity only when the simple approach falls short — not before. This is not about being conservative with the tools you've built. It's about using them with judgment instead of using them automatically. The tools are most powerful when they're chosen for a reason.

### What Module 6 is — and why this moment matters

Module 6 is the capstone module. It is different in structure from Modules 1–5.

In Modules 1–5, the curriculum determined what you worked on. Each lesson taught a specific capability, gave you examples, and asked you to practise it in a bounded way. The skills were defined. The tasks were guided.

Module 6 is self-directed. You bring the project. You define the scope. The result you're submitting in Module 6 is not a lesson exercise. It's a real piece of work produced for your actual SaaS using the full range of what you've built.

That's why this capstone matters. It's not an end point — it's a bridge. You're mapping your stack now so that when Module 6 asks you to select your tools deliberately, you know exactly what's in your toolkit, what you reliably use, and where your gaps are.

There's something else worth naming. By the time you complete this lesson, you will have built more structured Claude capability than most small SaaS founders in Australia and New Zealand have. Not because the course is exceptional — because almost no one does this work. They try a tool, get inconsistent results, and conclude that AI is either not useful or too complicated. You now know why the results are inconsistent: no context, no structure, no persistent system. You've built the alternative.

That doesn't mean the work is finished. Module 6 will surface gaps. There will be tasks you attempt in your real project where your current stack doesn't have a clean answer. That's expected. The difference between where you started in Lesson 1.1 and where you stand now is not that you know everything — it's that you know how to approach what you don't know yet. You have the prompt craft, the Projects infrastructure, and the capability layer. You know how to interrogate a task and match it to the right tool. That is a durable skill, not a fixed body of knowledge.

One prompt, written well, with the right context loaded and the right tool applied — that's the whole thing. Five modules to understand it. Module 6 to prove it in your own work.



## Graded deliverable

**Title:** Your Claude Stack

**Brief:** This is the Module 5 capstone and the last major submission before Module 6. Produce a one-page reference that accurately maps how you use Claude across all five modules — not how you plan to use it, not the ideal version, the actual current state. Three components: your Claude Stack map (four labelled sections), one showcase task that uses two or more capability tiers together, and a 150-word reflection on how your relationship with Claude has changed between Lesson 1.1 and today.

**What to submit:**

1. **Stack map — Prompting approach** — How do you write prompts now? 3–5 sentences. Name the specific Module 2 techniques you apply consistently (context-first, format instructions, constraints, templates). Describe how you personally apply them — not what they are.

2. **Stack map — Practical applications** — Your top 3 Claude uses from your working week. For each: the task name, how often you run it, and a one-sentence description of what the output replaces or saves.

3. **Stack map — Project system** — List the Projects you have active. For each: its name and one sentence on its purpose. If none active, name the one Project you would build if you started today and why.

4. **Stack map — Extended capabilities** — Which Module 5 capabilities (vision, file processing, automation) do you actively use? For each active capability: name the task it applies to and what it produces. For capabilities you haven't adopted: name one specific task where you could apply it.

5. **Showcase task — prompt(s), response(s), and capability-tier note** — A single task that uses at least two capability tiers from your stack working together. The task can be: (a) something you've already shipped and run during the course, (b) one you compose *now* for this submission, or (c) a Module 5 lab exercise that combined two tiers. Paste: (a) the prompt(s) you sent in full; (b) Claude's response(s) in full or summarised if very long; (c) a one-paragraph note identifying which tiers you used and why you combined them for this task.

6. **Reflection (150 words ± 10)** — How has your relationship with Claude changed from Lesson 1.1 to now? Name specific before-and-after differences — concrete skills or habits. What do you do differently now, and what does that difference produce in your work? 140–160 words.

**Where to submit:** Paste your submission into the Submission box at the bottom of this lesson on the Claude Mastery platform. The AI grading engine returns your grade card within 60 seconds.

**Pass mark:** 70 / 100. If you score below 70, the platform shows your grade card with feedback per criterion and activates a **Revise & Resubmit** button after a 24-hour cooldown.

**Rubric (100 points total — pass at 70):**
- **Specificity (25 pts)** — Is the Claude Stack map populated with real, current usage — not aspirational descriptions of what the student might use someday? Each section should name specific tasks, specific Projects, specific prompt techniques they apply today. "I plan to use file uploads for contracts" scores 6–10. "I upload supplier quotes as PDFs every week and use extraction prompts to pull price, lead time, and terms into a comparison table" scores 20+.
- **Structure (25 pts)** — Does the stack map cover all four sections with concrete, named examples? Does the showcase task include all three required components: prompt(s) in full, response(s), and a capability-tier note? Is the reflection present and within the word count? A submission with three strong sections and one missing scores no higher than 18/25.
- **Constraint clarity (25 pts)** — Does the showcase task demonstrably use two or more capability tiers — and is the multi-tier use intentional rather than coincidental? The capability-tier note is the key evidence: the student should name which tiers they used and explain why combining them produced a better result than using one alone.
- **Outcome focus (25 pts)** — Does the reflection name specific before-and-after differences — concrete skills or habits, not vague improvements in confidence? "I'm more confident using Claude" scores 6–10. "In Lesson 1.1 I sent a single-sentence question and rewrote it four times. Now I open my Client Comms Project, write a structured brief in two minutes, and send the first draft with minor edits" scores 20+.
